## Load-bearing claim inventory

1. **Abstract.** "This paradigm shift accelerates solving speed by up to three orders of magnitude compared to state-of-the-art diffusion baselines, while maintaining competitive optimality gaps."
2. **Sec 1, contribution iii.** "Orders-of-Magnitude Speedup: We empirically demonstrate that CycFlow attains competitive optimality gaps while running 2-3 orders of magnitude faster than leading diffusion models."
3. **Sec 4.2.** "CycFlow's TSP-1000 gap is 9.89% at 0.22 s... This accuracy is competitive with many previous methods, but is substantially higher than computationally intensive global solvers, as well as Fast T2T's lower gap which is 2-3 orders of magnitude slower."
4. **Sec 1, contribution ii.** "Deterministic Flow Matching... results in straight, deterministic flow paths that are easier to learn than diffusion reverse processes."
5. **Sec 3.1.** "By coupling the input distribution directly to this solution manifold, our method induces flow trajectories that are straighter and simpler than those in diffusion processes."

## Construct inventory

- **Construct: "competitive optimality gaps."** Operational metric: gap-vs-Concorde-optimal at 4 sizes (N=50, 100, 500, 1000). Tab. 1 shows TSP-50: CycFlow 0.08% (4 ms) vs. Fast T2T accuracy 0.01% (3 M). TSP-1000: CycFlow 9.89% (0.22 s) vs. Fast T2T accuracy 0.58% (8.6 M). Aspect under-discussed in the abstract (but acknowledged in Sec 4.2): at large N, "competitive" is true vs. constructive baselines but **not** vs. SOTA diffusion in absolute quality — CycFlow's 9.89% TSP-1000 gap is 17× the Fast T2T accuracy gap of 0.58%.

- **Construct: "orders-of-magnitude speedup."** Operational metric: median wall-clock per-instance time. Verified by Tab. 1, Tab. 2. TSP-50: CycFlow 4 ms vs. DIFUSCO accuracy 18 M = 270000× = 5.4 orders of magnitude. TSP-1000: 0.22 s vs. Fast T2T accuracy 8.6 M = 2345× = 3.4 orders of magnitude. Speed claim is well-supported.

- **Construct: "deterministic flow matching produces straighter, simpler trajectories."** Operational metric: end-to-end optimality gap of CycFlow vs. baselines. Aspect NOT measured: trajectory straightness or curvature directly (no measurement of flow path length, average velocity magnitude, or # of integration steps required to reach target). The "straighter trajectories" claim is structural/intuitive, not empirically measured.

- **Construct: "data-dependent coupling enables straighter trajectories."** The paper credits data-dependent coupling (Sec 3.1) as a key novelty distinguishing CycFlow from standard flow matching. Aspect NOT measured: data-dependent vs. independent (e.g., Gaussian-source) coupling ablation **within the same flow-matching framework**. Tab. 3 isolates flow matching against single-shot regression, but not data-dependent vs. independent coupling. This is a coupled-novelty attribution gap.

## Counter-hypothesis attempts

1. **The dominant speedup is structural (N coordinate evolution vs. N×N edge classification), independent of the data-dependent coupling claim.** Even with independent (Gaussian) coupling, an N-coordinate flow on R² would be O(N) vs. O(N²) edge denoising. The speed advantage is not contingent on data-dependent coupling — it would hold for any node-coordinate flow approach. Counter-hypothesis: the speedup is real but is attributed implicitly to flow-matching specifics when it's actually a representation-choice contribution. **Status: not directly refuted by ablations; the paper's Sec 4.3 Tab. 4 architecture ablation does compare backbones but always within their flow-matching framework.**

2. **At TSP-1000, "competitive" is misleading.** CycFlow's 9.89% gap is dominated by Fast T2T's 0.58% gap by a factor of 17. The Sec 4.2 paragraph acknowledges this, but the abstract's "while maintaining competitive optimality gaps" framing reads more strongly than the data supports for large N. **Status: partially refuted by the paper's own honest Sec 4.2 disclosure.**

3. **Tab. 4 backbone-ablation choices may be cherry-picked.** Equivariant GNN (0.34%) and standard Transformer (0.20%) and Transformer+Fourier (0.27%) are all within their CycFlow framework, all using flow matching. The fact that CycFlow's specific architecture (Transformer + RoPE + spectral canonicalization) lands at 0.09% is a 2.2× gain over vanilla Transformer. This is real but modest. **Status: ablation is fair on its face.**

## Mechanism-attribution audit

The framework couples four distinct novel choices:
- (a) Circular geometric target manifold (Sec 3.1).
- (b) Data-dependent coupling x_0 → x_1(x_0, π*).
- (c) Spectral canonicalization (Sec 3.3).
- (d) Linear-interpolant flow matching dynamics (Sec 3.2).

Ablations:
- **Tab. 3** isolates (d) vs. single-shot regression: 0.09% (with iterative FM) vs. 3.75% (direct angular regression on the same target). Strong evidence that the iterative ODE matters.
- **Tab. 4** isolates (c) vs. naive Transformer: 0.09% (CycFlow with canonicalization+RoPE) vs. 0.20% (vanilla Transformer). Modest but real gain.
- **No ablation** isolates (b) data-dependent vs. independent coupling within flow matching. The "straighter trajectories" benefit attributed to data-dependent coupling in Sec 3.1 is not isolated empirically.
- **No ablation** isolates (a) circular target vs. alternative manifolds (e.g., line target with sequence-position regression).

This is partial mechanism attribution. Two of four contributions ((d) and (c)) are isolated; two ((a) and (b)) are not.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

Speed vs. quality is the explicit trade-off. The paper reports both axes consistently across all 4 problem sizes and visualizes the Pareto frontier in Fig. 4. Sec 4.2 explicitly acknowledges the trade-off ("CycFlow should not be considered as a drop-in replacement for solvers where time is infinite and optimality is paramount"). Honest multi-axis reporting.
