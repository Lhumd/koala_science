# Scratch: Robust and Consistent Ski Rental with Distributional Advice (86271aa6)

## Load-bearing empirical claim inventory
1. **Abstract**: "Experimental results across diverse distributions (Gaussian, geometric, and bi-modal) demonstrate that our framework improves consistency significantly over existing point-prediction baselines while maintaining comparable robustness."
2. **Sec 1.1**: "Our method consistently improves consistency by roughly 5–20% over the baselines, with the largest gains observed on bi-modal and geometric distributions, where uncertainty is most impactful. Importantly, these improvements are achieved while maintaining the same robustness guarantees."
3. **Sec 6.1 Table 2** is the single load-bearing table: at `(b, R) = (50, 1.7)` ours vs Purohit-maj vs Purohit-mix on 5 distributions.
4. **Sec 6.2 Figure 1**: under Wasserstein perturbation η ∈ [0, ?] with true p = N(90, 12²), means over 25 transports.

## Construct inventory
- **Construct claimed**: "consistency" — the learning-augmented sense (ratio when prediction is accurate / near-accurate).
- **Operational metric**: `Cons(p) := E[g(Z)] / min_t g(t)` (Eq. 5). This is internally consistent with the LA literature.
- **Aspect not measured**: the **robustness–consistency trade-off frontier**. The paper claims "comparable robustness" + "improved consistency" but evaluates only ONE point on the 2D `(b, R)` frontier, namely `(b, R) = (50, 1.7)`. Whether the 5–20% advantage persists for tight robustness (R close to 1) or for very loose robustness (R → 2) is untested. Whether it persists across b values is also untested.
- Also unmeasured: empirical worst-case competitive ratio across realized D — robustness is enforced **analytically** (all three methods are R-robust by construction via Eq. 4), never measured. So "comparable robustness" is true by construction, not by experiment.

## Reward / objective inventory (here: cost function)
- The cost g(t) (Eq. 23/3) is identical for the proposed method and both baselines — they all evaluate Cons(p) using the same g derived from the true p. So the comparison is methodologically clean within its narrow scope. The proposed water-filling allocates probability mass on indices where g is small, subject to the same R-robustness LP constraints (Lemma 5.1) that the Purohit-style baselines also satisfy (via the analytical mapping Eq. 4). No reward-overlap or orthogonal-mechanism concern.

## Counter-hypothesis list
- **CH1**: Could the 5–20% gap on twopoint (1.04 vs 1.24) be a single-(b,R) artifact? The twopoint is `0.7δ_30 + 0.3δ_120` with `b=50` — both modes straddle b. A water-filling method that explicitly conditions on the bi-modal structure trivially beats a point-prediction method that collapses the distribution to one branch. At a different `(b, R)` where the modes don't straddle b, the gap likely shrinks. **Untested** because only one `(b, R)` is reported.
- **CH2**: The Gaussian baseline at N(50, 12²) is centered exactly at `b = 50` — the regime where rent-vs-buy is most ambiguous, hence where distributional info matters most. Off-center Gaussians (e.g., N(30, 12²) or N(100, 12²)) would test the method outside its sweet spot. **Untested**.
- **CH3**: Could "Purohit-majority" be a strawman? It uses a hard branch on `Pr[D ≥ b] > 1/2`, which discards almost all of the predicted distribution's information. A stronger baseline would be Purohit (2018) given the **mean** of `p̂` (the natural point projection) using their optimal randomized scheme, rather than the authors' two ad-hoc adaptations. The paper does not report this natural baseline.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit
- Robustness (R) is held fixed by construction across methods, so the "comparable robustness" claim is mechanical, not empirical. There is no measurement of the empirical worst-case ratio under adversarial D (which is what the theoretical R bound is supposed to underwrite).
- Consistency is reported but only at one `(b, R)`. The paper's central conceptual contribution is the robustness–consistency *trade-off* (consistency improves as R loosens), but no plot of consistency vs R (for fixed b) or consistency vs b (for fixed R) is shown in Section 6.

## Framing-vs-demonstration check
- Abstract: "Experimental results across diverse distributions… improves consistency significantly". The diversity is real (5 distribution shapes). But "significantly" is established at exactly one `(b, R)`. A reader naturally reads "significantly" as broad; the demonstration is narrow.
- Abstract: "maintaining comparable robustness". This is enforced by the analytical mapping Eq. 4, not measured. The paper is honest about Eq. 4 but a reader could interpret "comparable robustness" as an empirical finding.

## Headline-number denominator audit
- The 5–20% range cited in Sec 1.1 maps to Table 2 cells, e.g., twopoint: 1.04 vs 1.25 → 17%; gauss: 1.34 vs 1.42 → 6%. Denominator (the Purohit baselines) is well-defined. No denominator switch.

## Mechanism-attribution audit
- Single component (water-filling), no coupled-novelty issue.

## Variance / confidence intervals
- Table 2 reports point estimates with no error bars or seed counts. The distributions are fixed (no randomness in g), and the methods produce deterministic PMFs given (b, R, p̂), so a single-number-per-cell is technically defensible — but the bisection in water-filling has a tolerance, and the choice of "25 random transports" in Figure 1 (which IS reported as a mean) shows that variance reporting was considered for one experiment but omitted for the other.

## Missing comparison to closest prior work
- **Besbes et al. (2025)** is acknowledged in Sec 1.2 as having "studied the ski rental problem with full distributional predictions" — the most directly relevant baseline. The paper distinguishes its setting by saying Besbes assumes a known distance bound, but does NOT include Besbes's algorithm in Table 2 even with a fitted bound. Given the "improves consistency significantly over existing point-prediction baselines" framing, this is technically excluded by the qualifier "point-prediction", but a reader interested in distributional-prediction methods has no head-to-head data.
- **Cui & Dinitz (2026)** is mentioned as concurrent work on the same problem with unknown error — also no comparison.

## Soundness score rationale
The theoretical sections are well-developed and the operational definition of "consistency" is sound. The empirical claim "improves consistency significantly… while maintaining comparable robustness" appears in the abstract and is therefore load-bearing. It is established at exactly ONE `(b, R) = (50, 1.7)` slice of a fundamentally 2D `(b, R)` frontier; for a paper whose conceptual contribution IS the robustness–consistency trade-off, the failure to sweep either parameter is a structural thinness, not a minor omission. The Gaussian distribution is centered at exactly `b = 50` (the most favorable regime), and the bi-modal twopoint straddles b — both are sweet-spot configurations. Baselines are the authors' own adaptations of a point-prediction algorithm; no comparison to the directly-relevant Besbes et al. (2025) or Cui & Dinitz (2026) is reported. No score-2 trigger fires definitively (the framing carefully says "point-prediction baselines"); but the combination of single-(b, R) testing, sweet-spot distributions, no error bars in Table 2, and self-constructed baselines is a clear cluster of score-3-tier flaws. Theoretical execution is offsetting strength but does not rescue the empirical thinness on a multi-axis claim. Score 3, confidence 4.
