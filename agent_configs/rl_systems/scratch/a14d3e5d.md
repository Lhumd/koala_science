## Construct inventory
- **Construct: "sample efficiency"**
  - Verbatim claim: "FlyGM yields higher sample efficiency" (Abstract, no qualifier).
  - Operational metric: training loss / μ MSE / σ MSE during the **imitation learning stage** (Fig 3, Sec 4.1).
  - Aspects NOT measured:
    - RL-phase sample efficiency (PPO learning curves, samples to reach a reward threshold, reward at fixed sample budget) for any of the four architectures.
    - Sample efficiency vs. number of expert rollouts (held-data-budget curve).
    - Sample efficiency on out-of-distribution tasks the IL expert was not trained for.
  - The paper's own framing positions FlyGM as an "embodied reinforcement learning" controller (Intro, Conclusion: "FlyGM offers an anchor point for neural architecture search... improving data efficiency, stability, and generalizability across tasks"). The data efficiency / sample efficiency construct as used in the RL/architecture-search literature includes RL-phase efficiency, not just IL fit.

- **Construct: "stable control"**
  - Verbatim claim: "stable control across diverse locomotion tasks" (Abstract).
  - Operational metric: position error and angle error during velocity-tracking (Table 1); qualitative snapshots (Fig 4-7).
  - Aspects NOT measured:
    - Recovery time after external perturbation (push/pull).
    - Fall rate over long-horizon rollouts (no reported episode-length statistics or termination rates).
    - Robustness to terrain/morphology variation.
    - Variance across seeds for stability metrics (only training-loss std reported, not behavioral-stability std).

- **Construct: "no task-specific architectural tuning"**
  - Verbatim claim: "stable control across diverse locomotion tasks without task-specific architectural tuning" (Abstract).
  - Operational definition: same connectome graph used for all four tasks (✓).
  - However Appendix A.3: "Both tasks use identical graph topology... but **differ in input/output dimensions and network hyperparameters** due to behavioral constraints." LR for walking = 1×10^-4; for flight = 1×10^-5 (10× difference). Encoder/decoder dims differ. So "no architectural tuning" is true for the connectome topology, but encoder/decoder/LR are tuned per task. Moderate framing-vs-demonstration concern; not a score-2 trigger because the strict claim ("graph topology is task-invariant") is honored.

## Reward / objective inventory
- IL stage (Eq 8): KL(N(μ_t, σ_t²) || N(μ_s, σ_s²)) + λ(t)(||μ_s − μ_t||² + α||log σ_s − log σ_t||²). Annealed.
- PPO stage (Eq 9): standard PPO surrogate + value loss + entropy regularization, with task rewards from flybody.
- The PPO **task rewards themselves are not enumerated in the paper.** Reader has to consult flybody (Vaxenburg et al. 2025). Same reward used across all four architectures by inheritance, so this isn't a comparison-asymmetry problem; it is an opacity issue for reproducibility.
- Set-relationship: not applicable here — the comparison is across architectures sharing the same reward, not across reward designs.

## Counter-hypothesis list
1. **Sparsity-as-regularizer.** FlyGM is sparse; MLP baseline is dense (2-layer FCN, hidden 512). FlyGM trains faster than MLP in Fig 3. But Rewired and ER-Random ARE ALSO SPARSE (same edge count as connectome, just permuted). FlyGM beats both Rewired and ER-Random. So pure "sparsity vs density" is controlled by Rewired/ER-Random. ✓ Refuted by Fig 3 + Table 1.
2. **Inductive bias from preserved global graph statistics.** Rewired graph preserves degree distribution but randomizes which-neuron-talks-to-which. It does NOT preserve modularity, small-world structure, or hub identity. So FlyGM > Rewired could be due to modularity / small-world / hub structure rather than "exact wiring." Unrefuted — paper does not run a small-world-preserving rewiring control.
3. **MLP-teacher matching artifact.** IL expert is an MLP-based policy trained on real Drosophila motion capture (Sec 3.2). Both FlyGM and MLP-baseline imitate this MLP teacher. The MLP-baseline is structurally identical (FCN → action) to the teacher modulo training trajectory; FlyGM is structurally different. If the connectome's structure happens to align with the dynamics of MLP-teacher trajectories (which is plausible given fly motion is naturalistic and the connectome was wired by evolution for fly motion), the IL fit is faster. This is a SOURCE of inductive-bias gain that the paper does not separate from "the connectome is universally a good RL inductive bias." Unrefuted.
4. **Goodhart-degenerate policy.** Could FlyGM achieve low position/angle error via a degenerate creeping or freezing policy? Fig 4-7 + Fig 9 (joint kinematics show alternating tripod gait) refute this: the model produces realistic gait. ✓ Refuted.
5. **Seed selection.** "Shaded areas represent the standard deviation across multiple training runs" — n unspecified. Could be n=3, n=5, or different across architectures. Score-3-tier concern (under-specified protocol).

## Mechanism-attribution audit
- Three baselines to attribute "exact connectome wiring" effect:
  - MLP (rules out "any structure helps") ✓
  - ER-Random (rules out "any sparse graph helps") ✓
  - Rewired (rules out "degree distribution alone helps") ✓
- This is a CAREFULLY CONSTRUCTED ablation set targeting the "wiring specificity" hypothesis. Strong methodological hygiene.

## Soundness score rationale

**Primary critique: construct-coverage gap on "sample efficiency."** The abstract and Sec 4.1 make an unqualified "higher sample efficiency" claim while the operational metric is **only** Fig 3's IL training-loss curves. The paper explicitly states "We focused our evaluation on the imitation learning stage" (Sec 4.1). RL-phase sample efficiency (PPO learning curves; samples to reach a reward threshold; reward at fixed sample budget) is **not** reported for any of the four architectures, despite the paper's framing as an "embodied reinforcement learning" controller and the second training stage being PPO fine-tuning. The construct "sample efficiency" in the embodied-RL / architecture-search literature spans both IL fit speed and RL learning speed; the paper measures the former and frames as if measuring both.

This is a SCORE-2 trigger (construct-coverage gap on a load-bearing claim).

**Score-2 vs. score-3-or-4 discrimination:** The paper does have offsetting strengths — the Rewired vs ER-Random vs MLP baseline ablation is a careful, well-constructed control specifically targeting "exact wiring specificity," and Table 1's quantitative results (with std) support the wiring-specificity claim. Per the calibration principles, "a score-2 trigger fires regardless of offsetting strength on a load-bearing claim." So the offsetting strength belongs in the comment as a caveat, not as a score-rescuing factor.

**Final: Soundness score 2.**
