# ViGoR-Bench claim-vs-evidence audit (paper 3bdcdd22)

## Scope check
In scope: the paper introduces a benchmark, runs evaluations on 20+ generative models, reports human-alignment validation, and runs SFT/RL training experiments. Multiple load-bearing empirical claims. Per the broadened Phase A scope (any paper with empirical claims), this is in-scope for soundness audit even though it falls under "general" / vision-generative rather than RL.

## Load-bearing claim inventory
1. **Judge validity**: "evidence-grounded automated judge ensuring high human alignment" (abstract); "demonstrating superior MAE (Mean Absolute Error) and Pearson correlations" (abstract); "unprecedented alignment with human experts" (intro). Operational support: Sec. 3.3, Table 2.
2. **Holistic cross-modal coverage**: 20 sub-tasks across I2I/I2Is/I2V (abstract, Sec. 3.1, Fig. 3, App. A).
3. **Dual-track Process+Result**: Sec. 3.2 — Process scored 0-100 continuous, Result scored binary {0,1}.
4. **Main empirical findings (Sec. 4.1)**: (a) proprietary > open-source; (b) CoT improves interpretability but not always final accuracy; (c) video models show "Illusion of Reasoning". Tab. 3 supports.
5. **Training utility (Sec. 4.3)**: "Qwen-Image-Edit-2511-RL trained on 8×8 data achieves Reasoning Success (RS) of 97.0% and Average Score of 99.0... convincingly outperforms the state-of-the-art proprietary model." Also: "training on more challenging OOD data enhances generalization on simpler in-distribution tasks" and "RL > SFT where SFT saturates" (Fig. 6).

## Construct inventory (claim → metric → at-least-one-aspect-not-measured)

### Claim 1: "high human alignment"
- Construct: human alignment of automated judge. Quote: "demonstrates that the VLM's judgment distribution closely approximates that of human experts" (Sec. 3.3).
- Operational metric: Tab. 2 reports MAE, Accuracy, Variance, where Accuracy is computed after discretizing continuous 0-100 process scores into three 33-point buckets (Bad [0,33] / Moderate [34,67] / Good [68,100]).
- Reported numbers (with GT): Process Acc 73.3%, MAE 0.267; Result Acc 78.6%, MAE 0.213.
- **Aspects not measured**:
  - **Majority-class baseline** for the 3-bucket discretized accuracy. With many open-source models scoring single digits on RS (Tab. 3: Bagel 2.2, OmniGen2 0.8, Qwen-Image-Edit-2509 1.4, Qwen-Image-Edit-2511 4.9, etc.), a large fraction of cases likely cluster in "Bad". On a skewed 3-class problem, 73-78% accuracy can be at or below majority-class baseline. No baseline reported.
  - **Pearson correlation** is claimed verbatim in the abstract ("superior MAE and Pearson correlations") but not reported in Tab. 2 or any other table I found.
  - **Per-domain agreement**: Tab. 2 aggregates across all 20 sub-tasks. Reasoning over Sudoku grids vs Common-Sense vs Algebraic Calculation likely have different judge-error rates; a single aggregate accuracy can hide categories where the judge is essentially uninformative.
  - **Per-bucket disagreement structure**: 73-78% bucket-Acc with MAE 0.21-0.27 implies that even among "agreed" cases, absolute disagreement is substantial — a Result MAE of 0.21 on a binary {0,1} scale means roughly 1 in 5 disagreements at the binary level (consistent with 78.6% Acc), while a Process MAE of 0.267 on a 0-1 normalized scale (or 26.7 on 0-100) is large relative to the 33-point bucket width.
  - **Inter-judge robustness**: only Gemini-2.5-Pro tested as judge. No comparison with GPT-4o, Claude, or any non-Google VLM. Whether this is a Gemini-specific calibration or a general "VLM-as-judge" property is untestable from the data shown.

### Claim 2: "outperforms SOTA proprietary" (Sec. 4.3)
- Construct: outperforming SOTA on visual reasoning. Quote: "convincingly outperforms the state-of-the-art proprietary model" (Sec. 4.3).
- Operational metric: Tab. 4 shows Qwen-Image-Edit-2511-RL trained on 8×8 mazes scores RS 97% on test mazes (sizes 2×2-7×7), vs Nano Banana Pro at 11%.
- **Aspects not measured / framing gap**:
  - The training set is generated by the same rule-based algorithm that produces the test set (Sec. 3.1: "Maze Navigation... we employ rule-based algorithms... Input and ground-truth images are algorithmically generated"). This is in-distribution evaluation under a shared generative process. Calling it "outperforming SOTA" without distinguishing in-distribution training-set-style match from generic visual reasoning ability is misleading.
  - No cross-task transfer: model trained on Maze Navigation is not evaluated on Sudoku, Jigsaw, Block Building, or any non-maze task. The "RL > SFT" / "OOD generalization" conclusions are drawn from one task only.
  - Test set size is 100 mazes per cell in Tab. 4 (rounded percentages). Variance/CIs not reported.

### Claim 3: "video models exhibit Illusion of Reasoning"
- Construct: video models simulate motion but lack reasoning. Quote: "current video models excel at simulating fluid motion ... but struggle to internalize the underlying logical constraints required for rigorous reasoning tasks" (Sec. 4.1).
- Operational metric: Tab. 3 shows Sora 2 Pro / Kling 1.6 with high Process VQ (85.5/77) but low Result RS (10.1/1.6).
- **Aspects not measured**:
  - The Process metric is 0-100 continuous and the Result metric is binary. This scale asymmetry on its own can produce a large numerical gap between Process and Result without reflecting a reasoning-vs-fluency split. A Sora 2 Pro that gets every reasoning task "70% right" would still score 0 on binary Result RS.
  - The judge for both tracks is the same Gemini-2.5-Pro. If Gemini is more lenient on continuous Process scoring (giving partial credit) than on binary Result scoring, the gap is partly an artifact of the rubric design.

## Reward / objective inventory (Sec. 4.3 GRPO training)
Paper says GRPO is used (Liu et al. 2025a) but reward function is not specified in the main text. Whether RS-aligned reward is used (which would make the maze RS gain tautological) vs intermediate-reward decomposition is unclear. This omission limits how much the "RL > SFT" conclusion can be parsed.

## Counter-hypothesis list
- **Bucket-discretization hides disagreement**: 73-78% bucket-accuracy is consistent with substantial within-bucket MAE; the headline "high human alignment" framing depends on the discretized number rather than continuous MAE/correlation.
- **Judge-planner conflation favors Gemini-family entries**: Gemini-2.5-Pro is both judge AND used as external CoT planner for Nano Banana Pro†, GPT-image-1†, etc. (Sec. 4.1: "we employed external planners (GPT-5/Gemini-2.5-Pro) to decompose tasks into sequential generation steps"). This creates potential same-family judge-vs-planner alignment bias for † entries; the paper doesn't disambiguate per-entry which planner was used or run a controlled judge-vs-planner separation experiment.
- **In-distribution training masquerading as SOTA**: the "97.0% RS, outperforms SOTA" claim is on test mazes drawn from the same algorithmic distribution as training. Proprietary baselines have never seen this distribution.
- **Single-task generalization claim**: "training on harder OOD data enhances generalization" is established only on different sizes within the same task (Maze Navigation 8×8 → 2×2-7×7). Not generalization across tasks.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit
Process metrics use 0-100 continuous; Result uses binary. The same construct ("Background Consistency", "Rule Obey", "Visual Quality") gets different scoring rules across tracks. A model that nudges 5% off the binary threshold gets penalized identically to one that fails completely. This compresses the Result distribution and inflates the apparent gap between proprietary and open-source. No justification given.

## Framing-vs-demonstration check
- **Pearson claim**: abstract says "superior MAE (Mean Absolute Error) and Pearson correlations". Tab. 2 columns: MAE, Acc, Var. **Pearson is not reported anywhere.** Verbatim framing-vs-demonstration mismatch.
- **"Unprecedented alignment"**: framing implies the judge is a settled near-human surrogate. Demonstrated numbers (73-78% bucket-acc, MAE 0.21-0.27) without a majority-class baseline are moderate at best.
- **"Outperforms SOTA proprietary" (Sec. 4.3)**: framing implies the trained model genuinely surpasses proprietary visual reasoners. Demonstrated: in-distribution maze test where proprietary models have never seen the algorithmic generator.

## Headline-number denominator audit
- Maze Navigation: Qwen-Image-Edit-2511-RL 99.0 avg vs Nano Banana Pro 63.0 avg. Denominator = mazes generated by the same rule-based algorithm used for training; proprietary baselines are zero-shot on a distribution they've never been trained on. Framing reads as generic visual-reasoning superiority; actual comparison is in-distribution-trained vs zero-shot.

## Mechanism-attribution audit (limited applicability)
The benchmark itself doesn't claim a single mechanism; the four innovations (cross-modal coverage, dual-track, judge, granular diagnostics) aren't ablated against each other. For the training experiments, the paper bundles SFT+RL with GRPO and a particular grid-size mix; specific reward-function details and ablations on whether RL gains come from GRPO specifically or from any RL would clarify the "RL > SFT" claim.

## Soundness score rationale
The benchmark's headline methodological claim — "evidence-grounded judge ensuring high human alignment" — is supported by 73-78% three-bucket-discretized accuracy on a tiny human-validation split with only 3 annotators, no majority-class baseline reported, no per-domain breakdown, and no inter-judge robustness. The abstract additionally claims "superior MAE and Pearson correlations" but Pearson is never reported. Separately, the Sec. 4.3 headline that the trained model "convincingly outperforms the state-of-the-art proprietary model" at 97% RS is on in-distribution mazes drawn from the same algorithmic generator used for training, while proprietary baselines are zero-shot on a never-seen distribution. Both are framing-vs-demonstration mismatches on load-bearing claims (judge-validity and SOTA-superiority). The construct-coverage gap on judge validity is the clearest score-2 trigger: the central claim of the benchmark's reliability rests on a metric that does not span the construct (no baseline, no per-domain audit, no Pearson, no cross-judge check). The "outperforms SOTA" claim is an additional denominator-switch on a different load-bearing axis.

This is a score 2 — at least one and arguably two score-2 triggers fire on load-bearing claims, and they survive the paper's own evidence.
