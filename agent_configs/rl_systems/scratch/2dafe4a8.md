# Phase A scratch — claim-vs-evidence audit

## Load-bearing claim inventory

1. **(Abstract / §6 Conclusion) "NeSyS's consistent advantages over baselines in both WM prediction accuracy and data efficiency."** Supported by Tables 1, 2, 3 reporting "World modeling accuracy" averages on ScienceWorld, Webshop, Plancraft.
2. **(Abstract) "Reducing training data by 50% without loss of accuracy."** Supported by NeSyS-row data-fraction values (45% / 60% / 35% on the three envs respectively, footnote 2).
3. **(§1 Introduction, item 3) "Empirically demonstrate that NeSyS achieves consistent performance improvements across diverse interactive environments and model scales."** Same evidence base as #1.

The construct **"world modeling accuracy"** load-bears every headline number. Define it precisely.

## Construct inventory — what is "world modeling accuracy"?

- **Construct claimed.** A world model predicts the next state s_{t+1} given current belief b_t and action a_t (§3 Problem Setting). Accuracy of a world model is conventionally measured as the fidelity of its predicted next state to the ground-truth next state — for free-form environments this means token-level / semantic-level match against the recorded next-state text.

- **Operational metric used.** Multiple-choice question (MCQ) accuracy on a benchmark constructed by the authors (Section E "Benchmark Creation Details"). The literal benchmark protocol:
  > "After we sample the questions and the correct answers, we use Llama-3.2-1b-instruct to answer questions and use the wrong ones as the distractors."
  i.e., for every question, the wrong-answer candidates were drawn from one specific weak model's failure modes.

- **Specific aspects of the construct NOT captured by the operational metric:**
  1. **Free-form next-state generation quality** — exact-match, edit-distance, BLEU, or semantic similarity (e.g., embedding cosine) between an autoregressively generated next state and the ground-truth recorded trajectory. The paper's own §3 definition of a world model is "estimates the next state", which is free-form. Never measured.
  2. **Robustness to non-Llama-1B distractors** — distractors generated by a different family (e.g., gpt-5-mini, which the paper itself uses for rule induction; or Qwen3, the other backbone). Sensitivity-to-distractor-source is never reported.
  3. **Asymmetric difficulty across the rows being compared.** Llama-3.2-1B-NeSyS is evaluated against distractors from its own backbone family (a self/near-self setting). Qwen3-4B-NeSyS, Phi-4-mini, and GPT-5-mini are evaluated against distractors from a different family. The benchmark's effective discriminative difficulty is therefore not uniform across rows — strong models may "win" easily because the distractors are particularly soft for them, weak models from a different family may "lose" for the same reason.

Construct-coverage gap is explicit and verbatim-anchored. **Trigger: score-2 construct-coverage on the load-bearing claim.**

## Reward / objective inventory

There is no RL reward in the agent-loop sense; the training objective is supervised fine-tuning on filtered trajectory data (Phase 1) plus alternating refinement (Phase 2). The "energy term" γE_i is applied at inference time to modify the LLM's likelihood over the K candidates:

> "p̃_i = p_i exp(γ E_i)"

The metric being optimized is MCQ accuracy on the in-distribution benchmark; this is what improves in Tables 1–3.

## Counter-hypothesis list

- **CH1: The "data efficiency" gain is a measurement artifact.** The 45/60/35% data fractions are the *trajectory-data* fractions used for fine-tuning the small backbone. Rule induction, however, uses gpt-5-mini (Singh et al., 2025) — a much stronger proprietary model — to generate Python rules from clustered error cases (§4.2.1, Phase 1). The "data" cost of the gpt-5-mini rule-generation step is excluded from the accounting. A natural compute-matched baseline ("distill gpt-5-mini next-state predictions on the full trajectory set; fine-tune the small backbone on that") is missing. The headline data-efficiency claim therefore conflates trajectory-data with total annotation/compute. *Unrefuted by the paper's evidence.*

- **CH2: The Phase 1 → Phase 2 improvement is partly extra training rounds, not specifically reciprocal refinement.** Phase 2 alternates Neural WM and Symbolic WM updates. The Phase-1 vs. Phase-2 comparison is not compute-matched — Phase 2 simply runs more total updates. A control condition where Neural WM gets the same total fine-tune budget without any new symbolic refinement is missing. *Unrefuted by the paper's evidence.*

- **CH3: NeSyS gains might collapse if distractors come from a stronger model.** The benchmark distractors are Llama-3.2-1b-instruct's wrong answers. Symbolic rules induced by gpt-5-mini are asked to defeat distractors from a much weaker model. If distractors were generated by gpt-5-mini itself (or by the Phase-2 NeSyS variant that's being evaluated, in a self-adversarial setting), the rule set might no longer suffice. *Unrefuted by the paper's evidence.*

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

- **Plancraft Llama Phase-1 anomaly (paper §5.3 / §5.4):** "NeSyS (80.9%) slightly underperforms the standalone Symbolic WM (83.4%)" for Webshop on Llama Phase 1. The paper acknowledges this and attributes it to "extreme cases" where the Neural WM is too weak. This is a multi-objective trade-off case (Symbolic-only vs. fused) and is acknowledged but not investigated systematically.

- The "Move-Hard" Plancraft column (Table 3): NeSyS (Llama, 67.0%) ties or underperforms SFT-100% (67.0%) and Symbolic-Phase-2 (62.2%). The paper acknowledges this in narrative ("'Move-Hard' tasks involve complex side-effects that defy simple rule encoding"). Reasonable transparency.

## Framing-vs-demonstration audit

- **Framing.** Abstract: "reducing training data by 50% without loss of accuracy."
- **Methodology.** Footnote 2: "The percentage of filtered data depends on rule coverage, which varies by benchmark." Actual percentages: 45% (ScienceWorld), 60% (Webshop), 35% (Plancraft).

So "50%" is presented as a single headline number when the actual reduction varies by environment. This is a minor framing issue, not a denominator switch — but the abstract's flat "50%" is not a number that appears in any table cell.

- **Framing.** Abstract: "consistent advantages... in both WM prediction accuracy and data efficiency."
- **Demonstration.** Tables 1–3 show NeSyS top-of-row in *most* but not *all* cells. Mixed-cell-level performance (e.g., Move-Hard, certain Llama Phase-1 cases) is acknowledged in narrative but not foregrounded. Acceptable for a peer-review submission.

## Mechanism-attribution audit

The paper introduces two coupled novelties:

1. **Energy-based logit modification at inference** (§4.1): p̃_i = p_i exp(γ E_i).
2. **Rule-guided data selection during training** (§4.2.2 Phase 2): fine-tune Neural WM only on samples where Symbolic WM already disagrees / fails.

Existing ablation cells:
- "Neural WM Phase 1" — backbone, no fine-tuning, no symbolic fusion at inference.
- "Symbolic WM Phase 1/2" — symbolic-only, no LLM at inference.
- "NeSyS Phase 1" — energy-based fusion at inference, but pre-Phase-2 data selection.
- "NeSyS Phase 2 (ours)" — full method.

**Missing ablation cell.** "Energy-based fusion at inference + Neural WM trained on the *full* data, no rule-guided data selection." This would isolate whether rule-guided data selection is load-bearing for the headline accuracy gains, or whether the gains come essentially from the inference-time energy fusion. Without this cell, attributing the headline gain to "rule-guided data selection" specifically (the explicit data-efficiency narrative) is partially unsupported.

This is a moderate concern (score-3-ish on its own) but the construct-coverage gap above is sharper and dominates.

## Headline-number denominator audit

No clear denominator switch. The "X% improvement over SFT" framing is straightforward — the SFT baseline trained on 100% data is compared against NeSyS trained on (45/60/35)% data. No misleading denominator.

## Soundness score rationale

A load-bearing claim — "world modeling accuracy" measured across three interactive environments — is operationalized via an MCQ benchmark whose distractors are generated by **one specific weak model (Llama-3.2-1b-instruct)**, which is the same model family used as NeSyS's main backbone in roughly half the headline cells (all Llama Phase-1/Phase-2 rows in Tables 1–3). The metric the construct "world modeling accuracy" most naturally invokes — free-form next-state generation quality vs. ground-truth recorded trajectory — is never measured. This is a Score-2 construct-coverage trigger, anchored verbatim to Section E ("After we sample the questions and the correct answers, we use Llama-3.2-1b-instruct to answer questions and use the wrong ones as the distractors").
