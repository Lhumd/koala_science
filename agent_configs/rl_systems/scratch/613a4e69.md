## Construct inventory

- **"Out-of-distribution"** → operationalized as: model trained on DeepScaler-5K or DeepMath-5K; evaluated on AIME24/25, AMC, MATH500, Minerva, Olympiad. Aspect not measured: actual distributional separation between training and eval — these are all standard math reasoning corpora; "OOD" framing is loose. The +4.7% number specifically is the *training-set average*, not an OOD-specific metric.
- **"Hard samples"** → empirical pass rate `(1/K)Σ r(xi,yik) < τ`, K=6 rollouts. Coverage of construct is OK and the operational definition is consistent.
- **"Sample efficiency / exploration efficiency"** (implicit in "accelerates convergence" claim, Abstract) → measured by Fig. 3 training-reward curves and final accuracy. Aspect not measured: per-step training compute including GEPA reflection/eval calls. The "samples" axis in Fig. 3 reports policy-update samples only. GEPA budget Ctotal (Alg. 3) and Kimi-K2 reflection-call counts are not budgeted into a comparable axis.

## Reward / objective inventory

- P²O and GRPO use **identical** binary reward (r ∈ {0,1}, `\boxed{}` format + ground truth, Sec 4.1). No reward-overlap concern.
- P²O introduces a new gradient-computation pattern: rollouts on x̃ = T(x,z), gradient on x (Eq. 5 / Sec 3.3). This is "context distillation" (Snell et al. 2022 cited).
- P²O Teacher-Ref uses **Kimi-K2 (Team et al. 2025)** as the GEPA mutation operator. Self-Ref uses Qwen3-4B (same as policy). This is the central potential confound.

## Counter-hypothesis list (the score-2 candidate)

**H_paper: "Joint policy + prompt optimization improves over GRPO."**

**H_alt (mechanism-orthogonal outcome): "Most of the headline +4.7% gain comes from access to Kimi-K2 as an external teacher in the prompt-mutation step, not from the joint-optimization mechanism itself."**

Decomposition from Tab. 1, DeepScaler-5K row:
- GRPO: 60.5 avg
- P²O Self-Ref (Qwen3-4B as mutator): 62.4 avg → +1.9 vs GRPO
- P²O Teacher-Ref (Kimi-K2 as mutator): 65.2 avg → +4.7 vs GRPO

Of the headline +4.7, only 1.9 is attributable to the joint-optimization mechanism *holding the mutation source fixed at the policy model*. The remaining 2.8 is attributable to access to Kimi-K2 as a stronger external mutation operator. This is 2.8/4.7 = 60% of the headline gain.

**Status: unrefuted by paper's evidence.** The paper does not include a Kimi-K2-only baseline (e.g., direct SFT on Kimi-K2-generated trajectories for the same hard samples) that would isolate the joint-optimization contribution from the teacher-distillation contribution.

**H_alt2 (DeepMath inversion confirms the teacher-confound interpretation).** On DeepMath-5K:
- GRPO: 54.8
- Self-Ref: 61.7 (+6.9)
- Teacher-Ref: 57.9 (+3.1)

Self-Ref dominates Teacher-Ref by 3.8 points. The paper attributes this to *"a policy model may benefit more from self-generated refinements that strictly align with its own reasoning capacity, whereas an external teacher might introduce complex priors that are harder for the policy to internalize."* (Sec 4.2). This is exactly the predicted teacher-confound story: when the external teacher's reasoning style is misaligned with the policy's, the joint-optimization recipe loses ground. The paper interprets this as a finding about reflection-source choice; the alt interpretation is that the headline DeepScaler gain is partially a teacher-distillation artifact.

**H_alt3 (Seed noise on AIME headline).** AIME24 has 30 problems; AIME25 has 30 problems. The +12.9%/+11.7% AIME deltas are 16-rollout-averaged single-seed numbers (Sec 4.1: "we report the average performance across 16 rollouts per question"). No bootstrap CIs, no seed variance. Standard practice for AIME results in 2025-2026 is multi-seed runs with std reported. Status: unrefuted; the gap may include seed noise.

**H_alt4 (Minerva regression).** Tab. 1: GRPO Minerva = 41.5; P²O Teacher-Ref Minerva = 36.4. P²O *loses* by 5.1 points on one of the six benchmarks. The "consistently outperforms" claim is contradicted by this single benchmark. Score-3-tier framing concern.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

P²O and GRPO optimize the same scalar reward (binary correctness). No multi-objective trade-off. The trade-off is in compute, addressed below.

## Framing-vs-demonstration check

- *"Substantial improvements on out-of-distribution benchmarks (+4.7% avg.)"* (Abstract). The +4.7% is the training-set average, not an OOD-specific delta. The implicit framing ("OOD") is sloppy — every benchmark in Tab. 1 is held out from the SFT/RL training data of DeepScaler/DeepMath, so they're "OOD" only in the trivial sense. The paper has no held-out non-math eval to back the *generalization* claim.

- *"+12.3% avg."* on AIME (Intro, contributions). DeepScaler-5K → AIME24 (+12.9), AIME25 (+11.7). Average of those two = 12.3. Denominator audit: this is *Teacher-Ref* on DeepScaler. On DeepMath the AIME gain is much smaller (33.8→52.1 Self-Ref AIME24 = +18.3 with caveat; Teacher-Ref AIME24 = 44.6 = +10.8). The "+12.3%" headline cherry-picks the best variant. Not a clean denominator switch but a "best variant per dataset" headline pattern.

## Headline-number denominator audit

- "+4.7% avg" denominator = GRPO 60.5 on DeepScaler. Reader interprets this as a robust improvement over a fair baseline. Actual decomposition: 1.9 from joint optimization + 2.8 from Kimi-K2 access. The denominator implies "joint optimization beats GRPO by 4.7%" but the load-bearing intervention is partially Kimi-K2 distillation.

## Mechanism-attribution audit

The paper introduces three coupled novelties simultaneously: (a) hard-sample mining, (b) GEPA-evolved prompts as exploration aids, (c) context distillation (gradient on x, rollouts on x̃). Tab. 2 ablates (c) — without context distillation, performance collapses below GRPO. But Tab. 2 does NOT ablate (b): there is no run that uses hard-sample mining + context distillation but with random/template-set/oracle prompts instead of GEPA-evolved ones. This means the contribution of GEPA-specifically (vs any non-trivial prompt set) cannot be isolated.

A directly comparable missing baseline:
- **Kimi-K2 SFT only**: take the Kimi-K2-generated successful trajectories on hard samples used by GEPA, do straightforward SFT (no GEPA, no two-phase loop). What does that get?
- **Random good prompt**: use a fixed CoT/role prompt set (no evolution, just diversity) with hard-sample mining + context distillation. What does that get?

Without these, the contribution of GEPA's evolutionary loop specifically (vs the simpler "use a good external CoT teacher on hard samples") is unsupported.

## Cost / wall-clock audit

GEPA cost (Alg. 3): for each iteration, mini-batch B + dev set evaluation, calls to π_init (the mutation operator). Teacher-Ref means Kimi-K2 calls. The total compute for GEPA + reflection + evaluation is not reported in Sec 4 nor in any table. Fig. 3's x-axis is "Steps" of policy training — it does not show the wall-clock or token-budget cost of the parallel GEPA loop. So the "sample efficiency" / "accelerates convergence" implicit claim cannot be validated against GRPO at matched compute.

This is a moderate cost-claim concern. The paper does not explicitly claim "compute efficiency" — but the framing ("accelerates convergence", "sample efficiency for hard samples") implies it. Score-3-tier, not a clean score-2 trigger because the paper doesn't make a numerical compute claim.
