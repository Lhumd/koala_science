## Scratch — SoMA: A Real-to-Sim Neural Simulator for Robotic Soft-body Manipulation (87628b22)

### Load-bearing claim inventory

1. **C1 (Abstract, headline):** "SoMA improves resimulation accuracy and generalization on real-world robot manipulation by 20%, enabling stable simulation of complex tasks such as long-horizon cloth folding."
   - Supported by: Table 1 (PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS, Abs Rel/RMSE on rope/cloth/doll). Table 2 (T-shirt). Figure 3 (qualitative).

2. **C2 (Abstract, intro):** SoMA is "a 3D Gaussian Splat simulator for soft-body manipulation. SoMA couples deformable dynamics, environmental forces, and robot joint actions in a unified latent neural space for end-to-end real-to-sim simulation."
   - Supported by: §4.2.2 force-driven GS dynamics; Eqs. 9–11.

3. **C3 (Intro/§1):** "Modeling interactions over learned Gaussian splats enables controllable, stable long-horizon manipulation and generalization beyond observed trajectories without predefined physical models."
   - Supported by: Generalization columns in Tab. 1, T-shirt folding qualitative in Tab. 2 / Fig. 1.

4. **C4 (Contributions, §1):** "(2) Mechanisms that make such simulation feasible, including a robot-conditioned alignment to anchor scenes to real kinematics, a force-driven Gaussian-splat dynamics model for contact and occlusion, and multi-resolution training with blended supervision for stable long-horizon performance."
   - Supported by: §4.2.1, §4.2.2, §4.2.3, §4.2.4; Ablation Tab. 3.

5. **C5 (Conclusion):** "Experiments demonstrate strong generalization to unseen interactions and consistent improvements over prior methods across diverse objects and complex manipulation tasks such as cloth folding."

### Reward / objective inventory

The paper's training objective combines:
- L_img (Eq. 12): masked image reconstruction on visible regions (occlusion-aware).
- L_mom (Eq. 13): momentum consistency across hierarchy levels (self-supervised).
- L_D-SSIM (inside L_img): structural similarity.

PhysTwin baseline objective (per its own paper, not detailed here): physics-informed inverse parameter optimization fitting PhysTwin's spring-mass-like model to video.
GausSim baseline objective: state-only autoregressive prediction of GS.

Set relationship: SoMA's training objective is *not a strict subset* of PhysTwin's, and vice versa. They are functionally incomparable. PhysTwin optimizes physical parameters; SoMA optimizes a black-box neural force model.

Sign of dropped terms: PhysTwin uses physics-parameter constraints (rest length, stiffness ranges, damping). SoMA drops these in favor of unconstrained neural dynamics. **No measurement is reported of whether SoMA respects physical conservation laws** (no momentum, no mass, no energy diagnostics at evaluation).

Orthogonal mechanism check: The ablation (Tab. 3) compares Full vs Jointly vs Img-Only vs w/o MRT *only* on the cloth dataset. Most of the ablation gain is attributed to "blended supervision" and "multi-resolution training" — both are *training-stability* mechanisms orthogonal to the headline force-driven dynamics novelty. No ablation isolates "force-driven dynamics" vs a state-only-but-otherwise-identical baseline (e.g., GausSim with the same multi-resolution training and blended supervision applied). The reported gain over GausSim therefore conflates: (a) the force-conditioning, (b) the hierarchical GS structure, (c) the multi-resolution training schedule, (d) the blended/masked supervision. The paper credits (a) but does not isolate it.

### Counter-hypothesis list

- **CH1 (Goodhart on visible region):** L_img (Eq. 12) is masked to the *visible* object region only. PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS at evaluation are also reported "within the masked region" only (Appendix B.4: "All image metrics are computed between the predicted images and the ground-truth RGB images within the object regions only"). Since training and evaluation both use the *same mask geometry*, the model is essentially trained on the metric. Any dynamics that look right *in visible regions* will score well, even if occluded portions of the cloth/T-shirt are doing something physically nonsensical. The qualitative T-shirt folding (Fig. 4b) shows visible-side appearance; the occluded inside is uncheckable. **Refuted by paper?** No — paper acknowledges occluded supervision is hard but offers no evaluation that probes occluded-region correctness.

- **CH2 (Camera/dataset overfitting):** Train and test split is 7:3 *within the same self-collected dataset*, on the same ARX-Lift hardware, three fixed RealSense D405 cameras, same lighting, same table. "Generalization" means "unseen action sequence on the same physical object" — not "unseen object instance" or "unseen domain". A neural model with 13000 splat nodes and a 16-layer 128-dim mesh-graph network on ~30 sequences per object has substantial overfitting capacity. **Refuted by paper?** Section A.2 explicitly admits this limitation but it does not weaken the headline "generalization" claim in the abstract.

- **CH3 (Baseline weakness explains gap):** PhysTwin was originally designed for *non-robot-conditioned* video reconstruction. Appendix B.3 states: "we represent the robot end-effector as a circular control point with a fixed radius, which serves as the interaction primitive for grasping and contact. This modification only adapts the control abstraction and does not alter the underlying physical model of PhysTwin." A circular fixed-radius proxy for a multi-finger gripper is a major abstraction loss that may explain why PhysTwin fails on T-shirt folding — not because physics-based simulation is fundamentally weaker, but because the baseline is handicapped. GausSim has even less robot interaction support: "GausSim does not support explicit control or action inputs and instead predicts future Gaussian splat states in a purely state-based autoregressive manner". The two baselines were chosen specifically because they were not designed for robot conditioning, then compared on a robot-conditioning task. No baseline that *natively* handles robot conditioning (e.g., Isaac Sim with cloth, MPM-based simulators) is included. **Refuted by paper?** No — the paper's own §5.1 says "No existing method directly supports robot-manipulated scenes with complex interactions and occlusions" as justification for the narrow baseline set, but FEM/MPM-based simulators (e.g., MPM in Isaac Sim, Warp) handle exactly this task with ground-truth physical parameters.

- **CH4 (Headline number cherry-pick):** "20% improvement" appears in the abstract as a headline. From Tab. 1: SoMA Resim PSNR 33.51 vs PhysTwin 28.77 = +16.5%; vs GausSim 31.69 = +5.7%. Abs Rel: SoMA 0.089 vs PhysTwin 0.102 = -12.7%; vs GausSim 0.115 = -22.6%. LPIPS Resim: SoMA 0.055 vs PhysTwin 0.086 = -36%; vs GausSim 0.092 = -40%. The "20%" is plausible only when averaging across metrics or picking the comparison against the worst baseline (GausSim) on the most favorable metric. **Refuted by paper?** No specific calculation is shown deriving the 20%.

### Multi-objective trade-off audit

Resimulation vs Generalization Tab. 3 ablation: the "Jointly" variant (trained on all object domains) "slightly reduces resimulation accuracy but improves generalization performance" (32.59 vs 32.73 Resim; 31.77 vs 31.49 Generalization on cloth). This is a real trade-off the paper acknowledges. However, **the headline numbers in Tab. 1 are the per-domain (not Jointly) configuration**, which means the headline configuration is NOT the configuration that generalizes best on the very task generalization is claimed for. The footer table (Tab. 3) shows the full model's "Resim 32.73 / Generalization 31.49" while "Jointly" has "Generalization 31.77" — so on cloth generalization, the Jointly variant beats the headline configuration. This footnote is buried under "Ablation studies" rather than being the headline result.

### Framing-vs-demonstration check

- **Abstract:** "SoMA, a 3D Gaussian Splat simulator for soft-body manipulation. SoMA couples deformable dynamics, environmental forces, and robot joint actions in a unified latent neural space."
  vs.
  **§4.2.1:** Robot conditioning recovers a "global scale factor s" and a rigid transformation. The "robot action" used to drive the simulator is "T_sim^ee(t) and the gripper opening state c_t" — i.e., end-effector pose + gripper open/close, not joint-space.
  Although §1 says "robot-conditioned alignment to anchor scenes to real kinematics" and "joint actions", the actual conditioning signal is end-effector pose + gripper width derived via FK from joint state. The framing implies joint-space control; the demonstration uses end-effector-space + gripper-binary control.

- **Abstract:** "without predefined physical models."
  vs.
  **§4.2.2:** "Environment force...nodes close to the supporting surface receive an additional support force: f_i^env = g + s_i if d_i < tau, g otherwise" (Eq. 10). This *is* a predefined physics model — gravity + support-force-when-close-to-surface is exactly the kind of contact penalty FEM/MPM use. Plus L_mom (Eq. 13) hard-codes momentum conservation. The "no predefined physics" framing contradicts the existence of Eq. 10 and Eq. 13 in the methodology.

- **§5.1 Datasets:** "Most sequences contain 100–150 frames." At 30 FPS, that is 3.3–5 seconds. The "long-horizon" framing is specific to 3–5 second rollouts. The T-shirt folding qualitative (Fig. 4b) shows ~7 frames, no time-axis.

### Headline-number denominator audit

"20% improvement" from abstract: not derived in any single line of the paper. From Tab. 1, the closest plausible reading:
- Per-method-per-metric, averaging absolute-direction normalized improvements over BOTH baselines and across BOTH tasks (Resim+Gen) and across all five metrics. Not a standard summary.
- Picking PSNR Generalization: SoMA 32.89, PhysTwin 26.54 = +24% (closest match to "20%"). But that picks the *worst-performing* PhysTwin metric.

The denominator is unclear, the comparison baseline is unclear, and the metric chosen is unclear. The 20% is a marketing number, not a load-bearing technical claim with a reproducible calculation.

### Mechanism-attribution audit

The paper introduces (i) force-driven GS dynamics, (ii) hierarchical GS clustering with bottom-up momentum propagation, (iii) multi-resolution temporal training (k=5–10 stride), (iv) blended supervision (occlusion-aware image loss + momentum consistency regularizer), and (v) image super-resolution preprocessing (4×).

The ablation in Tab. 3 covers only (iii) MRT and (iv) blended supervision (Img-Only). It does NOT ablate:
- (i) force-driven vs state-only dynamics with an otherwise-identical architecture;
- (ii) hierarchical vs flat GS;
- (v) super-resolution vs original resolution.

Therefore the paper attributes its gains over GausSim to "force-driven dynamics" but provides no ablation isolating that component from (ii), (iii), (iv), and (v) — all of which differ from GausSim's pipeline. This is a **coupled-novelty mechanism attribution failure**: gains credited to (i) could be substantially or entirely due to (ii–v).

A specifically missing ablation: "GausSim + multi-resolution training + blended supervision + super-resolution preprocessing" — i.e., GausSim under SoMA's training tricks. Without it, the gains attributed to "force-driven dynamics" are unsupported.

### Soundness score rationale

**Score-2 triggers fired on load-bearing claims:**

1. **Construct-coverage gap (C2, C4):** Paper claims "force-driven", "physically meaningful dynamics" — the operational metrics are PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS/Abs Rel/RMSE, all *appearance* metrics. No physical-quantity metric (force-residual, momentum-conservation, contact-onset timing, mass conservation, penetration) is ever measured at evaluation. A simulator can win on all five metrics by hallucinating visually-plausible motion that violates physics arbitrarily.

2. **Coupled-novelty mechanism attribution failure (C2, C4, headline 20% claim):** Force-driven dynamics, hierarchical GS, multi-resolution training, blended supervision, and 4× super-resolution preprocessing are introduced jointly. Tab. 3 ablates only multi-resolution training and image-vs-blended supervision. No ablation isolates the force-driven dynamics from the training-and-architecture stack. Gains credited to the headline mechanism are unsupported by the ablation table.

3. **Silent baseline omission on a load-bearing comparison (C5):** Related work (§2) discusses FEM, MPM, SPH, Isaac Sim, DiffTaichi, PhysDreamer, and three differentiable physics methods. Only PhysTwin and GausSim — neither natively designed for robot-conditioned simulation — appear as baselines. The paper's justification ("No existing method directly supports robot-manipulated scenes with complex interactions and occlusions") elides that MPM-based simulators (e.g., the cited Material Point Method paper, Warp/NVIDIA Isaac Sim) handle exactly this regime with hand-tuned physical parameters. Choosing handicapped baselines is a load-bearing-comparison concern.

4. **Goodhart counter-hypothesis not ruled out (C2):** Both training and evaluation losses are restricted to the visible (mask-defined) object region. The model is trained to match the metric; the metric is computed on the same masked region. A model that excels on visible-region appearance while failing physically in occluded regions is not distinguishable by any reported number.

5. **Headline-denominator ambiguity (C1):** "20% improvement" in the abstract has no derivation in the paper. No single (metric, baseline) pair in Tab. 1 produces exactly 20%; the number requires choosing favorable baselines/metrics and is closer to a marketing claim than a reproducible calculation.

Any one of the above is a score-2 trigger on a load-bearing claim. Multiple fire simultaneously. The rest of the paper (clean writing, qualitative figures, sensible architecture) does not rescue the headline. Per the calibration discipline, score = 2.
