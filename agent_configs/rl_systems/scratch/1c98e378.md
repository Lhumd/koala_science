## Phase A scratch — SQUAD (1c98e378)

### Load-bearing claim inventory (abstract + intro)

1. **C1 (abstract):** "improving the test accuracy up to 5.95% compared to state-of-the-art dynamic solutions with a comparable computational cost". Supported by Table 1 row QUEST_Test vs NACHOS on ImageNet16-120 (57.05 vs 51.10, FM 93.12 vs 95.53).
2. **C2 (abstract):** "reducing the inference latency up to 70.60% compared to static ensembles while maintaining a good accuracy". Supported by Table 1 row QUEST_Mean vs NESBS on CIFAR-10 (FM 204.06 vs 694.02 = 70.60% reduction; accuracy 97.03 vs 97.64).
3. **C3 (abstract):** "the first inference scheme that integrates early-exit mechanisms with distributed ensemble learning". Originality claim, not Soundness.
4. **C4 (abstract):** "ensuring learners are complementary at every intermediate layer" (hierarchical diversity). Supported by Table 2 (PPD per exit, QUEST > NESBS).

### Construct vs operational metric audit

**For C1 ("comparable computational cost"):**
- Construct claimed: "comparable computational cost" — undefined in the abstract.
- Sec. 6.1 explicitly defines TWO computational metrics: **FM** = "metric related to the latency" (max MACs of voting branches at exit); **FMT** = "metric related to energy consumption" (total MACs incl. parallel-overhead leaked work).
- The "comparable" claim holds ONLY on FM. Table 1 numbers on FMT:
  - CIFAR-10: QUEST_Test 603.63M vs NACHOS 212.95M → QUEST consumes 2.83× more total work
  - CIFAR-100: QUEST_Test 878.73M vs NACHOS 312.14M → 2.81× more
  - ImageNet16-120: QUEST_Test 267.71M vs NACHOS 95.53M → 2.80× more
- Sec. 6.3 even acknowledges this on a side-line: "Single models found by CNAS and NACHOS show lower accuracy with comparable FM with respect to QUEST, while have better FMT since they require a forward pass for a single model, different from ensembles". Yet the abstract's "comparable computational cost" framing flattens this.
- **Aspect not measured for QUEST as a fair "comparable cost" claim:** the energy axis (FMT) is reported but not surfaced in the abstract's framing. A reader takes "comparable computational cost" as comparable across all reported cost dimensions.

**For C4 ("hierarchical diversity"):**
- Construct: complementary learners across all intermediate layers.
- Operational metric: PPD (pairwise predictive disagreement) on test set, Table 2.
- Aspect not directly measured: whether the diversity actually causes the early-exit accuracy gains (vs. base architecture quality of QUEST nets, which are searched fresh on DARTS while NACHOS uses MobileNetV3-OFA — different search spaces). The QUEST-vs-NESBS comparison in Table 1 conflates "ensemble + hierarchical diversity" with "DARTS supernet from scratch on this task".

### Reward / objective inventory

- QUEST objective (Eq. 9): minimize G(F_A, F_D) = bi-objective (accuracy ensemble + diversity).
- Training adds Joint Loss L_joint (Eq. 2 sum across exits) + KL regularization + MACs penalty L_C with λ=10⁻³.
- NACHOS objective: Pareto-optimal EENN under MAC constraint, single-model.
- Set-relationship: incomparable search spaces (DARTS for QUEST/NESBS vs MobileNetV3-OFA for NACHOS/CNAS), so reward-overlap analysis is muddier than usual; the more important comparison is QUEST vs NESBS (same DARTS space).

### Counter-hypothesis list

- **CH1: The 5.95% gain on ImageNet16-120 is search-space/architectural, not from quorum-voting.** QUEST uses DARTS-searched cells while NACHOS uses MobileNetV3-OFA. On ImageNet16-120 these may differ in baseline capacity. The paper's QUEST_Test → NESBS comparison on the same DARTS search space gives accuracy 57.05 vs 56.15 = +0.90% on ImageNet16-120 — not 5.95%. Not refuted, although the paper would argue NACHOS is the dynamic-NN baseline, not NESBS.
- **CH2: QUEST_Mean's CIFAR-100 accuracy drop is severe.** NESBS 85.45 → QUEST_Mean 79.97 = -5.48% accuracy drop for 56.36% FM reduction. The "while maintaining a good accuracy" framing on C2 is dataset-specific. Not refuted.
- **CH3: Single-seed everything.** Table 1 has no seed counts, no confidence intervals, no error bars. With <2% margins on CIFAR-10 and 1.63% margin on CIFAR-10 vs NACHOS, this is shaky. Not refuted.

### Multi-objective trade-off audit

- Critical finding: the paper has TWO computational metrics (FM, FMT) defined in Sec. 6.1, both reported in Table 1. The abstract's "comparable computational cost" implicitly equates these by mentioning only one axis-comparison. On the FMT axis (paper-defined energy metric), QUEST uses 2.8× the work of NACHOS on every dataset. The headline "+5.95% accuracy at comparable cost" is single-axis (FM) sold as multi-axis (computational cost generally).

### Headline-number denominator audit

- "+5.95% over dynamic solutions": denominator is NACHOS on ImageNet16-120, the smallest/most-downsampled (16×16) dataset. CIFAR-10 vs NACHOS: +1.63% — well within seed noise range without disclosure.
- "70.60% reduction" denominator: NESBS (static ensemble), CIFAR-10. Other datasets show 56.36% (CIFAR-100) and 42.19% (ImageNet16-120). The headline picks the largest reduction.

### Mechanism-attribution audit

- Coupled novelties: (a) SQUAD inference scheme (quorum + t-test), (b) QUEST NAS strategy (hierarchical diversity), (c) DARTS-based search space. The paper attributes gains to (a)+(b), but the (c) DARTS choice (vs NACHOS's MobileNetV3-OFA) is a confounder for the cross-baseline NACHOS comparison. The QUEST_Test vs NESBS comparison (same DARTS space, isolating the inference + NAS-objective change) is fairer but reduces the headline gains substantially (+0.90% on ImageNet16-120 vs +5.95% headline).

### Framing-vs-demonstration check

- Abstract: "comparable computational cost". Methodology: two cost axes (FM, FMT) reported, only one is "comparable", the other shows ~2.8× cost increase. **Mismatch.**
- Abstract: "while maintaining a good accuracy" for the 70.60% latency claim. Methodology: CIFAR-100 column for QUEST_Mean shows -5.48% accuracy drop vs NESBS — not unambiguously "good". **Borderline mismatch.**

### Soundness score rationale

The paper's two headline empirical claims fire a Score-2 trigger:
- C1's "comparable computational cost" is multi-axis (paper itself defines FM=latency, FMT=energy in Sec. 6.1) but "comparable" only holds on FM; FMT shows QUEST uses ~2.8× the total MACs of NACHOS on all three datasets. This is the **multi-objective trade-off / single-axis comparison sold as multi-axis** trigger from the rubric — the paper itself reports the contradicting axis but the abstract's framing flattens it.
- C2's "while maintaining a good accuracy" papers over a -5.48% accuracy drop on CIFAR-100 (QUEST_Mean vs NESBS). This is a **framing-vs-demonstration** mismatch on a load-bearing claim.

There is offsetting strength: clear methodology section, explicit metric definitions in Sec. 6.1 itself (FM vs FMT), reproducible hyperparameters in Appendix E, the search-space/baseline confounder is partially mitigated by including NESBS as a same-search-space baseline. But the rubric is explicit: "A score-2 trigger fires regardless of offsetting strength" on a load-bearing claim, and these are abstract-level claims that the headline numbers depend on.

**Soundness: 2.** Verbatim-anchored multi-axis-sold-as-single-axis on the abstract's main accuracy-vs-cost claim, with the contradicting axis reported in the same Table 1.

**Confidence: 5.** I quoted Sec. 6.1's verbatim definitions of FM and FMT, Table 1 verbatim numbers (FMT 603.63 vs 212.95 = 2.83×), and the abstract's verbatim framing. Cross-checkable on the paper's own text.
