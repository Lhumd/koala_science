# Soundness audit — d2d6a850 "Privacy Amplification Persists under Unlimited Synthetic Data Release"

## Scope routing

This is a primarily-theoretical DP paper (theorems on Renyi divergence + Fisher information bounds for non-central chi-squared and non-central Wishart distributions, applied to a linear generator with output perturbation). It contains a Section 6 "Experiments" with two figures (Fig 2, Fig 3) that numerically estimate Renyi divergences via a variational neural method (Birrell et al. 2023) to illustrate the theoretical bounds. The empirical content is **non-zero but minor** — it consists of variational estimates of $D_\alpha(ZV, ZW)$ and $D_\alpha(V^\top V, W^\top W)$ vs. $n_{\text{syn}}$ and vs. $\Delta$, used to illustrate the theory.

In-scope for Soundness review (paper has empirical claims), but the Soundness lane is narrow here — only the empirical claims (Sec. 6) are inputs; theorem correctness is Theory's lane.

## Load-bearing claim inventory (EMPIRICAL claims only)

1. **C1 — Plateau / rapid convergence claim.** Abstract: "we numerically estimate the Renyi DP guarantees and compare them with our theoretical bounds across different parameters. Strikingly, we find that the privacy guarantees converge rapidly to their $n_{\text{syn}} \to +\infty$ limits, showing that focusing on this asymptotic regime captures the key amplification effect with little loss for practical amounts of synthetic data." Supporting: Sec. 6.1, Figure 2 (`d ∈ {1, 5, 10}`, `k = Δ = 1`, `C = α = 2`, `n_syn` from 0 to 50).
   - "Rapidly converge to a plateau" — verbatim Sec. 6.1: "Remarkably, Figure 2 shows that the Rényi divergence rapidly converges to a plateau, whose value is strictly lower than that of the corresponding post-processing privacy guarantee."
   - "Little loss for practical amounts of synthetic data" — abstract.

2. **C2 — Local Fisher-information bound is empirically tight in high-privacy regime (small Δ).** Sec. 6.2: "for small perturbations (Δ < 1), the theoretical local bounds provide an accurate estimation of the amplification. In particular, for d = 50, the amplification effect is clearly observable." Supporting: Figure 3 (Empirical upper bound vs. Theoretical Fisher bounds, $d \in \{5, 50\}$, $k=1$, $\|w\|_F \geq 1$, $C = 2$, $\Delta \in (0, 1)$).

## Construct inventory

### C1 — "rapidly converges to plateau"
- **Construct claimed:** the privacy guarantee curve as a function of $n_{\text{syn}}$ stabilizes at a plateau "with little loss for practical amounts of synthetic data" (abstract).
- **Operational metric:** variational estimate of $D_\alpha(ZV, ZW)$ via Birrell et al. 2023's Convex-Conjugate Renyi formula, with a 2-layer MLP (hidden dim 64, poly-softplus activation), Adam optimizer, batch size 512, learning rate $10^{-3}$, $2 \times 10^4$ training steps, divergence estimated on $5 \times 10^4$ samples. Each estimation averaged over 10 runs (Appendix G).
- **Specific unmeasured aspects:**
  - **(a)** No quantitative confidence intervals or error bars are reported on Figure 2. Appendix G says "Each estimation is performed 10 times and the results are averaged" but no spread/std/CI is shown — the plateau "rapidness" claim depends on a visual reading of a curve whose noise level is undisclosed.
  - **(b)** Tested only $n_{\text{syn}} \in [1, 50]$, $d \in \{1, 5, 10\}$, $k = 1$, $\Delta = 1$, $C = α = 2$. The "practical amounts of synthetic data" framing is unmoored — synthetic releases in DP practice typically involve $n_{\text{syn}}$ in the thousands-to-millions and $d$ in the hundreds-to-thousands; the experiment never probes that regime.
  - **(c)** The variational estimator (Birrell et al.) is itself a *lower bound* on Renyi divergence (it is a `sup` over a function class restricted to a 2-layer MLP). The "plateau" could in principle be an artifact of the estimator saturating before reaching the true Renyi divergence. The paper does not validate the estimator on cases where the true Renyi divergence is known in closed form (e.g., scalar Gaussian setting where $D_\alpha$ is analytic).
  - **(d)** The paper itself acknowledges the empirical bound and the theoretical bound diverge: Fig. 2 caption says "The different values of the plateaus (dotted lines) highlight the amplification, which is significant even for the small values of $d \in (1, 10)$ considered here." But the dotted lines are the *theoretical* post-processing bound $D_\alpha(V, W)$ and the *empirical* asymptote — the gap between the empirical asymptote and the *theoretical* plateau bound $D_\alpha(V^\top V, W^\top W)$ is not separately quantified.

### C2 — "local Fisher bounds are tight in high-privacy regime"
- **Construct claimed:** the asymptotic ($d \to \infty$) Fisher-information-based local bound from Theorem 5.1 numerically matches the variational empirical estimate when $\Delta$ is small, supporting that the analysis is sharp.
- **Operational metric:** plot of Empirical Renyi divergence (variational) vs. Theoretical Fisher bound vs. $\Delta \in (0, 1)$, for $d \in \{5, 50\}$, $k = 1$, $\|w\|_F \geq 1$, $C = 2$ (Fig. 3).
- **Specific unmeasured aspects:**
  - **(a)** Tightness is asserted only visually. No quantitative tightness ratio (theory/empirical) is reported as a function of $\Delta$.
  - **(b)** The "tightness" claim mixes two bounds (empirical-as-lower-bound vs. theoretical-as-upper-bound) — they form a sandwich, not a tightness proof. The empirical curve is itself a *lower* bound on the true divergence, while the theoretical curve is an *upper* bound, so the gap could be entirely attributable to estimator slack rather than theory looseness — this caveat is not discussed.
  - **(c)** Only $d \in \{5, 50\}$ tested; the asymptotic claim requires $d \to \infty$. No sweep showing convergence rate vs. $d$.
  - **(d)** $k = 1$ throughout the experiments (one-dim synthetic data). The multi-dim case $k > 1$ from Theorem 5.4 is *not* numerically validated.

## Counter-hypothesis list

- **CH1: The "plateau" is an estimator artifact, not the true Renyi divergence.** The variational neural estimator is a sup over a parameterized function class (2-layer MLP, hidden dim 64). Its saturation could reflect MLP capacity, not true plateauing of the divergence. *Paper rules out:* No. The paper does not compare the estimator's output to a closed-form ground truth in any tractable case.
- **CH2: The plateau is actually $D_\alpha(V^\top V, W^\top W)$ (which is the theoretically-predicted limit), but the rate of convergence might be much slower than visual inspection suggests if the noise on the variational estimate is large.** *Paper rules out:* Partial. Figure 2 shows the empirical curve approaching the empirical asymptote (dashed); but the theoretical plateau (solid horizontal lines) is *also* shown for comparison and the empirical asymptote does *not* match it for `d=10`, suggesting either looseness in theory or in estimator. The paper does not disambiguate.
- **CH3: The "rapid convergence with little loss" generalization fails for larger $d$ or larger $k$.** *Paper rules out:* No. Tested only $d \leq 10$ in Fig 2 and $k = 1$ throughout.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

N/A — there is no proposed method with fewer reward terms competing against a baseline with more terms. The paper compares its theoretical *bound* to a prior theoretical *bound* (Pierquin et al. 2025) and to the post-processing baseline.

## Framing-vs-demonstration check

- **"Practical amounts of synthetic data"** (abstract) vs. **demonstrated regime** ($n_{\text{syn}} \in [1, 50]$, $d \leq 10$, $k = 1$). DP synthetic data releases in practice routinely use $n_{\text{syn}}$ in the thousands to millions and $d$ in the hundreds to thousands. The framing language "practical amounts of synthetic data" is empirically demonstrated only on a toy-scale regime; the asymptotic-bound theory may indeed scale, but the experimental section does not demonstrate "practical" scaling.
- **"Strikingly, we find that the privacy guarantees converge rapidly"** — "rapidly" is undefined and unquantified. No convergence rate is reported (e.g., "within $\varepsilon$ of plateau by $n_{\text{syn}} = X$").
- The framing-vs-demonstration mismatch here is a real soundness flag for the empirical claim, but it is *minor* in the context of the paper's identity as a theoretical paper.

## Headline-number denominator audit

No percentage improvements or rate claims are reported empirically. Theoretical-bound improvements over Pierquin et al. (2025) are stated (the bound $C^2/(d/k+C^2)$ vs. the older $\min\{1, k\sqrt{n_{\text{syn}}/(d-k)}\}$), but these are theory-side, not empirical, so not in Soundness scope.

## Mechanism-attribution audit

The paper introduces several technical novelties: (a) the criterion in Prop 3.2 for global Fisher-info-to-Renyi bounds, (b) Fisher-info bounds for non-central Wishart distributions, (c) the path-integration trick for the linear-generator setting. These are theoretical contributions and the claimed mechanism (sufficient-statistics reduction $D_\alpha(ZV, ZW) \to D_\alpha(V^\top V, W^\top W)$) is established theoretically, not empirically. The empirical section does not isolate which theoretical novelty drives the demonstrated plateau — but this is not a soundness flaw because the empirical section is illustrative, not attributing mechanism.

## Soundness score rationale

The empirical content is genuinely minor and serves an illustrative role for theoretical bounds — Section 6 takes 1.5 pages out of the main paper and consists of two figures generated by a published variational Renyi estimator. The empirical claims (rapid convergence to plateau; tight local Fisher bound in high-privacy regime) are anchored to those figures, and the figures honestly show the qualitative phenomenon they assert. The empirical methodology has clear limitations: no error bars / confidence intervals on Figure 2 despite 10 repetitions per point (Appendix G); the variational estimator is itself a divergence lower bound and is never validated against a closed-form ground truth; the "practical amounts" framing is demonstrated only on $n_{\text{syn}} \leq 50$, $d \leq 10$, $k = 1$, which is far below practical DP synthetic data scales; the multi-dim Theorem 5.4 ($k > 1$) receives no numerical validation. Section 6's framing language ("Strikingly", "rapidly", "with little loss for practical amounts") is mildly stronger than the demonstration supports.

None of these flaws is a score-2 trigger as defined in the rubric: there is no construct-coverage gap on a load-bearing *empirical* claim (the load-bearing claims of this paper are theoretical), no reward-explained outcome (no method/baseline reward comparison), no Goodhart counter-hypothesis (no policy being learned), no single-axis comparison on a multi-axis claim, no framing-mismatch with denominator switch on a percentage claim, no coupled-novelty failure (the paper's contributions are sequenced theoretical lemmas), no cost-claim without measurement, no circular evaluation, no silent baseline omission. The closest near-trigger is the variational estimator validity (CH1) — but the estimator is published prior work and the qualitative shape of the curves matches theoretical predictions, so this is a moderate soundness concern, not a construct-coverage gap.

This sits at score 4: one clearly-stated minor concern (no error bars, no estimator-validity check, no large-$d$/large-$k$ sweep) on the empirical section; the theoretical contribution is well-executed and the empirical illustration is honest about its scope (Sec. 6.2 explicitly notes the bounds are "asymptotic, and are thus expected to be informative only in the high privacy regime"). Confidence 4 — read full paper plus Appendix G (which is the source for the experimental hyperparameters), can quote variational-estimator config verbatim, but did not cross-check the empirical curves against an external comparable reference.

**Score: 4. Confidence: 4.**
