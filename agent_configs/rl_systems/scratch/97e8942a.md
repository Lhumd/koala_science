# Phase A scratch: Conformal Policy Control (97e8942a)

## Load-bearing claim inventory

**C1 (abstract).** "We show how to use any safe reference policy as a probabilistic regulator for any optimized but untested policy. Conformal calibration on data from the safe policy determines how aggressively the new policy can act, while provably enforcing the user's declared risk tolerance."
- Location: Abstract, sentence 4.
- Supported by: Theorem 4.5, Sec. 5.1 (medical QA), Sec. 5.2 (active learning), Sec. 5.3 (sequence opt).

**C2 (abstract).** "Unlike previous conformal methods, our theory provides finite-sample guarantees even for non-monotonic bounded loss functions."
- Location: Abstract.
- Supported by: Theorem 4.2 + Fig. 2 (synthetic) + Fig. 4 (medical QA FDR control).

**C3 (abstract, headline empirical claim).** "Our experiments on applications ranging from natural language question answering to biomolecular engineering show that safe exploration is not only possible from the first moment of deployment, but can also improve performance."
- Location: Abstract, final sentence.
- "Can also improve performance" supported by: Fig. 5 (lower test MSE under risk control vs. uncontrolled, in some cases) and Fig. 6 (moderate α stabilizes optimization, beats uncontrolled).

**C4 (Sec. 1).** "In medical question answering, gCRC controls the false discovery rate, a non-monotonic loss that standard CRC cannot handle, achieving tighter control and better claim recall than existing methods."
- Location: Sec. 1, paragraph 8.
- Supported by: Fig. 4 (FDR + recall vs α on MedLFQA).

**C5 (Sec. 1).** "In constrained active learning, CPC provides finite-sample guarantees under feedback-loop covariate shifts where previous approaches offered only asymptotic control."
- Location: Sec. 1, paragraph 8.
- Supported by: Fig. 5 (constraint violation rate at α=0.2 across 3 tabular regression datasets).

**C6 (Sec. 1).** "In black-box biomolecular sequence optimization, CPC extends conformal methods to a setting where no prior guarantees existed, and we find that moderate risk control can counter-intuitively improve optimization performance."
- Location: Sec. 1, paragraph 8.
- Supported by: Fig. 6 (Ehrlich function optimization with Pythia 14M + DPO).

## Construct inventory

**For C1 ("provably enforcing the user's declared risk tolerance"):**
- Construct: "risk tolerance" = a finite-sample bound on E[L_{n+1}] under marginal expectation over context and calibration draws.
- Operational metric: empirical fraction of constraint violations / FDR / infeasibility rate at deployment time, averaged over many runs.
- Aspects NOT measured:
  - **Conditional risk** under specific contexts (the paper acknowledges this in Discussion: "less informative for individual decisions, where one asks whether a particular action is safe for a particular context").
  - **Robustness to assumption violations**: what happens if the safe policy assumption is violated (i.e., π₀ does not actually satisfy E[ℓ] ≤ α)? No experiment perturbs this.
  - **Non-stationary context distribution**: the paper assumes stationary p(X); the empirical evaluation does not test what happens under genuine context shift.

**For C3 ("can also improve performance"):**
- Construct: "improve performance" — risk-controlled policy outperforms uncontrolled on the reward axis.
- Operational metric: test MSE (Fig. 5 active learning) and Ehrlich score (Fig. 6 sequence opt), comparing CPC at various α to "Uncontrolled".
- Aspects NOT measured:
  - **Whether the improvement is specific to CPC's calibration mechanism** vs. ANY constraint that biases sampling toward the high-PC1 manifold (in active learning) or away from infeasible-prone regions (in sequence opt).
  - **No ablation against a non-conformal but PC1-aware constraint** in active learning.
  - **No comparison to a fixed-β constraint without conformal calibration** (would isolate calibration value vs. mere clipping value).

**For C4 ("better claim recall than existing methods"):**
- Construct: recall of true claims at fixed FDR target.
- Operational metric: recall in Fig. 4 right panel.
- Aspects measured well — direct head-to-head against monotonized CRC and LTT on the same dataset/splits.

**For C5 ("finite-sample guarantees under feedback-loop covariate shifts"):**
- Construct: covariate shift induced by the agent's own multi-round acquisition.
- Operational metric: empirical constraint violation rate ≤ α=0.2 over 100 active-learning steps (Fig. 5 top row).
- Aspect NOT measured: the "feasibility" being controlled is a SYNTHETIC construct built from the first principal component of the covariate matrix (Appendix E.2: "Our datasets do not include recorded feasibility constraints, so we construct synthetic constraints based on the first principal component"). The construct that risk control is demonstrated on is one the authors hand-defined, not a real-world feasibility notion (e.g., physical safety, regulatory).

## Reward / objective inventory

This is a calibration/control paper, not an RL training paper, so "rewards" are tasks not policies. Tasks:
- MedQA: reward = recall of true claims; loss = FDR (non-monotonic in τ); subclaim score = self-evaluation, log-probabilities, or frequency.
- Active learning: reward = test MSE on holdout; loss = synthetic feasibility violation indicator built from PC1.
- Sequence opt: reward = Ehrlich function score; loss = infeasibility indicator from Markov-process feasibility set.

The proposed method (CPC) and the safe-policy baseline use the SAME objective. CPC just clips the likelihood ratio at calibrated β. So no reward-overlap audit applies in the standard reward-shaping sense — but the analogous question is whether the "improvement" claim is explained by the clipping mechanism (which restricts to the safe policy's support) rather than by the calibration mechanism (which sets the right β). The paper does not run a fixed-β baseline.

## Counter-hypothesis list

**For "moderate risk control improves performance" (C3):**

CH-A: The improvement is due to restricting acquisition to high-PC1 (high-feasibility-by-construction) regions, which also happen to be high-density-of-training-data regions for the GP — a generic distribution-shift mitigation effect rather than CPC-specific. Rule-out attempt: a non-conformal PC1-aware acquisition policy (e.g., simply down-weight low-PC1 samples) would test this. **Not ruled out** by the paper.

CH-B: The improvement is due to *any* clipping-style constraint that bounds the likelihood ratio, regardless of whether β is conformally calibrated. A fixed-β=1 baseline would test this. **Not ruled out**; only "Uncontrolled" and "Conformal Controller (Lekeufack et al. 2024)" appear in Fig. 5.

CH-C (sequence opt): The improvement at moderate α in Fig. 6 reflects DPO's known instability under high-infeasibility data (the appendix F.3 even cites Chen et al. 2025 noting DPO struggles with poor feasibility rates). Any feasibility-prefiltering would help, not specifically CPC's. **Not ruled out** — no ablation against a fixed-feasibility-budget baseline.

**For "provable risk control" (C1):**

CH-D: The empirical "control" is observed only on the same exchangeable distribution used for calibration. Under genuine context-distribution shift (e.g., MedLFQA train/test split with different question topics), would CPC still control risk? The Discussion acknowledges this is unaddressed. **Not tested.**

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

The paper claims "tighter control and better claim recall" for gCRC vs. monotonized-CRC and LTT. Both axes (FDR control, recall) ARE reported in Fig. 4 and Fig. 8 across three subclaim scoring methods. This is genuinely multi-axis. No single-axis hidden tradeoff issue here.

For active learning: claims "lower test MSE than uncontrolled" but reports BOTH constraint violation AND test MSE in Fig. 5 — multi-axis.

## Framing-vs-demonstration check

**Framing (abstract):** "provably enforcing the user's declared risk tolerance" + "applications ranging from natural language question answering to biomolecular engineering show that safe exploration is not only possible from the first moment of deployment, but can also improve performance."

**Demonstration:** Three experiments. Risk tolerance "enforcement" is shown empirically only on the same exchangeable distribution used for calibration — the theory is marginal-coverage which is what's tested. Improvement claim depends on the synthetic-feasibility setup in active learning and on DPO sequence opt with a 14M-parameter Pythia model — small-scale.

The "natural language question answering to biomolecular engineering" framing implies real-world applicability. The biomolecular experiment is on synthetic Ehrlich functions (Chen et al. 2025), not real wet-lab data. The active learning is synthetic feasibility constraints. The MedQA is the only "real" application. This is somewhat oversold by the framing — but the limitation is not hidden (Section 5.3 explicitly says "synthetic test functions designed to capture the geometric structure"). I'd call this aspirational framing rather than a hard mismatch — not a clean Score-2 trigger.

**Denominator audit:** The "improvement" claims are denominated against "Uncontrolled" — a deliberately-aggressive uncertainty-tilted policy. Not denominated against any tuned safe baseline (e.g., a Seldonian algorithm with the same risk level). The paper does not state percentage improvements, so no clean denominator-switch trigger.

## Headline-number denominator audit

No clean denominator-switch issue. The numbers reported are absolute (FDR, MSE, score per round), not percentage improvements over a chosen baseline.

## Mechanism-attribution audit

The paper claims that CPC's mechanism (conformally calibrating β to control likelihood-ratio expansion) drives both safety AND improvement. The improvement claim is the more vulnerable one:

- The 2x2 ablation that would isolate "conformal calibration of β" from "any β > 0 clipping" is missing for active learning and sequence opt.
- Specifically, what would a fixed β = 1 (i.e., π_t cannot exceed π_safe in any action's likelihood) baseline look like in Fig. 5 and Fig. 6? The paper does not show this.
- This is exactly the "coupled-novelty" pattern: novelty A = the clipping construction; novelty B = the conformal calibration of the clip threshold. Ablations show CPC(α) variants and "Uncontrolled" but not "Clipping(β=fixed)" as an intermediate.

## Soundness score rationale

Headline claims C1, C2, C4 are well-supported: theorem proven, FDR-control demonstrated on MedLFQA with 10 seeds (Appendix Table 2) across 3 subclaim scoring methods, head-to-head with appropriate baselines (monotonized CRC, LTT). The "tighter control + higher recall" claim in MedQA is a clean win with multi-axis reporting and 25-split error bars in Fig. 8.

The vulnerability is on C3's "can improve performance" claim, which leans on Fig. 5 (active learning) and Fig. 6 (sequence opt). Specifically:

(a) **Counter-hypothesis CH-A (PC1-coupling)**: in active learning, the "feasibility" is defined as a logistic-CDF of rank of exponentially-tilted PC1 (Eq. 73). The conformally-controlled acquisition policy is constrained to act more like the initial safe policy, which was sampled with EXACTLY THE SAME exponential tilting toward high-PC1 (Appendix E.3: "Initial pool size n_initial=48... exponential tilting toward high PC1 values, using the same bias parameter γ from Section E.2"). So the synthetic infeasibility is correlated with the same PC axis along which the GP regression has more training data. Lower test MSE under CPC is at least partially explained by "stay near training data manifold" — a generic distribution-shift mitigation orthogonal to CPC's calibration novelty. No ablation rules this out.

(b) **Mechanism attribution gap**: CPC = clipping construction (novelty A) + conformal β calibration (novelty B). The "improvement" claim attributes gains to the calibration. A fixed-β clipping baseline (without calibration) is missing from Fig. 5 and Fig. 6.

(c) These are real but not fatal — they apply to a secondary claim (the "improvement" half of C3) while the primary claims (C1: provable control, C2: non-monotonic loss extension, C4: FDR-control on MedLFQA) are well-supported.

The paper has an offsetting well-executed section (medical QA: 10 seeds, 25 random splits in Fig. 8, multi-axis reporting, three subclaim scoring methods, three baselines including LTT). Theory is rigorous. Reproducibility appears high (code released at github.com/samuelstanton/conformal-policy-control).

The mechanism-attribution gap on the "improvement" claim is a genuine score-3 flaw on a load-bearing abstract sentence ("can also improve performance"). The synthetic-feasibility-correlated-with-GP-support coupling in active learning is a counter-hypothesis the paper does not rule out. Combined with the missing fixed-β ablation, this is one major gap.

This is best characterized as **Score 4**: one major mechanism-attribution gap on a load-bearing-but-secondary improvement claim, with strong offsetting execution on the primary claims (theorem + medical QA). I considered Score 2 under the coupled-novelty trigger, but: (i) the "improvement" claim is qualified ("can also improve" rather than "improves") and the primary contribution (provable risk control) is genuinely demonstrated; (ii) the construct of "risk control" itself IS appropriately measured under the marginal-coverage framing the paper claims; (iii) the limitation around context-shift and conditional guarantees is openly acknowledged in Discussion. The flaws are real but concentrated on a secondary claim.

**Score: 4**
