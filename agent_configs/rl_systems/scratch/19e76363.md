# Scratch: 19e76363 — Scaling Medical Reasoning Verification via Tool-Integrated RL (Med-TIV)

## Load-Bearing Claim Inventory

**Claim 1 (Abstract, lines 31–37):** "Across four medical reasoning benchmarks, Med-TIV achieves substantial gains over existing methods, improving MedQA accuracy by 23.5% and MedXpertQA by 32.0% relative to the base generator model alone."

**Claim 2 (Abstract, lines 35–38):** "Med-TIV demonstrates an 8× reduction in sampling budget requirement compared to prior reward model baselines."

**Claim 3 (Abstract, lines 38–41):** "These findings establish that grounding verification in dynamically retrieved evidence offers a principled path toward more reliable medical reasoning systems."

**Claim 4 (Introduction, lines 100–106):** "Med-TIV trains strong medical verifiers: when guiding inference-time search for a 7B generator model, our trained verifier achieves relative improvements of 23.5% on MedQA and 32.0% on MedXpertQA compared to the generator model alone."

**Claim 5 (Introduction, lines 103–106):** "Moreover, Med-TIV consistently outperforms existing medical reward model baselines and surpasses the performance of models that are up to 4× larger in scale."

**Claim 6 (Introduction, lines 105–107):** "Notably, Med-TIV also demonstrates an 8× gain in sampling efficiency compared to prior reward-based approaches, achieving equivalent accuracy with substantially fewer sampled reasoning traces during test-time search."

**Claim 7 (Sec. 4.3 Q6 / Table 3):** "RL training drives the primary gain, boosting MedQA accuracy of Qwen2.5-7B by 8.64%, confirming that the verifier effectively internalizes reasoning patterns. Tool integration provides a critical secondary boost, further elevating accuracy to 70.54%." — This is the mechanism-attribution claim for RL vs. tool components.

## Construct Inventory

### Claim 1 & 4: "Improving MedQA accuracy by 23.5% relative to the base generator model"

- **Construct claimed:** Med-TIV improves medical reasoning accuracy substantially over the base generator.
- **Operational metric:** MedQA accuracy (%) using Hard-Weighted Self-Consistency with N=32 candidate traces, comparing Qwen2.5-7B base (60.96%) to Med-TIV guided search (75.26%). Relative improvement = (75.26 − 60.96)/60.96 ≈ 23.5%.
- **Denominator audit:** The 23.5% is a *relative* improvement over the *unaided base generator* (60.96%). The comparison is not against the strongest same-scale baseline (AlphaMed-7B at 71.01% standalone, or Med-PRM at 69.99% standalone). The paper does compare Med-TIV against verifier-guided AlphaMed-7B at 75.65% (Table 2), which is competitive, so the headline number over base generator is framing-dominant.
- **Specific aspect NOT captured by this metric:** The metric evaluates a verifier's ability to select among multiple-choice answers (binary classification of reasoning traces on USMLE-style MCQs). It does not capture: (a) open-ended clinical reasoning quality, (b) calibration of the confidence score r_τ in the confidence range [0,1], (c) error type distribution (false positive rate on correct traces vs false negative rate on incorrect traces). Binary classification accuracy on MCQ final-answer selection is a narrow proxy for "reliable medical reasoning."

### Claim 2 & 6: "8× reduction in sampling budget requirement"

- **Construct claimed:** Med-TIV requires 8× fewer candidate traces to achieve equivalent performance to prior reward model baselines at N=32.
- **Operational metric:** From Figure 3, Med-TIV achieves ~72.1% on MedQA at N=4, while Med-PRM requires N=32 to reach ~70.0%. Ratio: 32/4 = 8×.
- **Specific aspect NOT captured:** (a) Wall-clock cost is never measured. The 8× sampling ratio assumes inference cost scales linearly with N, but Med-TIV's verifier performs multiple iterative tool-retrieval steps per candidate trace evaluation (multi-turn search), which inflates per-trace evaluation cost substantially relative to Med-PRM's static RAG pass. The total FLOPs or wall-clock comparison is not reported anywhere in the paper (main text or appendix). The claim "equivalent performance at one-eighth the generator inference cost" (lines 375–378) is stated as if cost ≈ number of sampled generator traces, ignoring verifier inference overhead.

### Claim 3: "Grounding verification in dynamically retrieved evidence offers a principled path toward more reliable medical reasoning systems"

- **Construct claimed:** Dynamic retrieval causes or explains the performance gains (mechanism attribution).
- **Operational metric:** Table 3 ablation — Qwen2.5-7B base 60.96% → +Med-TIV (RL): 69.60% → +Med-TIV (RL + Tool): 70.54%. Tool adds +0.94 percentage points (pp) over RL alone.
- **Specific aspect NOT captured:** The marginal contribution of *dynamic* tool use vs. simply having a retrieval-augmented verifier at all is not isolated. The paper equips Med-PRM with the same retrieval corpus for controlled comparison (Appendix B.3), but does not ablate: (a) a static-RAG variant of Med-TIV (fixed top-k pre-context, no iterative search) to isolate the iterative-retrieval mechanism, (b) the number of search steps (whether 1 step vs. multi-step matters). The 0.94 pp gain attributable to tools in Table 3 is described as a "critical secondary boost," but the primary gain is from RL (8.64 pp), and tool integration is marginal at +0.94 pp on MedQA with Qwen2.5-7B.

### Claim 7: Mechanism-attribution (RL vs. Tool)

- **Construct claimed:** RL internalizes reasoning patterns and tools provide additive grounding benefit.
- **Operational metric:** Table 3 two-step ablation on MedQA (Qwen2.5-7B and AlphaMed-7B).
- **Specific aspect NOT captured:** The ablation does not isolate whether the tool integration benefit derives from: (a) the model having seen tool-retrieval trajectories during RL training (changing the policy distribution), vs. (b) the retrieved information itself at inference time being informative. A model trained with RL but evaluated with tool access (without training on tool-integrated trajectories) is missing.

## Reward / Objective Inventory

**Correctness Reward R_c:** Binary indicator — 1 if the verifier's final answer tag matches the ground-truth trace-level label ℓ ∈ {0,1}; 0 otherwise. Formula: R_c = 1[extract(o) = ℓ].

**Format Reward R_f:**
- R_f = 1 if all formatting constraints satisfied AND ≤10 <answer> tag pairs.
- R_f = 0.25 if output is correct but exhibits tag overflow.
- R_f = 0 otherwise.

**Total Reward:** R = R_c × R_f.

**Objective (Dr.GRPO):** Maximize advantage-weighted log-probabilities of trajectories. Advantage computed as R(q, o_i) − mean({R(q, o_1), ..., R(q, o_G)}).

**Training data:** Derived exclusively from the Med-PRM dataset (trace-level labels only; step-level labels intentionally excluded). Balanced distribution enforced: 50% correct / 50% incorrect traces per batch.

**Baseline reward comparison:** Med-PRM uses step-level labels with static RAG for process reward modeling. MedS³ uses self-evolved soft dual-sided process supervision. Neither of these is directly analogous to Med-TIV's binary trace-level correctness reward on search trajectories.

## Counter-Hypothesis List

**Counter-hypothesis 1 (Benchmark ceiling / test contamination):** The four benchmarks (MedQA, MedMCQA, MMLU-Med, MedXpertQA) are standard, widely-used benchmarks. Med-TIV training uses the Med-PRM dataset, which is derived from the same USMLE-style questions that underlie MedQA. If training data overlaps with evaluation questions (even indirectly through shared question pools or derived sets), accuracy improvements may reflect memorization rather than verification ability. The paper does not report any overlap analysis between Med-PRM training questions and evaluation set questions. This is a genuine concern with Med-PRM-derived training data and MedQA evaluation.
→ Status: **Not ruled out.** The paper acknowledges training uses Med-PRM data and evaluates on MedQA, but provides no decontamination analysis.

**Counter-hypothesis 2 (Tool-retrieval acts as an additional reasoning budget, not grounding):** The iterative search process adds additional compute steps (reasoning tokens + retrieval). Even without meaningful document retrieval (e.g., retrieving generic or off-topic snippets), the additional chain-of-thought reasoning steps could produce accuracy gains. A control with a "null retrieval" verifier (same architecture, same iterative reasoning structure, but retrieval returns empty or random results) is missing.
→ Status: **Not ruled out.** Table 3 ablates RL vs. RL+Tool, but does not ablate what happens if the search engine is replaced with noise or empty results.

**Counter-hypothesis 3 (Hard-Weighted Self-Consistency dominates):** The primary inference strategy (Hard-Weighted SC) filters out traces the verifier labels incorrect, then majority-votes on final answers. The filtering step already does substantial work — any verifier that reaches moderate precision could produce similar gains. The 23.5% improvement claim does not isolate how much comes from the verifier's discriminative quality vs. the majority-voting aggregation over 32 samples.
→ Status: **Partially addressed.** Table 2 compares multiple test-time strategies (Best-of-N, Soft-SC, Hard-SC), showing Hard-SC is best. But the marginal contribution of Med-TIV's verifier quality (above a threshold verifier that simply labels everything as "correct") is not shown.

**Counter-hypothesis 4 (Wall-clock cost equivalence of "8× sampling efficiency"):** Med-TIV's verifier performs multi-turn retrieval for each trace evaluation. If the verifier makes 3–5 retrieval calls per trace and each call + processing adds latency comparable to generating one candidate trace, the actual wall-clock cost at N=4 for Med-TIV may exceed Med-PRM at N=32. The paper explicitly does not measure wall-clock or FLOPs.
→ Status: **Not ruled out.** Confirmed by the Limitation section not addressing wall-clock.

## Multi-Objective Trade-Off Audit

Med-TIV is a verifier, not a policy model — it does not directly optimize multiple reward terms simultaneously in the generator. However, for the evaluation construct:

- The verifier is trained on trace-level binary labels (correct/incorrect). It produces a single binary judgment plus a confidence score.
- The "reliable medical reasoning" construct the paper claims to advance includes: factual correctness, logical soundness, calibration, and interpretability.
- **Factual correctness** is measured (MCQ accuracy on 4 benchmarks).
- **Calibration** of the confidence score r_τ ∈ [0,1] is never measured — expected calibration error, reliability diagrams, or Brier score are absent from main text and appendix.
- **Interpretability** of the verification trace is qualitatively illustrated in one case (Appendix D), but no systematic analysis of critique quality, hallucination rate in retrieved evidence, or factual accuracy of the verifier's own reasoning steps is provided.
- **False positive / false negative rates** (verifier precision/recall as a classifier) are not reported — only the downstream MCQ accuracy improvement is reported.

## Framing-vs-Demonstration Check

**Framing 1 (Abstract):** "improving MedQA accuracy by 23.5% and MedXpertQA by 32.0% relative to the base generator model alone."
**Methodology reality:** The 23.5% is a *relative* gain over Qwen2.5-7B single-sample (60.96%), using Hard-Weighted SC over N=32 traces (Table 2: 60.96% → 75.26%). This is a favorable framing. A reader might interpret "base generator model" as the same generator with standard SC, but standard SC at N=32 already gives 66.38% — a 14.3% relative gain, not 23.5%. The headline improvement is computed relative to the weakest baseline (single-trace, no aggregation), not relative to competitive aggregation strategies.

**Framing 2 (Introduction, lines 105–107):** "achieving equivalent accuracy with substantially fewer sampled reasoning traces during test-time search."
**Methodology reality:** The "substantially fewer" = 8×, comparing N=4 for Med-TIV (~72.1%) vs. N=32 for Med-PRM (~70%). This comparison is N-of-generator-traces only and ignores the verifier's own inference cost (multi-turn retrieval loops per trace). The paper claims inference cost "scales approximately linearly with the number of sampled traces" (lines 375–376), which is only true if the verifier cost per trace is negligible or equal to prior baselines — an undemonstrated assumption.

**Framing 3 (Sec. 4.3 Q6):** "Tool integration provides a critical secondary boost, further elevating accuracy to 70.54%."
**Methodology reality:** The "critical" descriptor is used for a +0.94 pp gain (69.60% → 70.54% on MedQA, Qwen2.5-7B). This is marginally statistically meaningful, not "critical." No confidence intervals or standard deviations are reported anywhere in the main results (Table 1, Table 2, Table 3).

## Headline-Number Denominator Audit

**23.5% improvement claim:**
- Denominator: Qwen2.5-7B single-pass accuracy = 60.96% (Table 1, first row of Table 2).
- Numerator: 75.26% (Med-TIV Hard-Weighted SC, N=32, Table 2).
- Relative gain = (75.26 − 60.96) / 60.96 = 23.45%. ✓ Arithmetic checks out.
- **Issue:** The denominator is the unassisted, single-sample base model, not any aggregation or competitive same-scale baseline. Self-Consistency (no verifier) at N=32 already reaches 66.38% (Table 2). The improvement over a strong, aggregation-matched baseline is only (75.26 − 66.38)/66.38 ≈ 13.4% relative.

**32.0% MedXpertQA improvement claim:**
- From Table 1: Qwen2.5-7B base MedXpertQA = 12.15%; Med-TIV = 16.04%. Relative gain = (16.04 − 12.15)/12.15 ≈ 32.0%. ✓ Arithmetic checks out.
- **Issue:** MedXpertQA absolute accuracy is 16.04% — a very low base, and the absolute improvement is 3.89 pp. The large relative gain (32%) reflects a low-accuracy denominator, not a clinically significant improvement in expert-level reasoning. MedXpertQA is described as "expert-level benchmark" — at 16.04%, the system is far from expert-level performance.

**8× sampling efficiency claim:**
- Med-TIV at N=4 ≈ 72.1% (MedQA, Figure 3); Med-PRM at N=32 ≈ 70.0% (Figure 3). Ratio = 32/4 = 8×.
- **Issue:** This comparison ignores per-trace verifier inference cost. Med-TIV's verifier is running multi-turn search for each of the N=4 traces. If the verifier adds 2–4 additional inference passes per trace (search formulation + retrieval + reasoning), total compute is not 4 units but 4 × (1 + k_retrieval) where k_retrieval is unspecified. The paper never reports retrieval step counts or verifier FLOPs.

## Mechanism-Attribution Audit

**Coupled novelties:** Med-TIV introduces three coupled components simultaneously:
1. Tool-augmented iterative retrieval during verification.
2. Dr.GRPO RL training on trace-level binary labels.
3. Adaptive curriculum formulation (filtering trivial/impossible instances).

**Ablations provided:**
- Table 3: Qwen2.5-7B base → +RL only (69.60%) → +RL+Tool (70.54%). This isolates RL vs. RL+Tool.
- Figure 4: Base → Iter-1 → Iter-2 (effect of iterative training).
- Q6 text: qualitative description of RL driving primary gain.

**Missing ablations:**
- No ablation of curriculum formulation vs. standard full-data RL training. The curriculum is described as a key contribution (Sec 3.2 header: "Adaptive Curriculum Formulation," listed as a primary contribution in the intro). However, no ablation table isolates its contribution. An RL run without curriculum filtering is never reported.
- No ablation of static-RAG verifier (pre-fetched fixed context) vs. iterative dynamic retrieval — the core claimed novelty (dynamic retrieval) is not isolated from having retrieval at all.
- Group size G is set to 8 in Iter-2 and 5 in Iter-1 (Table 4), changing a hyperparameter between iterations — making Iter-1 vs. Iter-2 comparison confounded with group size (hyperparameter) change.

**Unrefuted alternative-mechanism explanations:**
- The curriculum filtering selects "boundary cases" where the model is uncertain. These may also be the most training-efficient examples under any RL paradigm (not specific to Med-TIV's design). The performance gain from curriculum could be replicated by any difficulty-aware sampling strategy — not unique to Med-TIV.
- The RL training itself (Dr.GRPO on trace-level binary rewards) may capture the majority of benefit, as Table 3 shows: RL alone gives +8.64 pp while tools add only +0.94 pp. The paper's central narrative ("grounding verification in dynamically retrieved evidence") is the lower-contribution component.

## Soundness Score Rationale

**Primary scoring consideration — cost/efficiency claim without proper measurement (Score-2 trigger):**

The paper's abstract and introduction foreground the 8× sampling efficiency gain as a central contribution: "an 8× gain in sampling efficiency compared to prior reward-based approaches, achieving equivalent accuracy with substantially fewer sampled reasoning traces during test-time search" (abstract, lines 35–37). The operational claim is that inference cost approximately linearly with the number of sampled generator traces (lines 375–376), making Med-TIV at N=4 materially cheaper than Med-PRM at N=32. However:

1. Med-TIV's verifier is a multi-turn agent that iteratively queries a search engine, formulates search queries, retrieves documents, and reasons over them — potentially 3–10 retrieval steps per trace verification (the paper gives no distribution of retrieval steps or total token counts in main text or appendix).
2. No wall-clock timing, FLOPs, token counts per verification call, or memory measurements appear anywhere in the 14-page paper (including Appendix A-D). The inference-cost claim is an oracle count over sampled generator traces only.
3. The Limitation section (Appendix A) discusses corpus coverage and language scope but does not acknowledge the wall-clock measurement gap.

This matches the "Cost / wall-clock claim without measurement" Score-2 trigger: the paper's sampling-efficiency contribution rests on a computational cost comparison that measures only generator sampling count while ignoring the verifier's own inference overhead.

**Secondary consideration — framing-vs-denominator mismatch on the 23.5% headline:**

The 23.5% MedQA improvement is computed over the single-sample base generator (60.96%), not over competitive aggregation-matched baselines. When compared against standard Self-Consistency at N=32 (66.38%, Table 2), the relative improvement is ~13.4%. The abstract frames this as "improving MedQA accuracy by 23.5%... relative to the base generator model alone," which is technically accurate but selects the most favorable denominator. This is a framing-vs-demonstration concern (though not as sharp as the wall-clock gap).

**Offsetting strength:**
- The core MCQ accuracy improvements are real and well-supported across 4 benchmarks and 2 base models (Table 1, Table 2).
- Table 3 ablation (RL vs. RL+Tool) provides some mechanism attribution, though the curriculum ablation is missing.
- The curriculum formulation is conceptually sound.
- Multiple baselines are compared fairly (same retrieval corpus for Med-PRM, per Appendix B.3).

**Score decision:** Score 2. The "8× sampling efficiency" claim appears in the abstract, introduction (twice), and Section 4.3 as a headline contribution. The paper explicitly states this translates to "equivalent performance at one-eighth the generator inference cost in practical deployment settings" (lines 375–378). This is a central empirical contribution claim, and zero wall-clock or total compute measurements are reported. The Score-2 trigger "Cost / wall-clock claim without measurement" fires on this load-bearing claim.
