**DES achieves 38% MoE kernel latency reduction, but end-to-end GPU speedup is 8–14%, and the multi-GPU/CPU-offloading use cases that motivate the paper are not experimentally evaluated.**

The paper frames Dynamic Expert Sharing as a solution for the memory-bound regime "typical of multi-GPU and CPU-offloading environments" (Sec. 1). The abstract reports "latency by up to 38%." Three points deserve examination:

- **Latency scope mismatch.** Sec. 5.3 discloses that the 38% figure is MoE *kernel* latency on a single B200 GPU; total end-to-end GPU kernel time improves by only 8.2–14.3%. The abstract's unqualified "latency" implies end-to-end, which is 3–5× smaller than the headline number.
- **Evaluation gap vs. stated motivation.** All efficiency experiments (Figure 5, Table 1) use a single NVIDIA B200 GPU. The paper's core motivation — that DES benefits multi-GPU and CPU-offloading deployments where HBM-to-SRAM traffic is the binding constraint — is never empirically evaluated. In multi-GPU settings, expert weights may be distributed across devices, changing both the bottleneck structure and the DES benefit profile.
- **B200 specificity.** The B200 has substantially higher memory bandwidth than H100 or A100 GPUs. Whether the 38% MoE kernel speedup transfers to prior-generation hardware (where DES's motivation is arguably stronger) is not reported.

The paper's technical methodology is sound within its single-GPU scope: the ablation studies (coreset size, block lengths, active expert count in Figures 7–8) are clean, the custom kernel speedup (6×, Figure 6) is properly profiled with Nsight Systems, and the accuracy-vs-experts Pareto frontier (Figure 1c) is well-characterized. However, the gap between the stated deployment context and the actual evaluation scope is worth clarifying.
