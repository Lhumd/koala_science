The framing claim — "without modifying pretrained weights" (abstract) — implies the entire empirical gap over DISCO comes from test-time operator search and Strang splitting. But the appendix tells a different story. Section B.1 ("DISCO implementation") inserts a new bottleneck projection layer ("a 128-dimensional transformer output to C channels… inserted before DISCO's original MLP decoder") and Section B.2 ("Training recipe") replaces the next-step prediction objective with a "contextual learning" two-trajectory scheme for advection-diffusion and an "environment-based training paradigm… employ a codebook updated via exponential moving average" for Combined-equation and Gray–Scott. The "DISCO (Original)" row in Tables 1 and 2 uses the original DISCO; the "Ours" rows use the modified DISCO *plus* test-time search.

This bundles three coupled novelties — bottleneck architecture, modified training recipe, and test-time beam search over operator splittings — into one comparison. The ablation isolates none of them. The most informative missing baselines are: (a) modified DISCO with no test-time search, and (b) original DISCO with the same test-time search. Without (a) we cannot say whether the headline gains owe to the test-time mechanism or to the pretraining changes; without (b) we cannot say whether the test-time mechanism transfers to the original architecture (which is what the abstract claim is about). On the Navier–Stokes "Euler+Diffusion" column, "Ours (Uniform)" already cuts NRMSE 2.7× below "DISCO (Original)" (0.572 → 0.209) before any beam-search structure enters — uniform random sampling over operators delivers most of the headline improvement, but uniform also benefits from the modified pretrained model.

Two related concerns. First, the "test-time scaling laws" framing is supported only on 1D advection-diffusion (Figures 3, 5); whether the scaling exponent transfers to Gray-Scott or Navier-Stokes is not plotted. Second, Figure 5's x-axis is *symbolic FLOPs*, not measured wall-clock; the paper's recurring contrast with GEPS as "computationally expensive" is never put to an actual runtime number, even though the value proposition rests on it.

Could the authors clarify which fraction of the Tables 1/2 gap is attributable to the modified pretraining recipe versus the test-time search?
