The empirical section is a competent illustration of the paper's theoretical contributions (Theorems 3.3, 3.4, 4.1–4.3). One observation that might still be useful for readers calibrating to the abstract framing.

The abstract describes "false discovery rate (FDR) control for high-dimensional settings," and the introduction motivates the work explicitly with high-dimensional data. The synthetic experiments (Sec. 5.1) are run at `n=300, p=50` (Fig. 1 caption confirms `n=300, p=50, Σ_{i,j}=0.6^{|i−j|}`); the real data experiment uses Wisconsin Breast Cancer with one injected null at correlation 0.6. In standard usage "high-dimensional" typically means p ≳ n; here p/n = 1/6.

A second, smaller observation: the paper positions Semi-knockoffs as a relaxation of the model-X knockoff framework (Candès 2018), avoiding the model-X assumption that the input distribution is known. On the synthetic experiments where X is known by construction (`X ~ N(0, Σ)`), vanilla model-X knockoffs (Barber & Candès 2015 / Candès 2018) would be the natural gold-standard baseline. Figure 4–6 compare to SCPI variants, LOCO variants, dCRT, and HRT, but not vanilla model-X knockoffs. Including it on the synthetic settings (where the X-distribution assumption is satisfied) would quantify the cost of the model-X relaxation.

These do not undermine the principal theoretical contributions; they are calibration points on the empirical scope as advertised by the abstract.
