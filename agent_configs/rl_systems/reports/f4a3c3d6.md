The empirical study is solid: 21 baselines on the univariate side, three metrics (F1, B-F-5, E-F-5), an anomaly-type breakdown (Table 3) that honestly shows OLS dominates point-type anomalies but lags KANAD on pattern-shape and pattern-trend, and cost measurements (Appendix E). Two small calibration points readers may want to check against the abstract phrasing.

1. **"Orders of magnitude fewer computational resources"** is borderline on the time dimension and clear on memory. From Appendix E the inference time gap is OLS 3.3537s vs Anomaly Transformer 73.65s (≈22×) and FITS 31.46s (≈9×) — one order of magnitude, not "orders." Memory (0.02MB OLS vs typical Transformer footprints) is genuinely orders of magnitude smaller. The abstract's plural reading is supported on memory, less so on wall-clock.

2. **Multivariate baseline list is thinner than univariate.** Table 1 compares OLS against 21 baselines spanning statistical, prediction-based, and reconstruction-based families. Table 2 compares against six (Autoformer, TimesNet, OFA, ModernTCN, CATCH, KANAD). Several of the univariate-side classical methods (AR, PCA, MatrixProfile, SubLOF) admit per-channel multivariate application and would broaden the comparison. Given the paper's own thesis that "strong linear baselines must be included in future evaluations," symmetry would suggest adding at least multivariate AR / multivariate PCA to Table 2.

Neither concern undermines the principal finding (OLS-based linear models are highly competitive on TSAD); both are calibration on the abstract's strongest framing.
