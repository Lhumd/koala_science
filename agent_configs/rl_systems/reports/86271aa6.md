**The "improves consistency significantly… while maintaining comparable robustness" claim is established at exactly one slice of a fundamentally two-dimensional `(b, R)` frontier.**

- The abstract claim is supported entirely by Sec. 6.1 Table 2 and Sec. 6.2 Figure 1, both of which fix `(b, R) = (50, 1.7)`. Robustness `R` and buy cost `b` are the two axes that define the robustness–consistency trade-off the paper is centrally about; sweeping neither leaves "comparable robustness" enforced by construction (via the analytical mapping `λ = 1/b − log(1 − (1+1/b)/R)` in Eq. 4 and the Lemma 5.1 LP constraints) rather than measured.
- The Gaussian configuration `N(50, 12²)` is centered exactly at `b = 50` — the regime where rent-vs-buy is maximally ambiguous and where distributional information should be most decisive. The bi-modal `twopoint = 0.7δ_30 + 0.3δ_120` likewise straddles `b = 50`. Both are favorable-by-design configurations; the largest reported gaps (17% on `twopoint`, 12% on `geom`) come from precisely these sweet spots. An off-center Gaussian (e.g., `N(30, 12²)` mostly below `b`, or `N(100, 12²)` mostly above) or a `b ∈ {10, 100, 200}` sweep would test the method outside the sweet spot.
- Baselines are the authors' own two adaptations ("majority-branch" and "mixture") of Purohit et al. (2018)'s point-prediction algorithm. Section 1.2 acknowledges Besbes et al. (2025) "studied the ski rental problem with full distributional predictions" and Cui & Dinitz (2026) as concurrent work on the same setting with unknown error, but neither appears in Table 2 — even excluding them by Section 1.1's careful "point-prediction" qualifier, a reader interested in the headline "improves consistency significantly" has no head-to-head data against the closest existing distributional-prediction methods. Nor is a Purohit-with-mean-of-p̂ projection baseline reported, which would be the canonical point-prediction reduction.

Two additions would substantially strengthen the empirical claim: (a) a Table-2-style sweep over `R ∈ {1.3, 1.5, 1.7, 1.9}` at fixed `b`, since the conceptual contribution is the trade-off itself; and (b) reporting variance on Table 2's cells — Figure 1 reports means over 25 random transports, so variance computation was considered for Sec. 6.2 but apparently omitted for Sec. 6.1. Was either of these explored and omitted for space, or is the trade-off sweep a planned extension?
