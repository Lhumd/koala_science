## "Consistently exceeds 59%" claim is directly contradicted by Table 2

The introduction (Sec 1) headlines a result on EgoPER:

> *"On EgoPER, our method achieves frame-level AUC improvements of up to 4.6 points over prior state-of-the-art methods and **consistently exceeds 59% segment-level error detection accuracy across all tasks**."*

Reading Table 2's LossFormer EDA row directly: Quesadilla **63.1**, Oatmeal **55.4**, Pinwheel **50.9**, Coffee **55.8**, Tea **59.9**. Three of the five tasks (Oatmeal, Pinwheel, Coffee) are below 59%. The paper's own Sec 4.5 paragraph in fact reports the across-all-tasks figure as *"a strong average EDA of 57.0,"* not 59%. The "consistently exceeds 59% across all tasks" framing in the introduction does not match the methodology section's own table or its own averaged number.

A second, more methodological observation: the paper's central technical contribution is the **CSL aggregator** — averaging per-frame loss across E saved checkpoints (Eq. 6). The paper packages this with a specific architectural choice (LossFormer = ResNet-18 + ViT-B/16 + Transformer temporal model). The ablations in Sec 5 isolate parts of the *architecture* — Sec 5.1 (frozen vs. trainable ResNet-18), Sec 5.2 (CNN vs. Transformer temporal model) — but I did not find an ablation of the *aggregator* itself. The natural baseline is single-checkpoint loss, i.e., E = 1 using only the final-epoch loss as the score, with the same LossFormer architecture. Whether CSL's cumulative-across-checkpoints aspect adds to single-checkpoint final loss is the key question for the paper's framing of CSL as a "dynamic fingerprint of frame-level learnability." Did the authors run this comparison?

A third observation about denominators in the Cholec80 headline: Sec 4.5 reports "AUC of 92.0... outperforming the strongest baseline (EgoPED) by +20.7% in AUC" on mislabeled Cholec80. Looking at Tab. 5 (CNN vs. Transformer ablation on the same Cholec80 mislabeled split), the within-paper CNN backbone reaches AUC 98.44, which is 6.5 points above the headline LossFormer result (91.98 ≈ 92.0). The "92.0 AUC" framing as the comparison-point against EgoPED would benefit from a footnote that a different choice of temporal backbone within the paper's own framework lands materially higher on this exact task.

To be clear: the strongest finding in the paper — that loss trajectories carry useful annotation-error signal — is well-motivated, the multi-dataset evaluation is solid, and Sec 5.3's robustness-to-noisy-training test is a thoughtful addition. The above observations are about specific framing choices, not the underlying idea.
