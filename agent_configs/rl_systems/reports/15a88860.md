The paper attributes its accuracy gains to *"An Iterative HyperNode Retrieval Strategy for Reasoning Path Expansion"* (Sec 1, contributions). Two ablations make it hard to reconcile this attribution with the evidence.

**Table 2 (2Wiki)** shows that switching from M=0 (pure DPR baseline at L=5) to M=4 (HELP's full pipeline) raises F1 from 61.55 to 73.90 — a 12.35-point swing that the abstract credits to HyperNode Expansion. **Figure 3 (HotpotQA)**, however, varies the *expansion depth* N ∈ {1,2,3,4} while holding the rest of the pipeline fixed: F1 = 74.5 / 75.65 / 76.18 / 75.0%. At N=1 there is no iterative chaining at all — only the seed HyperNodes (top-n triples by embedding similarity, Sec 3.2.2) anchored through Φ. The iterative-chaining mechanism that the title and contribution #1 foreground appears to add at most ~1.6 F1 points over the no-chaining version.

The remaining bulk of the M=0 → M=4 swing seems to trace to the structural scaffold present even at N=1 — the precomputed Triple-to-Passage Index Φ (Eq. 1) plus the consensus passage scoring of Eq. 4. Two clarifying ablations would help disentangle the credit:

1. A baseline that disables HyperNodes entirely and instead retrieves passages by directly scoring the query embedding against the triple embeddings indexed in Φ ("DPR over triples → Φ → passages", no chaining, no beam search). This isolates whether HyperNodes' value is the chaining itself or just access to the precomputed graph-text mapping.
2. The N-sweep of Figure 3 reported on the same dataset (2Wiki) used in Table 2, so M and N effects can be directly compared on a single benchmark.

A related question on the efficiency side: the "up to 28.8× speedup" headline is query-time retrieval only (Figure 2). Was the offline cost of building Φ — OpenIE on every passage plus per-triple density weight w_τ — measured? On large corpora this could shift the accuracy/efficiency frontier the paper claims.
