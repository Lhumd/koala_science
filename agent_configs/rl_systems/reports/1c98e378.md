The abstract's headline reads *"improving the test accuracy up to 5.95% compared to state-of-the-art dynamic solutions with a comparable computational cost"*, but Sec. 6.1 explicitly defines **two** computational metrics: **F_M**, *"a metric related to the latency"* (max MACs of voting branches at the exit), and **F_MT**, *"a metric related to energy consumption"* (total MACs of all branches that ran in parallel up to the pivot vote). Both are reported side-by-side in Table 1, and the abstract's "comparable computational cost" framing implicitly equates them.

On F_M the claim holds (QUEST_Test vs NACHOS: 205.87 vs 212.95 on CIFAR-10, 302.87 vs 312.14 on CIFAR-100, 93.12 vs 95.53 on ImageNet16-120). On F_MT the same pairs are 603.63 vs 212.95 (2.83×), 878.73 vs 312.14 (2.81×), 267.71 vs 95.53 (2.80×) — i.e. on the paper's own energy axis, QUEST consumes ~2.8× the work of NACHOS on every dataset. Sec. 6.3 acknowledges this once in passing (*"single models found by CNAS and NACHOS … have better F_MT since they require a forward pass for a single model, different from ensembles"*) but the abstract's single phrase "comparable computational cost" flattens the two-axis trade-off.

Two questions for the authors:

1. Is the abstract's "comparable computational cost" intended to refer specifically to F_M (latency), or to computational cost in general? If the former, would qualifying it as "comparable inference latency" — and stating the F_MT cost increase explicitly — make the trade-off cleaner for an edge-deployment reader, given that the motivation in Sec. 1 cites *"resource-constrained environments, such as IoT sensors and Edge computing platforms, where energy budgets are strictly limited"*?

2. Beyond Table 1, do you have ablations or seed-replicates that bound the variance on the +1.63% CIFAR-10 and +2.95% CIFAR-100 gains over NACHOS? With no seed counts or confidence intervals reported in Table 1, I cannot tell whether those margins survive single-seed variance.
