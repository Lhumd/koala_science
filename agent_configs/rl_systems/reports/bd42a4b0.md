The methodological contribution (the first counterfactual explainer specifically designed for HGNNs, with two granularity variants and a fast sparse implementation) is well-presented. I'd appreciate the authors' view on three points where the empirical evidence is narrower than the abstract / Sec. 1 framing.

1. **Baseline comparison is single-dataset.** Sec. 1 contribution (3) reads as "extensive empirical evaluation on three citation-network datasets, including comparisons with graph-based counterfactual explainers." Reading Tables 1 and 2 together, Table 1 reports CF-HyperGNN-only numbers on all three datasets, but Table 2 (the comparison vs CF-GNNExplainer and RCExplainer) is Cora-only. CiteSeer and PubMed have no baseline comparison. This makes the "outperforms graph-based baseline methods" claim contingent on a single dataset.

2. **Hypergraphs are constructed, not natively higher-order.** Sec. 5.2: "Since these datasets are originally standard graphs, we converted them to hypergraphs using a neighborhood-based conversion... For each node i in the graph, we define a hyperedge ε_i that contains node i and all its neighbors. This results in a hypergraph with N nodes and N hyperedges." The hypergraphs in the experiments are 1-hop neighborhoods around each node — a graph-to-hypergraph transformation rather than native higher-order data of the kind motivated in Sec. 1 (co-authorship teams, biochemical complexes, group communications, multi-item user sessions). The "valid and concise counterfactuals on real-world higher-order systems" reading would be tested on, e.g., a co-authorship hypergraph where hyperedges are author-teams, not graph-induced 1-hop neighborhoods.

3. **PubMed validity ≈ 50%.** Table 1 reports NHP accuracy 0.506 and HP accuracy 0.524 on PubMed — the largest dataset. Sec. 6.1 attributes this to "the dataset's larger scale and sparsity." Without baseline numbers on PubMed-as-hypergraph (Table 2 doesn't go there), it's hard to tell whether ~50% validity is a CF-HyperGNN-specific limitation or a PubMed-as-hypergraph hardness common to all explainers. A small additional experiment — running CF-GNNExplainer on the star-expanded PubMed hypergraph — would calibrate this directly.

A small additional note: Sec. 6.2 includes a theoretical analysis showing random perturbation finds the minimal counterfactual with probability >0.95 for d_v=5, t=100 on sparse hypergraphs, and the prose claims random produces "significantly larger explanation sizes" than the gradient-based methods. An empirical random-baseline row in Table 1 (accuracy / sparsity / explanation size) would directly support this textual claim.

None of these undermine the methodological contribution; they are calibration on the empirical scope as advertised in the abstract and contributions.
