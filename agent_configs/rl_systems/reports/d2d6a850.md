## On the empirical scope of the "rapid plateau" claim

The abstract states that the privacy guarantees "converge rapidly to their $n_{\text{syn}} \to +\infty$ limits, showing that focusing on this asymptotic regime captures the key amplification effect with little loss for practical amounts of synthetic data." This claim is supported by Figure 2, which sweeps $n_{\text{syn}} \in [1, 50]$ for $d \in \{1, 5, 10\}$, $k = \Delta = 1$, $C = \alpha = 2$. Two observations on the operational evidence backing this empirical claim:

**1) Estimator validity is asserted but not validated.** Appendix G specifies the divergence is estimated via Birrell et al. (2023)'s Convex-Conjugate Renyi Variational Formula with a 2-layer MLP (hidden dim 64, poly-softplus, Adam, $2 \cdot 10^4$ steps, batch 512, divergence evaluated on $5 \cdot 10^4$ samples, averaged over 10 runs). This estimator is a supremum over a parameterized function class, hence a *lower* bound on the true Renyi divergence. The paper does not report a sanity check against a tractable case where $D_\alpha$ is known in closed form (e.g., the scalar Gaussian limit), so it is hard to disentangle "the true divergence plateaus" from "the variational sup-estimator saturates due to MLP capacity." Has this calibration been done in any regime where ground truth is available?

**2) No quantitative spread on Figure 2.** Each point is averaged over 10 estimation runs (Appendix G), but the figure shows only mean curves — no shaded confidence band, no error bars, no reported standard deviation. Since "rapid" convergence is read off the visual curve shape, the noise level on the estimator matters for interpreting how rapid is rapid.

**3) Scope of "practical amounts."** The demonstrated regime ($n_{\text{syn}} \leq 50$, $d \leq 10$, $k = 1$) sits well below standard DP synthetic data release scales, where $n_{\text{syn}}$ is in the thousands-to-millions and $d$ is in the hundreds-to-thousands. The theoretical bound is $n_{\text{syn}}$-independent, so this is consistent — but the abstract's "practical amounts" framing reads as an empirical generalization that the figure does not directly demonstrate. Was a larger-$d$ or larger-$k$ sweep attempted (Theorem 5.4's $k > 1$ regime is not numerically illustrated)?
