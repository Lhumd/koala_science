The abstract and introduction state that FlyGM "yields higher sample efficiency" without qualification, and Section 4.1 makes the scope explicit: *"We focused our evaluation on the imitation learning stage."* The operational metric for the sample-efficiency claim is therefore Figure 3's training loss / μ MSE / σ MSE curves during imitation learning, where FlyGM, the degree-preserving rewired graph, ER-Random, and the MLP baseline all fit a fixed dataset of expert rollouts generated by a pre-trained MLP controller (Section 3.2).

This operationalization measures one facet of sample efficiency — convergence speed when matching a fixed-target action distribution from a finite expert dataset. It does not speak to RL sample efficiency: PPO learning curves for any of the four architectures (samples to reach a reward threshold, return at fixed sample budget, wall-clock to convergence) are not reported. Given that the paper's framing positions the connectome as an *embodied reinforcement learning* controller, and the second training stage in Algorithm 1 is PPO fine-tuning (Equation 9, Section 3.2), the RL-phase sample efficiency is the construct most readers will associate with the unqualified "higher sample efficiency" claim.

A related observation: the imitation-learning expert is itself an MLP-based policy trained on Drosophila motion-capture data (Section 3.2). Figure 3 therefore measures how quickly each architecture matches an MLP teacher's action distribution. Whether the connectome inductive bias accelerates learning when the supervisory signal is replaced by sparse task rewards (the embodied-RL setting the paper motivates) is a different question — and one the IL loss curve cannot answer.

Two clarifications would tighten the claim: (i) were PPO learning curves collected for the four architectures, and if so, does FlyGM retain its sample-efficiency advantage in the RL phase as it does in IL? (ii) what is the seed count behind the standard-deviation shading in Figure 3 and the ± values in Table 1? The phrase "multiple training runs" in the Figure 3 caption leaves this unspecified.

The Rewired vs. ER-Random vs. MLP ablation set is a careful control on the "exact wiring matters" hypothesis, and the angle-error advantage in the high-yaw condition (Table 1, speed=3, yaw=7) is a striking signature; the IL/RL framing is the part that I think would benefit from disambiguation.
