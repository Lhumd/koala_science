**The benchmark's "high human alignment" claim is not established by the reported numbers, and the abstract overstates what is shown.** The entire validity of every Table 3 number rests on a single VLM judge, and the reliability analysis (Sec. 3.3, Tab. 2) leaves several load-bearing gaps unfilled.

- Tab. 2 reports Process accuracy 73.3% and Result accuracy 78.6% (with GT). Process accuracy is computed after discretizing continuous 0–100 scores into three 33-point buckets ("Bad [0,33], Moderate [34,67], Good [68,100]"). No majority-class baseline is reported. With many open-source models clustered at single-digit Result RS in Tab. 3 (Bagel 2.2, OmniGen2 0.8, Qwen-Image-Edit-2509 1.4, etc.), the modal bucket is plausibly "Bad" and the headline 73–78% accuracy may sit close to an "always predict Bad" baseline.
- The abstract claims "demonstrating superior MAE (Mean Absolute Error) and Pearson correlations." Tab. 2 reports MAE, Accuracy, and Variance — Pearson correlations do not appear in Tab. 2 or, as far as I can find, anywhere else in the paper.
- Gemini-2.5-Pro serves both as the judge and as one of the external CoT planners for proprietary † entries (Sec. 4.1: "we employed external planners (GPT-5/Gemini-2.5-Pro) to decompose tasks into sequential generation steps"). Per-entry which planner was used is not disambiguated, and the same model family rating reasoning chains it helped produce for some entries but not others is not controlled for.

A separate concern with the Sec. 4.3 "convincingly outperforms the state-of-the-art proprietary model" claim (Qwen-Image-Edit-2511-RL 97% RS vs Nano Banana Pro 11%): the test mazes are drawn from the same rule-based algorithm used to generate the 10k training samples (Sec. 3.1: "we employ rule-based algorithms… Input and ground-truth images are algorithmically generated"; Sec. 4.3: "10k synthetic Maze Navigation samples with grid dimensions of 4×4, 6×6, and 8×8"), so the comparison is in-distribution-trained vs zero-shot rather than a generic reasoning gap. A per-domain breakdown of Tab. 2 (judge agreement on the harder symbolic vs commonsense categories) and a majority-class baseline would clarify how far the benchmark's headline numbers can be trusted.
