**AMD's headline claim — "improves the HPSv2 score on SDXL from 30.64 to 31.25" (Abstract) — uses the same metric as its training reward signal, creating a circular evaluation loop for the paper's primary result.**

Section 4.1 states: "Regarding the reward models, we utilize HPSv2 (Wu et al., 2023) for SDXL." The reward-aware advantage ā_i (Eq. 12) is computed directly from HPSv2 scores, and the adaptive coefficients α and β (Eq. 13) steer training to prefer high-HPSv2 samples and escape low-HPSv2 regions. The headline result then evaluates improvement on HPSv2 (Table 3: AMD 31.25 vs. DMD2 30.64). This measures the training objective, not an independent quality dimension.

Three specific observations:

- **Self-referential headline metric.** The abstract leads with HPSv2 improvement. But AMD explicitly shapes the SDXL training landscape to prefer high-HPSv2 regions (Eq. 12–13). Demonstrating higher HPSv2 after training toward HPSv2 does not provide evidence that AMD improves perceptual quality in general; it shows the reward optimization is working. The paper correctly flags DMDR for "reward hacking behavior" on IS (Sec 4.2, Table 4) but applies no analogous scrutiny to its own HPSv2 headline.

- **Component ablation is on a different backbone.** Figure 5 shows the contribution of Dynamic Score Adaptation and Repulsive Landscape Sharpening, but only on SiT-XL/2 (class-conditional ImageNet, DiT architecture). The headline SDXL results (Table 3) show only the full AMD system; no component breakdown exists for the text-to-image U-Net setting. The ablation result on a different architecture and task may not transfer.

- **Non-circular evidence does support the method.** GenEval (0.57 vs. DMD2's 0.51, Table 2), FID on ImageNet (3.4690 vs. DMD's 3.5573, Table 4), and VBench Total Score (197.45 vs. LongLive's 173.59, Table 6) are independent metrics. Does the HPSv2 improvement on SDXL reflect the same quality gains seen in GenEval and FID, or is it partially explained by the reward signal alignment?
