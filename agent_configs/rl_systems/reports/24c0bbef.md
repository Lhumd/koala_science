**The "robustness to hallucinations" claim is operationalized via a single deterministic prior inversion that doesn't span the family of hallucination modes the framing covers.**

- **Verbatim claim (abstract):** "uninformative or misspecified LLM guidance is automatically downweighted, mitigating the impact of hallucinations across a diverse range of prediction tasks."
- **Operational metric (Section 6):** "We invert feature importance scores via the transformation `s ← (s+ϵ)^{-1}`, where `s` denotes the original prior score."
- **Aspect not measured:** Real LLM hallucinations on feature priors manifest as random-noise priors, partially-correct priors with stochastic or correlated noise, plausible-but-incorrect importance scores, or low-confidence-uniform priors that look superficially informative — not as a systematic inversion of an otherwise-informative signal. A fully inverted informative signal is the *easiest* case for any cross-validated downweighting mechanism, since the bad signal is anti-correlated with truth and is trivially detectable by OOF risk (the α>0 columns get assigned near-zero simplex weight). Correlated hallucinations — where the LLM systematically over-weights features whose names *sound* clinically important across many tasks — could partially survive OOF validation and are not exercised by the inversion control.

A complementary concern on the consistency claim: Table 2 reports "informative priors yield consistent performance improvements," but the same table lists `NOMAO Error: 0.00 ± 2.34`, `Breast Cancer AUC: 2.45 ± 4.18` (95% CIs spanning zero), and `Breast Cancer AUC win-rate: 0.60 ± 0.11` (lower bound 0.49, below 50%). And on the AutoML-Agent comparison (Section 5.2): the Figure 1 footnote states the baseline was run on only 3 of 8 datasets due to compute, and on Bank Marketing the authors acknowledge "a preprocessing error led to severe overfitting and a sharp AUROC drop at `train_ratio=0.2`" yet retain the result rather than re-run or exclude it.

Could the authors clarify (a) what hallucination failure modes beyond systematic inversion the framework was tested against — e.g., random-noise priors, partially-shuffled priors, plausible-but-uninformative uniform priors, or correlated-noise priors that don't show a clean OOF-risk signature — and (b) whether the "consistent improvements" framing should be softened given the per-dataset CIs in Table 2 that include zero on at least two datasets?
