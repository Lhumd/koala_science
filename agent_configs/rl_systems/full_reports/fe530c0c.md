## Construct-Validity Audit

### Claim 1 — "75% less compute"

**Construct claimed:** GeCO requires substantially less computation (75% less) while achieving equal or better task success.

**Operational metric:** Number of Function Evaluations (NFE) = number of forward passes through f_θ before convergence or budget exhaustion. The ratio is 5 (GeCO) vs. 20 (Rectified Flow), hence 75%.

**Unmeasured aspects:**

1. **Wall-clock inference time per step is never measured.** Algorithm 1 (Appendix A.3) uses a fixed step-size schedule η₁=0.1, η₂₋₄=0.05, η₅₋₁₆=0.02, η₁₇₋₃₀=0.01 and evaluates convergence via ‖f_θ(a^(k), s)‖₂ < 0.4 after each step. Both GeCO and Rectified Flow perform forward passes through the same DiT-1D backbone with identical architecture (Appendix A.1: "GeCO uses the same backbone modules for fair comparison"). Since both use the same network for the same type of forward computation, per-step wall-clock cost is plausibly equal. However, the paper never reports latency, FLOPs, or memory at inference for either method. The claim "75% less compute" rests entirely on NFE count without any measured time/FLOPs evidence. I did not find wall-clock measurements in the main text or Appendices A–F.

2. **NFE averaging conflates easy and hard tasks.** Table 1 reports "Efficiency NFE" as 5.0 (GeCO 5-step), 8.7 (10-step), 11.6 (20-step), 12.8 (30-step) — these are averages. The average NFE at 20 steps is 11.6, meaning GeCO at its "20-step max budget" actually uses 11.6 steps on average — less than the maximum but substantially more than 5. The headline comparison "5 GeCO steps vs. 20 Rectified Flow steps" picks GeCO's minimum-budget configuration against Rectified Flow's standard configuration, not the matched-performance configuration. At GeCO (20-step max), the Long subtask improves from 82.4% to 83.8%, meaning the early-exit configuration leaves some performance on the table.

3. **Action temporal consistency unmeasured.** GeCO initializes from Gaussian noise each planning call and optimizes via gradient descent. Rectified Flow integrates a smooth ODE. Whether consecutive action chunks from GeCO are temporally consistent (a standard CoRL/RSS concern for action chunking policies) is never measured. A less coherent action sequence could pass task success metrics while exhibiting jerky robot motion.

### Claim 2 — "Training-free OOD detector"

**Construct claimed:** The field residual norm reliably identifies OOD robot states without additional training, applicable for safe deployment.

**Operational metric:** Score(s_t) = ‖f_θ(â(s_t), s_t)‖₂ after convergence; binary OOD decision via threshold τ_OOD. Evaluated as AUROC and TNR/TPR on LIBERO-Goal (ID) vs. LIBERO-Spatial (OOD).

**Unmeasured aspects:**

1. **The OOD distribution is suite-level layout shift, not semantic OOD.** Per Appendix D: "We study a controlled suite-level distribution shift on LIBERO by treating LIBERO-Goal as in-distribution (ID) and LIBERO-Spatial as out-of-distribution (OOD)." LIBERO-Spatial uses the same workspace, objects, and manipulation vocabulary as LIBERO-Goal — only spatial arrangements differ. The abstract's phrase "significantly increasing for anomalies" implies broader scope than a controlled workspace layout shift. Genuine deployment OOD (novel object categories, lighting extremes, physical perturbations, gripper contact failure) is never tested.

2. **False-positive cost on ID tasks is unmeasured.** Table 5 shows TNR = 82.4% (Moving Average) or 84.0% (Leaky Bucket) at TPR ≈ 90%. This means 15.6–17.6% of in-distribution planning calls are incorrectly flagged as OOD, immediately terminating the episode (Sec. 4.3: "we immediately terminate the episode to avoid executing the remaining interactions"). The paper reports "Time Saved = 42.4–44.3%" exclusively on OOD episodes. The success rate degradation on ID episodes from false terminations is never measured. This omission makes the OOD claim's operational value unassessable for real deployment.

3. **Threshold τ_OOD is not evaluated for generalizability.** The threshold is calibrated to achieve TPR ≈ 90% on the LIBERO-Goal/Spatial split. Whether the same threshold generalizes to different ID/OOD pairs (different robots, different task families) is untested.

### Claim 3 — "Better drop-in replacement for VLA action heads"

**Construct claimed:** GeCO uniformly improves over time-conditional flow matching heads across VLA architectures and benchmarks.

**Operational metric:** Task success rate on VLABench, RoboTwin 2.0, and LIBERO.

**Unmeasured aspects:**

1. **LIBERO π₀ result is below baseline.** Table 4 shows Ours (π₀) = 93.9 vs. π₀ baseline = 94.2 — GeCO underperforms on LIBERO for π₀. The claim "better drop-in replacement" is not uniformly supported. Sec. 4.2 notes "it matches a similar average (93.9 vs 94.2)" but the framing "better drop-in replacement" in the abstract and the Sec. 4.2 summary is not warranted for this data point.

2. **VLA adaptation freezes time conditioning via t=0 fixing.** Appendix C states: "Following the GeCO formulation, we fix the time conditioning to t=0 during fine-tuning to learn a stationary velocity field, using the pre-trained weights, rather than a sequence of time-varying fields." The π₀ backbone was pre-trained with time-conditional dynamics; fine-tuning with t=0 does not retrain the backbone from scratch. Whether the pretrained internal representations (learned under time-varying flow) are compatible with stationary equilibrium field semantics is never ablated. A GeCO model trained from a randomly-initialized backbone (rather than warm-starting from a time-conditional checkpoint) might behave differently.

## Reward/Objective Audit

This is an imitation learning paper with no RL reward. The training objective comparison:

**GeCO Loss (Eq. 3):** L(θ) = E_{(s_t,a)} E_{ε,γ} ‖f_θ(x_γ, s_t) − g*(a, ε, γ)‖²  
where g*(a, ε, γ) = (ε − a)c(γ), c(γ) = piecewise decay (Eq. 8, λ=4, a₀=0.1).  
γ is NOT provided to the model during training.

**Baseline (Rectified Flow) Loss:** Standard flow matching with time-conditioned v_θ(x_γ, γ, s_t), regressing toward constant direction (ε − a). γ IS provided to the model.

**Key objective differences:**
1. GeCO removes γ as input — forces a single stationary field consistent across all noise levels.
2. GeCO applies velocity rescaling c(γ): at γ→1, c(γ)→0, so the target field vanishes at the data manifold, creating a geometric attractor structure.
3. Both use the same DiT-1D backbone, same dataset, same training budgets (30k steps).

**No reward-overlap concern** (imitation learning, no RL reward). The objective differences are clean: the GeCO modification is the removal of γ and addition of c(γ). No multi-reward trade-off issues apply.

## Counter-Hypothesis Attempts

### Claim 1 — "75% less compute" / adaptive efficiency

**CH1a: Time-conditional model with adaptive early stopping achieves equivalent NFE savings.**
The key architectural claim is that the stationary field's geometry is *necessary* for adaptive inference. Algorithm 1's convergence check (‖f_θ(a^(k), s)‖₂ < τ_opt) uses the field norm as the stopping criterion. A time-conditional Rectified Flow model evaluated at a fixed time t=1 (clean action space) could similarly be run iteratively with gradient descent, using the same convergence criterion: stop when the output norm falls below τ. If this hypothetical baseline achieves similar NFE savings at similar success rates, the time-unconditional reformulation is not necessary — the adaptive efficiency gain is from the stopping criterion applied to any trained model, not from the stationary field geometry. The paper does not evaluate this baseline. This counter-hypothesis is **unrefuted** by the paper's evidence.

**CH1b: Performance gains from c(γ) velocity rescaling, not time-unconditional design.**
GeCO introduces both time-unconditioned training AND velocity rescaling c(γ). The c(γ) schedule creates a strong attractor at γ=1 (target field vanishes at data manifold), which directly drives the optimizer toward clean actions. The absence of γ as input may be incidental — c(γ) alone could be responsible for the equilibrium structure. A time-conditional model augmented with the c(γ) target schedule (but with γ still as input) would isolate this. This ablation is missing. Partially refuted by: Table 6 (α ablation) shows performance degrades at α=0.6 and 0.9, suggesting the schedule shape matters, but does not isolate time-conditioning removal from the schedule.

### Claim 2 — OOD detection

**CH2a: High field norm at OOD states reflects dataset memorization, not geometric OOD signal.**
The GeCO field is trained on LIBERO-Goal (s_t, a) pairs. For OOD spatial arrangements (LIBERO-Spatial), the scene visual features are slightly outside the training distribution, so f_θ(·, s_t) may simply have poor regression quality (high loss) rather than a principled geometric OOD signal. Any regressor trained on Goal would exhibit elevated prediction error on Spatial — the paper's baseline (flow-matching training loss at t=1) does achieve AUROC=0.53, worse than GeCO's 0.93, suggesting GeCO's signal is stronger. This partially refutes CH2a — GeCO's field norm is a stronger discriminator than the flow-matching training loss. However, the mechanism (geometric attractor vs. elevated regression loss) is not isolated.

**CH2b: False-positive rate renders detector inoperable for long-horizon tasks.**
At TNR=82.4% (Moving Average method), 17.6% of ID planning calls are flagged OOD and terminate the episode immediately. For a long-horizon task requiring 50+ planning calls (LIBERO-Long), the probability of at least one false termination is ≥1-(0.824)^50 ≈ 1.0 (effectively 100% episode termination). The "Time Saved" metric applies only to OOD episodes — the cost of false terminations on ID episodes is completely absent. **This counter-hypothesis is not addressed by the paper's evidence.**

### Claim 3 — VLA plug-and-play

**CH3a: LIBERO π₀ underperformance reflects backbone incompatibility, not marginal variation.**
GeCO (π₀) = 93.9 vs π₀ baseline = 94.2 on LIBERO (Table 4). The 0.3% gap is small, but the paper frames GeCO as uniformly "better." The π₀ backbone was designed for time-conditional flow; freezing t=0 at fine-tuning forces stationary field learning on representations optimized for temporal dynamics. If the gap reflects systematic backbone misalignment rather than random variation, scaling to more diverse VLA architectures could widen it. This remains **unrefuted** in the paper — no variance/seed count for VLA experiments is reported, making the significance of the gap unassessable.

### Claim 4 — Real-world results

**CH4a: Baseline underperformance reflects insufficient fine-tuning, not GeCO's architectural advantage.**
The baseline G0 Plus achieves 10% (Nut Assembly) and 20% (Tube Arrangement) — strikingly low rates for a pre-trained VLA model on two manipulation tasks. Fine-tuning uses 4 epochs with batch size 32 (Appendix E: "We fine-tune the model on the expert demonstrations for 4 full epochs"), which is a minimal training budget. The paper states "we control the same total gradient steps during training" for both methods, confirming equal training budgets. However, if the baseline is genuinely undertrained (4 epochs may be insufficient for G0 Plus to adapt to the specific tasks), GeCO's adaptive inference may simply be more robust to underfitting — not a general advantage. This is **not addressed** by the paper. Binomial 95% CI for 1/10 = [0.003, 0.45] and for 7/10 = [0.35, 0.93] — the intervals substantially overlap and no confidence intervals are reported.

## Mechanism-Attribution Audit

GeCO introduces four coupled novelties that are never decoupled experimentally:

1. **Time-unconditional training** — γ removed as model input (Sec. 3.2)
2. **Velocity rescaling schedule c(γ)** — piecewise decay that vanishes at γ=1, creating geometric attractors (Eq. 2, 8)
3. **Convergence-based adaptive early stopping** — terminate when ‖f_θ(a^(k), s)‖₂ < τ_opt (Algorithm 1, line 6)
4. **Gradient-descent optimization at inference** — iterative update a^(k+1) = a^(k) − η_k f_θ(a^(k), s) instead of ODE integration (Algorithm 1, lines 4–10)

**What Table 6 ablates:** The truncation parameter α ∈ {0.6, 0.8, 0.9} controls the onset of the c(γ) decay schedule. This is a sensitivity analysis of hyperparameter (2) — it does not isolate any of the four novelties from each other.

**Missing baselines for mechanism isolation:**

| Missing Baseline | Would Isolate |
|---|---|
| Time-conditional Rectified Flow + adaptive early stopping | Whether novelty (1) is necessary for adaptive inference |
| GeCO without c(γ) rescaling (constant velocity target) | Whether novelty (2) is necessary for attractor geometry |
| GeCO with fixed-schedule ODE inference (no early stopping) | Whether novelty (3) drives the efficiency gain |
| Standard flow matching with GD inference (no time-uncond.) | Whether novelty (4) independently improves performance |

The paper's most critical gap is the absence of the first baseline: a standard Rectified Flow model with adaptive early stopping applied at inference. If ‖v_θ(x^(k), γ, s)‖₂ < τ on a time-conditional model, would the same early-exit efficiency materialize? The paper's theoretical argument (Sec. 3.2) that the stationary field is *necessary* for principled convergence is not tested empirically.

Attribution is further complicated by novelty (3) and (4) being jointly sufficient for adaptive efficiency regardless of (1) and (2): any iterative solver that can detect convergence achieves adaptive NFE allocation.

## Multi-Objective Trade-Off Audit

This is an imitation learning paper with no multi-term reward. No multi-objective RL trade-offs apply.

**Implicit trade-offs present but unmeasured:**

1. **Action smoothness vs. efficiency.** GeCO reinitializes from Gaussian noise at each planning call and optimizes via gradient descent. Rectified Flow integrates a smooth ODE. Whether the two methods produce equally temporally coherent action sequences (across consecutive planning calls) is never measured. The paper shows success rate but not action jerk/smoothness, joint velocity profiles, or gripper contact consistency.

2. **OOD false-positive rate vs. Time Saved.** Table 5's "Time Saved" metric is measured only on OOD episodes. The 17.6% false-positive rate means approximately 1 in 6 ID planning calls is incorrectly flagged, causing unnecessary episode termination. The joint metric (ID success with OOD detection active) is never reported — the two quantities are presented in isolation.

3. **Convergence threshold τ_opt sensitivity.** Appendix A.3 sets τ_opt = 0.4 (fixed empirically). The paper notes this threshold "empirically" and shows α sensitivity in Table 6, but does not show τ_opt sensitivity. The threshold controls the trade-off between NFE and action quality — it is a critical hyperparameter for the efficiency claim, and its sensitivity is untested.

## Framing-vs-Demonstration Audit

**Framing 1 (Abstract):** "As a plug-and-play replacement for standard flow-matching heads, GeCO *improves* success rates and efficiency"  
**Demonstration:** Table 4 shows GeCO (π₀) = 93.9 vs. π₀ baseline = 94.2 on LIBERO — GeCO underperforms for π₀. "Improves success rates" is not uniformly demonstrated across all VLA experiments. Improvement is selective (strong on VLABench Track 1, weaker on LIBERO π₀).

**Framing 2 (Sec. 4.1):** "requiring **75% less compute**"  
**Demonstration:** The 75% figure is NFE-based (5 vs. 20 forward passes). The paper provides no wall-clock measurement. Per Appendix A.1, both GeCO and baseline use the same DiT-1D backbone and the same type of forward computation, making per-step costs plausibly comparable — but this is never measured. The "75% less compute" framing in the context of inference efficiency implies actual compute savings; the paper delivers NFE savings, which is a model-evaluation count, not a measured compute quantity.

**Framing 3 (Abstract, Sec. 4.3):** "training-free OOD detector … safe deployment … intrinsic safety signal"  
**Demonstration:** OOD detection is tested only on LIBERO-Goal → LIBERO-Spatial, a controlled suite-level layout shift within the same workspace, object vocabulary, and manipulation family. "Safe deployment" implies robustness to genuine deployment anomalies (novel objects, lighting changes, physical failures). These are never tested. The 17.6% false-positive rate on ID states, which would cause unnecessary episode termination, is not disclosed as a limitation in the main text.

**Framing 4 (Sec. 3.1):** "stopping too early under-integrates the ODE/SDE; letting ∑_k Δγ_k > 1 pushes the system beyond the interval on which the field is defined"  
**Demonstration:** This is presented as a fundamental limitation of time-conditional inference that GeCO solves. However, the paper's comparison baseline uses Rectified Flow with a fixed 20-step schedule — it does not test a time-conditional model with early stopping (which would also avoid the "over-integration" problem by stopping at γ < 1). The presented limitation motivates GeCO but does not empirically establish that it matters in practice, since no early-stopped time-conditional baseline is run.

## All Weaknesses Found

**Severity: Moderate (score-3-tier)**

- **Missing mechanism isolation ablation (primary).** GeCO bundles time-unconditional training, velocity rescaling c(γ), adaptive early stopping, and gradient-descent inference into a single system. Table 6 ablates only the α hyperparameter (c(γ) onset). The critical missing baseline — time-conditional Rectified Flow with adaptive early stopping — is never evaluated. The claim that the stationary field is necessary for adaptive efficiency is asserted but not demonstrated. [Sections 3.2, 4.4, Table 6]

- **"75% less compute" without wall-clock measurement.** The efficiency claim is NFE-based. No inference latency, FLOPs, or memory measurements are reported anywhere in the paper or appendices. NFE is a reasonable proxy when per-step costs are equal (both use the same DiT-1D backbone), but the claim is never verified empirically. [Sec. 4.1, Appendix A.3]

- **OOD evaluation on narrow suite-level shift.** OOD detection is tested only on LIBERO-Goal → LIBERO-Spatial (same objects, same workspace, same manipulation vocabulary, different spatial arrangement). The "safe deployment" and "intrinsic safety signal" framing implies broader scope. Genuine visual/semantic OOD is untested. [Sec. 3.4, 4.3, Table 5]

- **False-positive rate cost unmeasured.** TNR = 82.4% means 17.6% of ID planning calls are incorrectly flagged as OOD, immediately terminating the episode. The joint impact on ID task success with OOD detection active is never reported. "Time Saved" applies only to OOD episodes. [Table 5, Sec. 4.3]

- **Real-world n=10 without confidence intervals.** Table 7 reports binary success outcomes for n=10 trials. Binomial 95% CI for 1/10 = [0.003, 0.45]; for 7/10 = [0.35, 0.93] — intervals overlap substantially. Statistical significance of the real-world gains is unverifiable. [Sec. 4.5, Table 7]

**Severity: Minor**

- **VLA π₀ LIBERO underperformance inconsistent with "better drop-in" framing.** GeCO (π₀) = 93.9 vs. π₀ baseline = 94.2 (Table 4). The abstract's "improves success rates" is not uniformly supported.

- **GeCO (5-step) headline picks minimum-budget config.** The matched-performance comparison (GeCO 20-step max, avg. NFE=11.6 vs. Rectified Flow 20-step fixed) is not highlighted. The headline "5 steps beats 20 steps" selects an asymmetric comparison point.

- **Temporal action coherence not measured.** GeCO reinitializes from noise each planning call. Temporal consistency of action chunks across planning steps is never evaluated — a CoRL/RSS standard concern for action-chunking policies.

- **τ_opt sensitivity not evaluated.** Appendix A.3 sets τ_opt = 0.4 empirically; no sensitivity analysis is provided despite this being a critical parameter for the efficiency claim.

- **Attractor property not directly verified.** The paper claims expert behaviors form stable attractors (Abstract, Sec. 1). Figure 1 illustrates this conceptually but no Lyapunov stability analysis, convergence rate measurement, or attractor basin characterization is provided.

## Defense Paragraph

The paper's strongest evidence: Table 1 uses 50 rollouts per LIBERO suite, providing reasonable statistical power for the simulation results. GeCO at 5 steps achieves 91.9% average success vs. 90.0% for Rectified Flow (20 steps fixed), and at max budget (20 steps, avg. NFE = 11.6) reaches 93.5% — a real performance advantage at substantially lower average NFE. Figure 2 and Figure 6 provide qualitative and quantitative visualization of adaptive NFE allocation across task complexity (free-space transit exits at NFE≈3; contact-rich insertion uses NFE≈10), making the adaptive computation mechanism visually compelling. The OOD detection AUROC = 0.93 (Table 5) substantially outperforms the Diff-DAGger-style baseline (AUROC = 0.53), establishing the field norm as a useful discriminator. The real-world deployment on Galaxea R1 Lite (Table 7) demonstrates the method works on physical hardware with randomized object positions, which is a meaningful validation step even at n=10. An owner challenged on the mechanism-attribution gap can point to the α-ablation (Table 6) showing that the smoothness-convergence trade-off is genuine (α=0.6 and 0.9 both degrade, confirming that field geometry matters) and to Appendix C's documentation of the fine-tuning protocol (30k steps, same training budget for baseline and GeCO) as evidence that the VLA gains are not from training asymmetry.
