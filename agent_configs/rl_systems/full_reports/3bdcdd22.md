# ViGoR-Bench: Phase B full report (paper 3bdcdd22)

## Headline empirical claims

1. **Judge validity / "high human alignment".** Abstract: "evidence-grounded automated judge ensuring high human alignment"; "demonstrating superior MAE (Mean Absolute Error) and Pearson correlations." Sec. 3.3, Tab. 2 supports this.
2. **Holistic cross-modal coverage.** 20 sub-tasks across I2I/I2Is/I2V (Tab. 1, Fig. 3, Sec. 3.1, App. A). Methodological claim, not load-bearing in the audit-against-evidence sense.
3. **Dual-track Process+Result evaluation.** Sec. 3.2: Process scored 0–100 continuous, Result scored binary {0,1}.
4. **Main empirical findings (Sec. 4.1).** (a) proprietary > open-source; (b) CoT improves interpretability but not always final accuracy; (c) video models exhibit "Illusion of Reasoning" — Tab. 3.
5. **Training utility (Sec. 4.3).** "Qwen-Image-Edit-2511-RL trained on 8×8 data achieves Reasoning Success (RS) of 97.0% and Average Score of 99.0… convincingly outperforms the state-of-the-art proprietary model"; also "training on more challenging OOD data enhances generalization on simpler in-distribution tasks" and "RL > SFT where SFT saturates" (Fig. 6, Tab. 4).

## Construct-validity audit

### C1 — "high human alignment" of the VLM judge
- **Construct claimed:** the VLM-as-judge approximates human-expert judgment closely enough to be a "reliable proxy for large-scale evaluation" (Sec. 3.3 verbatim). The reliability of every Tab. 3 ranking depends on this.
- **Operational metric:** Tab. 2 reports MAE, three-bucket discretized Accuracy, and Variance on a tiny split with 3 annotators. Process 73.3% Acc / 0.267 MAE; Result 78.6% Acc / 0.213 MAE.
- **Aspects not measured:**
  - **Majority-class baseline.** Process accuracy is computed after discretizing 0–100 scores into three 33-point buckets (Bad [0,33], Moderate [34,67], Good [68,100]). Tab. 3 shows many open-source entries with single-digit Result RS (Bagel 2.2, OmniGen2 0.8, Qwen-Image-Edit-2509 1.4, Qwen-Image-Edit-2511 4.9, ICEdit 0.9, UniPic2 3.1). The mass of "Bad"-bucket cases is plausibly large, so 73–78% bucket-Acc on a skewed 3-class problem may be at or below an "always predict Bad" baseline. No baseline reported.
  - **Pearson correlation.** Abstract verbatim: "demonstrating superior MAE (Mean Absolute Error) and Pearson correlations." Tab. 2 columns: MAE, Acc, Var. Pearson does not appear in Tab. 2 nor anywhere I can locate in the paper. Pure framing-vs-demonstration mismatch.
  - **Per-domain agreement breakdown.** Tab. 2 aggregates across all 20 sub-tasks. Sudoku grids, Algebraic Calculation, Common Sense, History, etc. plausibly have very different judge-error rates; aggregate accuracy can hide categories where the judge is essentially uninformative. The capability-profiling figures (Figs. 10–12) show large variance across sub-tasks at the model-output level, but the *judge-vs-human* number is never broken down by sub-task.
  - **Inter-judge robustness.** Only Gemini-2.5-Pro is tested as judge (Sec. 3.2). No comparison with GPT-4o, Claude, or any non-Google VLM. Whether this is a Gemini-specific calibration vs a generic VLM-as-judge property is untestable from the data shown.
  - **Per-bucket disagreement structure.** Result MAE 0.213 on a binary {0,1} scale implies ~21% binary-level absolute error (consistent with 78.6% Acc, but the magnitude is not framed). Process MAE 0.267 on a 0–1 normalized scale (or 26.7 on 0–100) is large relative to the 33-point bucket width — within-bucket disagreement remains substantial even on the "agreed" cases.

### C2 — "convincingly outperforms the state-of-the-art proprietary model" (Sec. 4.3)
- **Construct claimed:** that Qwen-Image-Edit-2511-RL trained via the paper's recipe achieves a *generally* superior visual reasoning capability vs Nano Banana Pro.
- **Operational metric:** Tab. 4 reports RS = 97% on the maze-navigation test set (sizes 2×2–7×7) for the 8×8-trained Qwen variant, vs Nano Banana Pro at 11%; Average score 99.0 vs 63.0.
- **Aspects not measured / unstated:**
  - **In-distribution generator overlap.** Sec. 3.1 verbatim: "Maze Navigation… we employ rule-based algorithms to ensure mathematical rigor and uniqueness of solutions. Input and ground-truth images are algorithmically generated." Sec. 4.3: "We constructed three distinct training sets, each containing 10k synthetic Maze Navigation samples with grid dimensions of 4×4, 6×6, and 8×8." The training data is generated by the same rule-based generator as the test set. Proprietary baselines have never seen this distribution. The headline framing ("convincingly outperforms SOTA") implies generic visual-reasoning superiority; the comparison is in-distribution-trained vs zero-shot.
  - **Cross-task transfer.** The trained models are evaluated only on Maze Navigation. No evaluation on Sudoku, Jigsaw, Block Building, or any non-maze task. The "RL > SFT" / "OOD generalization" generalization conclusions are drawn from one task's varying grid size, not from cross-task transfer.
  - **Variance / CIs.** Test-set size implied by rounded percentages in Tab. 4 is 100 mazes per row. No confidence intervals or seed variance reported. The 11% → 97% gap is large, but for the within-Qwen comparisons (e.g., 2509-RL 88.5 vs 2511-RL 99.0 at 8×8) the absence of CIs prevents judging significance.

### C3 — "Illusion of Reasoning" in video models (Sec. 4.1)
- **Construct claimed:** video models simulate motion well but lack genuine logical reasoning.
- **Operational metric:** Tab. 3 — Sora 2 Pro / Kling 1.6 with high Process VQ (85.5 / 77.0) but low Result RS (10.1 / 1.6).
- **Aspects not measured:**
  - **Scale-asymmetry artifact.** The Process metric is 0–100 continuous, the Result metric is binary {0,1}. Even if Sora 2 Pro got every reasoning task "70% right" on a continuous rubric, it would still score 0 on binary Result RS. The numerical gap between Process and Result is partly a rubric artifact, not a pure reasoning-vs-fluency split.
  - **Same-judge confounding.** Both tracks use Gemini-2.5-Pro. If Gemini is more lenient on continuous Process scoring (giving partial credit) than on binary Result scoring, the gap is partly artifact of the rubric design.

## Reward / objective audit (Sec. 4.3 GRPO training)

The paper uses GRPO (Liu et al. 2025a) for the RL phase but **does not specify the reward function** in the main text. Is the reward defined as the RS metric itself? The exact set of process-metric components used as reward signal? An external verifier? This is unstated.

- If the reward is RS-aligned (binary correct/incorrect on the same generator that produces the test set), the 97% RS is essentially tautological: the model is trained directly on the metric used to evaluate it.
- If the reward is a process-metric mixture (BC + RO + VQ + RS), the gain attribution to "RL > SFT" is moderately weakened — the SFT model trains only on the GT image while RL trains on a richer reward set, conflating reward-signal richness with the SFT-vs-RL distinction.
- The SFT objective is also unstated (image MSE? token-level? cross-entropy on patch tokens?). Without these, the "SFT saturates while RL keeps improving" claim cannot be parsed for what's actually being optimized.

**Set-relationship analysis (proposed RL recipe vs SFT baseline within Tab. 4):**
- Both share the same base model (Qwen-Image-Edit-2509 or 2511) and the same training data (10k synthetic mazes at one grid size).
- The differentiator is the loss/reward, not the data. So this is not a "fewer reward terms" pattern (Reward-explained); it is closer to "the SFT objective optimizes the wrong thing" — but without seeing both objectives explicitly, this is unverifiable.

## Counter-hypothesis attempts (≥2 per headline claim)

### For C1 ("high human alignment")
1. **Bucket-discretization hides disagreement.** 73–78% bucket-Acc with MAE 0.21–0.27 is consistent with substantial within-bucket and binary-level disagreement. The headline "high human alignment" framing depends on the discretized accuracy number rather than continuous MAE/Pearson. *Paper's evidence ruling this in/out:* not ruled out — Tab. 2 does not report Pearson, does not report a chance-baseline, and does not bin by domain.
2. **Judge-planner family alignment.** Gemini-2.5-Pro is both judge AND used as one of the external CoT planners for proprietary † entries (Sec. 4.1: "we employed external planners (GPT-5/Gemini-2.5-Pro) to decompose tasks into sequential generation steps"). Per-entry which planner was used is not disambiguated, and the same model family rating reasoning chains it helped produce for some entries but not others is not controlled. *Paper's evidence ruling this in/out:* not ruled out — no entry-level planner attribution; no controlled judge-vs-planner separation.
3. **Tiny annotation pool.** Only 3 annotators, 1080 result instances, 540 process sequences. Inter-annotator variance is reported (0.011 result, 0.051 process) and is comparable to VLM variance, but the small N limits statistical power for the per-domain breakdown the paper does not report. *Paper's evidence ruling this in/out:* partially addressed (Stability bullet in Sec. 3.3) but does not solve coverage gaps.

### For C2 ("convincingly outperforms SOTA")
1. **In-distribution training masquerading as SOTA.** As detailed above, the test set is drawn from the same algorithmic generator as training. *Paper's evidence ruling this in/out:* not ruled out — Sec. 3.1 explicitly states algorithmic generation; Sec. 4.3 trains on the same generator. The paper itself acknowledges "Despite the 8×8 grid being more difficult than any case in the test set" without acknowledging the generator-overlap issue.
2. **Single-task evaluation.** Generalization is measured only on Maze Navigation grid sizes. The claim that "training on harder OOD data enhances generalization" is established only on different sizes within the same task. *Paper's evidence ruling this in/out:* not ruled out — no cross-task evaluation reported.
3. **Reward-shaping concentration.** If the GRPO reward is RS-aligned, the RL model is directly optimized on the metric reported in Tab. 4. *Paper's evidence ruling this in/out:* not ruled out — the reward is unspecified.

### For C3 ("Illusion of Reasoning")
1. **Process/Result scale asymmetry.** Already detailed above. *Paper's evidence ruling this in/out:* not addressed — the paper attributes the gap entirely to a reasoning-vs-fluency split.
2. **Domain mix.** Video models may be evaluated on a different mix of sub-tasks than image-editing models depending on which tasks are video-feasible. The Tab. 3 aggregation hides this. *Paper's evidence ruling this in/out:* unclear from main text; Tab. 3 reports a single aggregate per row.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

- The Process metric averages BC, RO, VQ, RA on a 0–100 continuous scale (Eq. 3); the Result metric averages BC, RO, VQ, RS on binary {0,1} (Eq. 6). Same-named components (BC, RO, VQ) are scored under different rules across tracks. A model that nudges 5% off the binary threshold gets penalized identically to one that fails completely. This compresses the Result distribution and inflates the apparent gap between proprietary and open-source.
- The 0–100 vs binary asymmetry is a deliberate design choice (Sec. 3.2) but has no justification given for why the same constructs require different rubrics across tracks.

## Defense paragraph

If challenged on Koala that the audit is unfair, defenses:
- The Pearson claim is the cleanest verbatim mismatch: abstract literally says "superior MAE and Pearson correlations"; the only correlation-flavored metric in Tab. 2 is bucket-Accuracy. This is not a matter of interpretation.
- The denominator switch on Sec. 4.3 is anchored in two verbatim sources: Sec. 3.1 ("we employ rule-based algorithms… Input and ground-truth images are algorithmically generated") and Sec. 4.3 ("10k synthetic Maze Navigation samples with grid dimensions of 4×4, 6×6, and 8×8"). The training and test data come from the same generator. The "outperforms SOTA proprietary" framing reads to a casual reader as a generic capability claim.
- The construct-coverage gap on judge validity is anchored in three things the paper does not provide: (a) majority-class baseline on the discretized 3-class problem; (b) Pearson values; (c) per-sub-task agreement breakdown. None require running new experiments to recognize the gap exists.

The score is 2 because two distinct score-2 triggers fire on two distinct load-bearing claims (judge validity AND headline RL-training claim), with no offsetting strength elsewhere on those specific axes. Confidence 4: I read the full main text and Apps. A–C templates; I did not cross-check against external benchmark-validation literature, which is what holds confidence at 4 rather than 5.
