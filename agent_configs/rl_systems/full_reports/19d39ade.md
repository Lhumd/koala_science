# Phase B Full Report — Test-time Generalization for Physics through Neural Operator Splitting (19d39ade)

**Paper:** Test-time Generalization for Physics through Neural Operator Splitting
**Soundness score:** 2 / **Confidence:** 4
**Primary trigger:** Coupled-novelty mechanism-attribution failure on the load-bearing "without modifying pretrained weights" / "test-time generalization" claim
**Secondary trigger:** Framing-vs-demonstration mismatch — the methodology section (Sec B.1, B.2) silently re-engineers the DISCO baseline that the comparison rests on

---

## Headline empirical claims

1. **C1 (abstract + intro):** "Our approach achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization results." Supported by Tables 1, 2 and Section 5.
2. **C2 (abstract):** "without modifying pretrained weights" — frames the method as a true test-time-only adaptation.
3. **C3 (intro):** "We propose a novel test-time generalization strategy for evolving PDEs based on neural operator splitting to approximate OOD spatiotemporal dynamics."
4. **C4 (intro):** "We adapt a beam search procedure to combine pretrained operators, balancing accuracy and compute, and provide corresponding test-time scaling laws." — Figures 3, 5 are the support.
5. **C5 (intro):** "We demonstrate state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across diverse nonlinear PDEs on tasks such as parameter extrapolation and operator composition, outperforming adaptive neural operator methods and transformer-based architectures." — Tables 1, 2.
6. **C6 (intro):** "To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to tackle test-time generalization with fixed model weights for predicting PDEs."

## Construct-validity audit

## Counter-hypothesis attempts

1. **CH1 — Gains come from the modified pretraining recipe, not the test-time search.** Section B.1 adds a bottleneck projection; B.2 changes the training procedure (contextual + environment-based + EMA codebook). Table 1's DISCO baseline uses the *original* DISCO; "Ours" uses the *modified* DISCO + test-time search. No ablation isolates these. **Unrefuted.**
2. **CH2 — Gains come from inference-time compute scaling rather than the splitting structure.** A naïve baseline that simply ensembles many forward passes from the (modified) DISCO and picks the best one (without composing them via Strang splitting) is not reported. Beam search over compositions is compared only to uniform composition search — both use the splitting scheme. **Unrefuted.**
3. **CH3 — The "operator composition" benchmarks are constructed in a way that uniquely advantages the additive-decomposition assumption.** Three of four datasets (advection-diffusion, combined equation, Gray-Scott) are *literally additive* in the PDE form (∂_t u = Σ operators). The decomposition assumption matches the data-generating process exactly. The Navier-Stokes case (incompressible vorticity form: ∂_t ω + u·∇ω = ν Δω) also additively reproduces the dynamics. **Unrefuted by any non-additive PDE benchmark.**
4. **CH4 — Cherry-picking on the "5 of 6" claim.** Reading Table 1: on "Nonlinear Adv. + Diffusion" the reported best is Zebra (0.022) vs Ours-Beam (0.056). On "Reaction + Diffusion" Ours-Beam = 0.089 is *tied* with Ours-Uniform = 0.089. So Ours-Beam wins 5/7 columns by bolded entry; the abstract "5 out of 6" is a particular task-counting convention — minor framing issue.

## Mechanism-attribution audit

The paper introduces *three* coupled novelties simultaneously:

1. **(Pretraining-side) Bottleneck projection in DISCO architecture** (Section B.1).
2. **(Pretraining-side) Modified training recipe** — contextual learning + environment-based + EMA codebook (Section B.2).
3. **(Test-time-side) Beam search over operator compositions + Strang splitting** (Sections 4.2, 4.3).

The ablation in Tables 1 and 2 only contrasts the **original** DISCO (no novelties) vs **Ours** (all three novelties). Missing baselines:

- Modified DISCO + no test-time search (would isolate novelties 1+2).
- Modified DISCO + test-time search but without splitting (would isolate novelty 3 from splitting structure).
- Original DISCO + test-time search via splitting (would tell us whether the modified pretraining is actually needed).

Critical observation: on the Navier-Stokes "Euler+Diffusion" column, the gap is DISCO 0.572 → Ours-Uniform 0.209 → Ours-Beam 0.066. The DISCO→Ours-Uniform gap (0.572→0.209) is already 2.7× — uniform random search gives most of the headline improvement. The splitting+beam-search structure is not isolated from the pretraining changes that allow uniform search to be effective at all.

The paper credits the gains to "neural operator splitting" / "test-time generalization," but the empirical attribution to that specific mechanism (vs. the architectural/training changes) is not isolated. This is the canonical coupled-novelty score-2 trigger on the headline claim "test-time generalization with fixed model weights."

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

The paper's headline metric is NRMSE only. Not measured:
- Wall-clock test-time cost per trajectory (despite "test-time scaling laws" framing — Fig 5 reports *FLOPs* counted symbolically, not measured wall-clock; the paper motivates the value proposition via "GEPS… can be computationally expensive" without ever putting a number on the GEPS-vs-Ours wall-clock).
- Memory cost of holding the operator dictionary in memory (256 operators for advection-diffusion, 96 for combined, 17 for Navier-Stokes).
- Variance / stability across multiple test trajectories (Tables show point estimates without error bars or CIs).
- Sample efficiency at the test-time stage (number of OOD-trajectory snapshots needed; L=16 is fixed).

## Framing-vs-demonstration audit

- **Abstract verbatim:** "without modifying pretrained weights" implies test-time-only intervention. **Demonstration:** also includes a re-engineered pretraining recipe with bottleneck + contextual-learning + environment-codebook changes (Sections B.1, B.2). The pretrained model in "Ours" is *not* the same model as "DISCO (Original)" in the comparison table.
- **Abstract:** "scaling laws". **Demonstration:** scaling shown on advection-diffusion only; no scaling exponent reported for Gray-Scott or Navier-Stokes.
- **Intro:** "outperforming adaptive neural operator methods and transformer-based architectures". **Demonstration:** no FNO/DeepONet/U-Net baseline; only adaptive (GEPS) and transformer (MPP, Zebra) baselines.

**Headline-number denominator audit.** "11× on advection speed extrapolation to over 200× on diffusion coefficient tasks" (§5.2): Table 2, advection-diffusion, "D" column. Ours-Beam = 0.002, DISCO = 0.159. 0.159/0.002 = 79.5×. Comparing against MPP (0.409): 204×. The "200×" denominator is MPP, not the closest method-comparator. Closest-method comparator (DISCO original) yields ~80× — still a large number; the framing is a moderate denominator-switch concern but not a smoking gun. The "11×" similarly uses MPP as denominator.

## Weaknesses (severity)

- **[Major]** Coupled-novelty mechanism-attribution failure on the headline "test-time generalization with fixed model weights" claim. Three coupled changes (bottleneck arch, training recipe, test-time search); only joint ablation (original DISCO vs full package) reported. No "modified DISCO + no test-time search" or "original DISCO + test-time search" rows.
- **[Major]** Framing-vs-demonstration mismatch: "without modifying pretrained weights" contradicted by Sec B.1/B.2 architectural and training-recipe changes that materially differ from the original DISCO this paper compares against in Table 1.
- **[Moderate]** "Test-time scaling laws" framing demonstrated only on 1D linear-PDE family (advection-diffusion); never validated on Gray-Scott or Navier-Stokes.
- **[Moderate]** Wall-clock cost claim implicit in "GEPS can be computationally expensive" framing never put to a measured comparison; Fig 5 reports symbolic FLOPs.
- **[Moderate]** No standard fixed-architecture neural-operator baselines (FNO, DeepONet, GNO, U-Net) in Tables 1, 2.
- **[Minor]** Operator-composition benchmark construction (3 of 4 datasets are literally additive PDE form) advantages the splitting assumption.
- **[Minor]** No error bars / CIs on Tables 1, 2 NRMSE entries.

## Defense paragraph

If challenged on the coupled-novelty trigger: Section B.1 verbatim describes "a bottleneck projection layer ... inserted before DISCO's original MLP decoder ... a minimal architectural change that improves generalization." Section B.2 verbatim describes "an alternative training strategy based on contextual learning" and "environment-based training paradigm ... codebook updated via exponential moving average." Tables 1 and 2 contrast "DISCO (Original)" vs "Ours (Uniform)" vs "Ours (Beam)" — the "Ours" rows use the modified DISCO from B.1/B.2. A "DISCO with our modified pretraining recipe + no test-time search" row is absent. The paper's credit for the headline gains goes to "neural operator splitting" / "test-time generalization with fixed model weights," but this attribution is not empirically isolated from the bundled pretraining changes.

If challenged on the "test-time scaling laws" weakness: Fig 5 caption confirms 1D linear-PDE family scope (advection, diffusion, advection+diffusion); the Combined-equation, Gray-Scott, and Navier-Stokes settings — where the splitting assumption is most interesting — receive no scaling-law plot.

## Theoretical context (out of Soundness scope)

The Strang-splitting derivation underlying §4 is mathematically standard; correctness of the second-order error bound is not in dispute under the assumption that constituent operators are sufficiently regular and weakly commuting. Whether that assumption holds for the chosen non-linear PDE benchmarks is a modeling question for Theory's lane.
