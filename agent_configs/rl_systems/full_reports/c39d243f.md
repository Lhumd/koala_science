## Headline empirical claims

1. **"11–52% higher average success rates"** (Abstract, Table 1): VLM-RB achieves higher ASR versus UER and PER, averaged across algorithms and tasks.
2. **"19–45% improvement in sample efficiency"** (Abstract, Table 1): Fewer steps needed to reach the baseline's own best performance.
3. **"~12% wall-clock overhead"** (Sec. 4.3, Table 6): VLM inference adds only modest throughput cost (PER vs VLM-RB: 111 vs 97 steps/s on A100).
4. **"21/24 configurations reduce absolute wall-clock time by up to 58%"** (Sec. 4.3, Table 7): Sample efficiency gains outweigh throughput overhead.
5. **"Semantically grounded prioritization signals closely aligned with task objectives"** (Sec. 4.2, Fig. 3): VLM scores correlate with learned critic value estimates.

## Construct-validity audit

### "Sample efficiency" (Claims 2, 4)
- **Construct claimed**: Ability to learn with fewer environment interactions.
- **Operational metric**: Steps required to reach *the baseline's own best* ASR (Table 1 caption: "Sample Efficiency tracks the steps required to reach the *baseline's* best performance").
- **Aspect not measured**: The denominator is the baseline's peak, not VLM-RB's own peak. When a baseline achieves low absolute ASR (e.g., DQN/UER on DoorKey-16x16: 0.24 ASR), the "efficiency improvement" measures VLM-RB's speed of matching a weak bar. The construct "sample efficiency" ordinarily implies learning more with the same data — this single-axis definition is disclosed and internally consistent, but conflates "matches a low bar faster" with "learns more efficiently."

### "Semantically grounded" mechanism (Claim 5)
- **Construct claimed**: VLM scores capture task-relevant semantic meaning, not mere terminal-event detection.
- **Operational metric**: Single-episode qualitative correlation (Fig. 3), Modified Game ablation (App. F.3).
- **Aspect not measured**: The DoorKey prompt ("Does this clip contain a clear instance of goal satisfaction anywhere in it? If no visible success occurs, answer No") is a *terminal goal detection* prompt. Fig. 3 shows VLM assigns high score to "Clip A" (semantically dense: key pickup and door approach). This is consistent with the VLM detecting that the terminal event is visible in that clip, not necessarily that it understands sub-task progression per se. No quantitative correlation coefficient across episodes is reported — Fig. 3 is a single reference trace.

### OGBench "sparse-reward tasks" framing
- **Construct claimed**: OGBench is described as a "long-horizon, sparse-reward task" (Intro p. 1).
- **Operational reality**: Appendix B discloses: r_t = −1 + γφ(s_{t+1}) − φ(s_t), where φ(s) = (1/K)Σ 1[subgoal_i(s)]. This is a *dense* potential-based shaped reward providing gradient information at every step. The framing of OGBench as sparse-reward is not borne out by the reward definition in the appendix. DoorKey is genuinely sparse; OGBench is not.

## Reward/objective audit

### DoorKey (DQN/IQN)
- Reward: zero at all intermediate steps; positive only at goal completion; linearly decaying with steps taken.
- **Genuinely sparse** — VLM prioritization provides signal absent from the environment reward.
- VLM prompt: binary terminal-success detection ("Does this clip contain a clear instance of goal satisfaction…").

### OGBench/Scene (SAC/TD3)
- Reward: r_t = −1 + γφ(s_{t+1}) − φ(s_t) with φ encoding subgoal satisfaction fraction. Dense shaped reward.
- **Not sparse**: the environment reward already encodes sub-goal progress at every step via the potential function.
- Replay buffer warm-started with 10 expert demonstrations, held fixed across all methods. These demonstrations achieve goal completion and receive VLM score = 1 (Yes). VLM-RB may preferentially re-sample these demonstrations at high priority throughout training.
- VLM prompt: binary success detection ("Is there at least one clear instance of goal satisfaction… contact + displacement consistent with the goal (lift off surface, place into receptacle, open/close articulation, move to target zone)?").

## Counter-hypothesis attempts

### Claim 1–2 (sample efficiency / success rate gains)

**Counter-hypothesis A: Annealing schedule explains gains without VLM semantics.**
The linear warm-up schedule (λ: 0 → λ_max = 0.5 over first 500K steps) begins at pure uniform sampling and gradually introduces prioritization. Curriculum-style replay — starting uniform and transitioning to prioritized — is independently known to stabilize early value learning by preventing overfit to initially high-TD transitions. The PER baseline uses "Prioritized Sampling Ratio: 1.0 (Always Prioritized)" (Table 3), with no annealing. The comparison is therefore VLM-RB-with-annealing vs PER-always-prioritized — a confounded comparison. The "None" condition in Table 4 (λ_max = None, full VLM prioritization from step 0) correctly shows that full VLM prioritization without annealing fails (≈0% ASR). But this demonstrates that *unscheduled* prioritization fails — it does not show whether *PER with the same annealing schedule* would also succeed. The critical missing control is "PER + linear annealing (0 → 0.5)." **Verdict: not ruled out by any ablation in the paper.**

**Counter-hypothesis B: Expert demonstration re-sampling drives OGBench gains.**
The 10 fixed expert demonstrations get VLM score = 1 throughout training (they contain visible goal completions). VLM-RB's high-priority re-sampling of these 10 fixed episodes may effectively implement a form of offline-to-online demonstration-anchored learning. Without a VLM-RB variant that treats demonstrations with the same priority as other transitions (or excludes them), the contribution of VLM scoring on *novel agent trajectories* vs. systematic privileging of fixed demos is unclear. **Verdict: not ruled out; the paper notes demos are "held fixed across all methods" but does not ablate their re-sampling priority.**

**Counter-hypothesis C: Any curriculum heuristic (HER, temporal-recency) would match gains.**
Hindsight Experience Replay (HER) and temporal-recency prioritization are natural baselines for OGBench-style goal-conditioned manipulation tasks. AER, ERO, and ReLo are compared; HER is not. **Verdict: not ruled out for OGBench; addressed only for DoorKey via comparison against AER/ERO/ReLo.**

### Claim 5 (VLM scores "semantically grounded")

**Counter-hypothesis D: VLM detects terminal event occurrence, not semantic task progress.**
The DoorKey prompt asks about "goal satisfaction anywhere in [the clip]" — binary detection of whether the terminal state appears in a 32-frame window. This is consistent with the VLM assigning high scores only to clips that happen to contain the final reward event, not to clips showing intermediate sub-goal progress. Fig. 3 (single episode) is the only visualization — Clip A is shown as "semantically dense" (key+door approach visible), but this could be because those clips physically contain states proximal to the goal rather than because the VLM understands sub-task structure. **Verdict: partially addressed by Fig. 3 (VLM score rises before reward propagates), but no quantitative multi-episode evidence is presented.**

## Mechanism-attribution audit

### Coupled novelties: VLM binary scorer + mixture annealing schedule

VLM-RB simultaneously introduces two design choices:
1. A frozen VLM binary scorer that labels clips as goal-relevant (p^VLM ∈ {0,1}).
2. A linear annealing mixture schedule (λ_t: 0 → 0.5 over first T_schedule = 5×10^5 steps).

Table 4 (Appendix F.1) ablates λ_max ∈ {None, 0.25, 0.50, 0.75, 1.00} on DoorKey-16x16, **all with VLM scores active**. Results:
- λ_max = None (pure VLM, no uniform mixing, no schedule): N/A — fails entirely (0.00 ASR vs UER baseline of 0.24 ASR)
- λ_max = 0.25: +233.3% perf, +40.6% sample efficiency vs UER
- λ_max = 0.50 (chosen): +316.7% perf, +62.9% sample efficiency vs UER
- λ_max = 0.75: +266.7% perf, +57.0% sample efficiency vs UER
- λ_max = 1.00: +316.7% perf, +69.0% sample efficiency vs UER

The ablation correctly identifies that *no annealing at all* (None) fails, and that λ_max = 0.50 is optimal. However, **the ablation only varies λ_max within VLM-scored configurations**. There is no row for "PER + same annealing schedule (0 → 0.50)." The unresolved question: does the λ-annealing schedule itself provide the benefit (by starting uniform and gradually introducing prioritization), and would PER + same schedule perform similarly?

The Modified Game ablation (App. F.3) provides meaningful indirect evidence against the schedule-only explanation: Sprite Swap and Texture corruption variants use the same schedule and architecture, but performance degrades toward PER levels when visual semantics are corrupted. This shows the *VLM's visual recognition* contributes, not merely the schedule. However, this tests whether corrupted VLM scores hurt relative to accurate VLM scores — it does not compare VLM-RB with schedule against PER with schedule. These are related but distinct questions.

**Net assessment**: The Modified Game ablation provides genuine supporting evidence that VLM visual semantics contribute beyond any schedule effect. The mechanism-attribution gap is real but partially mitigated. The paper would be strengthened by a "PER + annealing" row in Table 4.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

**DoorKey**: Single-axis metric (ASR = binary success). No multi-objective concern — the task is binary and the same metric applies to all methods.

**OGBench/Scene**: Both VLM-RB and baselines optimize the same potential-based shaped reward r_t = −1 + γφ(s_{t+1}) − φ(s_t). VLM-RB does not modify the reward, only the replay sampling. No dropped reward terms — no multi-objective trade-off concern here.

## Framing-vs-demonstration audit

**Framing in Introduction (p. 1)**: "In long-horizon, sparse-reward tasks, such as robotic manipulation in OG-Bench (Park et al., 2024)"  
**Methodology in Appendix B (p. 13)**: r_t = −1 + γφ(s_{t+1}) − φ(s_t), where φ(s) = (1/K)Σ 1[subgoal_i(s)] — potential-based shaped reward providing non-zero gradient at every step.

This is a framing-vs-demonstration mismatch. The OGBench environment uses dense reward shaping — subgoal satisfaction is encoded in the per-step reward signal. Characterizing this as "sparse reward" in the abstract and introduction is inconsistent with the appendix disclosure. The DoorKey results represent the genuine sparse-reward evidence; OGBench does not.

**Abstract claim — "11–52% higher average success rates"**: Range is accurate across (task, algorithm, baseline) combinations. The lower bound (11%) reflects settings where baselines already perform well. The upper bound (52%) reflects Scene-5 where baselines struggle. Range construction is disclosed and internally consistent. No denominator switch detected.

**Claim — "pre-trained VLM (requiring no fine-tuning)"**: Accurate. Perception-LM-1B is used fully frozen.

**Claim — "modular plug-and-play"**: Accurate; the framework wraps any off-policy replay buffer.

## All weaknesses found

**Major:**
1. **Missing "PER + annealing schedule" ablation (mechanism attribution).** VLM-RB's two coupled novelties (VLM scorer + annealing schedule) are never independently isolated against each other. Table 4 only ablates λ_max within VLM-scored conditions. The PER baseline uses always-prioritized sampling (no schedule), making the comparison confounded. Whether PER + same linear annealing (0 → 0.5) matches VLM-RB's gains is unanswered. The Modified Game ablation partially mitigates this but does not substitute for the direct control.
   
2. **OGBench "sparse-reward" framing mismatch.** The introduction describes OGBench as a "sparse-reward task" (p. 1), but Appendix B discloses dense potential-based shaping with a per-step subgoal reward. The motivation for VLM-RB in the sparse-reward setting is primarily supported by DoorKey, not OGBench. This is a load-bearing framing claim (the paper's core motivation is sparse-reward RL) that does not fully hold for half the experimental domain.

**Minor:**
3. **Expert demonstration re-sampling confound in OGBench.** The 10 fixed expert demos receive VLM score = 1 throughout training. A targeted ablation removing demos or equalizing their sampling priority would isolate VLM scoring on agent-generated trajectories.

4. **Single-episode Fig. 3 as evidence for "semantic alignment."** No quantitative correlation coefficient across episodes is reported. The claim of "semantically grounded prioritization" rests on a single reference trace visualization.

5. **Missing HER baseline for OGBench.** For goal-conditioned manipulation tasks (OGBench/Scene), Hindsight Experience Replay is the natural comparison. AER, ERO, and ReLo are compared; HER is not. This limits the confidence that VLM-RB outperforms all semantically informed replay methods on this domain.

6. **Fig. 3 qualitative evidence.** The claim that VLM scores "closely align with task objectives" is illustrated with a single training run on a single reference episode. SEM or multi-episode correlation statistics would sharpen this.

## Defense paragraph

The paper's strongest counterargument to the mechanism-attribution concern is the Modified Game ablation (App. F.3): when rendered sprites are swapped (keys rendered as lava, doors as boxes) or replaced with abstract textures, VLM-RB's performance degrades to PER levels despite the underlying MDP, agent observations, and annealing schedule remaining identical. Since the schedule is constant across all Modified Game variants, any performance degradation must be attributable to degraded VLM scoring quality — directly implicating VLM visual semantics as an active contributor, not mere epiphenomenon of scheduling. On the OGBench framing question, the owner can argue that the shaped reward is designed to encode sub-goal structure precisely because the task is compositionally hard, making VLM's ability to detect visible goal satisfaction genuinely useful even with shaped rewards. The throughput claim (Table 6, ~12% overhead) is well-measured and conservative — three hardware configurations with explicit steps/second numbers.
