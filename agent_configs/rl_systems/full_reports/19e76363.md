## Headline empirical claims

**Claim 1 (Abstract, lines 31–35; Introduction, lines 100–104):** "Med-TIV achieves substantial gains over existing methods, improving MedQA accuracy by 23.5% and MedXpertQA by 32.0% relative to the base generator model alone."
- Operationalized: Qwen2.5-7B single-sample base (60.96%) → Hard-Weighted Self-Consistency with Med-TIV verifier at N=32 (75.26%). Relative gain = (75.26 − 60.96)/60.96 ≈ 23.5% (Table 2). MedXpertQA: 12.15% → 16.04%, relative gain ≈ 32.0% (Table 1).

**Claim 2 (Abstract, lines 35–37; Introduction, lines 105–107; Sec. 4.3 lines 373–378):** "Med-TIV demonstrates an 8× reduction in sampling budget requirement compared to prior reward model baselines... achieving equivalent accuracy with substantially fewer sampled reasoning traces during test-time search."
- Operationalized via Figure 3: Med-TIV achieves ~72.1% at N=4 on MedQA; Med-PRM requires N=32 to reach ~70.0%. Ratio: 32/4 = 8×. Section 4.3 (lines 375–378) explicitly converts this to a deployment cost claim: "Since inference cost scales approximately linearly with the number of sampled traces, this translates to equivalent performance at one-eighth the generator inference cost in practical deployment settings."

**Claim 3 (Abstract, lines 38–41; Conclusion):** "Grounding verification in dynamically retrieved evidence offers a principled path toward more reliable medical reasoning systems."
- Operationalized via Table 3: Qwen2.5-7B base 60.96% → +Med-TIV (RL) 69.60% → +Med-TIV (RL + Tool) 70.54%. Tool adds +0.94 pp.

**Claim 4 (Contributions bullet 2; Sec. 3.2):** Adaptive curriculum formulation is a primary contribution ("We introduce an iterative RL paradigm with curriculum-based difficulty adaptation").
- Not ablated against non-curriculum RL anywhere in the paper.

**Claim 5 (Introduction, lines 101–103):** "Med-TIV consistently outperforms existing medical reward model baselines and surpasses the performance of models that are up to 4× larger in scale."
- Operationalized via Table 1: Med-TIV 75.26% on MedQA vs. Med-PRM 69.99%, MedS³ 64.89%, and Qwen2.5-32B 75.26% (a tie, not surpassing).

## Construct-validity audit

### Claim 1 — "23.5% improvement in MedQA accuracy"

**Construct claimed:** Substantial improvement in medical reasoning accuracy relative to the base generator.

**Operational metric:** MedQA % accuracy using Hard-Weighted Self-Consistency with N=32 candidate traces. Binary MCQ final-answer accuracy on USMLE-style questions.

**Unmeasured aspects of the construct:**
- **Denominator misframing (framing-vs-demonstration trigger).** The 23.5% is relative to the *single-sample* base model (60.96%), not relative to competitive aggregation-matched baselines. Standard Self-Consistency at N=32 — same generator, no verifier — already reaches 66.38% (Table 2). The improvement over that directly comparable baseline is (75.26 − 66.38)/66.38 ≈ 13.4% relative. The abstract states "relative to the base generator model alone" but the phrase "base generator model" is ambiguous to readers expecting comparison against a full test-time compute-matched baseline.
- **Calibration of confidence score r_τ.** The verifier produces a confidence score r_τ ∈ [0,1] (Section 4.1), which is used in Soft-Weighted SC and Best-of-N strategies. The reliability/calibration of this score (expected calibration error, Brier score, reliability diagram) is never reported anywhere in main text or appendices A–D. The score is used as a discriminative ranking signal without any calibration validation.
- **Verifier precision/recall decomposition.** The downstream MCQ accuracy improvement is measured, but the verifier's discriminative performance as a binary classifier (precision, recall, F1 on trace-level labels) is not. This conflates the quality of the verifier's judgment with the adequacy of majority-voting aggregation over N=32 samples.
- **Open-ended clinical reasoning.** All four benchmarks (MedQA, MedMCQA, MMLU-Med, MedXpertQA) are multiple-choice. No open-ended generation task is evaluated. The "reliable medical reasoning" framing implies broader validity than closed-form MCQ answer selection.

### Claim 2 — "8× sampling efficiency gain / one-eighth the generator inference cost"

**Construct claimed:** Med-TIV achieves equivalent deployment cost at one-eighth the number of candidate traces.

**Operational metric:** Sampling budget N (number of generator-produced candidate traces) at which two methods cross the same MedQA accuracy threshold in Figure 3. Ratio: N_Med-PRM = 32 vs. N_Med-TIV = 4.

**Unmeasured aspects of the construct:**
- **Verifier inference cost.** Med-TIV's verifier is a multi-turn agent that iteratively: (a) performs `<think>` reasoning steps, (b) formulates `<search>` queries, (c) retrieves top-3 documents from a 24-million-snippet FAISS index (Appendix B.2), and (d) reasons over retrieved documents before producing a `<answer>` judgment (Table 5, Appendix B.4). The average number of search calls per verification, the number of tokens consumed per verification trajectory, and the wall-clock latency per call are **never reported anywhere in the paper or appendices A–D**. I did not find any timing, FLOPs, or per-call token count in main text, Appendix A (Limitation), Appendix B (Implementation), Appendix C (Benchmarks), or Appendix D (Case Analysis).
- **Cost-linearity assumption stated without support.** Section 4.3 (lines 375–376) asserts: "Since inference cost scales approximately linearly with the number of sampled traces." This claim is only true if the per-trace verifier cost is identical between Med-TIV and Med-PRM. Med-PRM uses static RAG (one pre-fetch pass per trace, as confirmed in Appendix B.3: "we equip [Med-PRM] with the same retrieval corpus, encoder, and top-k setting as our framework"). Med-TIV's verifier runs an unbounded number of iterative search queries per trace ("You can search as many times as you want," Table 5). Med-TIV's per-trace cost is strictly higher than Med-PRM's per-trace cost; the 8× sampling ratio is not an 8× inference cost ratio.
- **FAISS index serving overhead.** The retrieval index contains 24 million snippets distributed across multiple GPUs (Appendix B.2). Query latency for this infrastructure during inference is not reported.

### Claim 3 — "Dynamic retrieval provides principled path toward reliable medical reasoning"

**Construct claimed:** Dynamic, iterative retrieval specifically (not merely having any retrieval) explains the verifier's performance advantage.

**Operational metric:** Table 3 two-step ablation: RL alone (69.60%) vs. RL + Tool (70.54%) on Qwen2.5-7B MedQA.

**Unmeasured aspects:**
- The ablation isolates having-tool vs. not-having-tool but does not isolate *dynamic/iterative* retrieval vs. static RAG. A "Med-TIV-StaticRAG" variant — same verifier, same RL training on tool trajectories, but at inference using fixed top-k pre-fetched context rather than iterative search — is absent. This is the direct test of the "dynamic" in the paper's central contribution.
- The +0.94 pp tool contribution on MedQA is itself small. The paper describes this as "a critical secondary boost" (line 435), but does not report statistical significance or confidence intervals for any result in the paper.

### Claim 4 — Adaptive curriculum formulation (listed as primary contribution)

**Construct claimed:** Curriculum filtering meaningfully improves verification capability above standard RL training on the full dataset.

**Operational metric:** None. No ablation of "RL with curriculum" vs. "RL without curriculum" appears anywhere in the paper. Figure 4 shows iterative training progress (Base → Iter-1 → Iter-2), but curriculum is always active in both iterations; there is no no-curriculum control.

## Reward/objective audit

**Proposed method (Med-TIV):**
- Correctness Reward R_c = 1[extract(o) = ℓ], where ℓ ∈ {0,1} is trace-level binary label. R_c = 1 if verifier's final `<answer>` matches the ground-truth; 0 otherwise.
- Format Reward R_f = 1 (all formatting satisfied, ≤10 `<answer>` pairs); R_f = 0.25 (correct but tag overflow); R_f = 0 otherwise.
- Total: R = R_c × R_f.
- Training data: trace-level triplets (q, τ, ℓ_trace) from Med-PRM dataset. Step-level labels intentionally excluded.

**Baseline (Med-PRM):** Process reward model trained with step-level labels using static RAG. Entirely different supervision signal (dense step-level vs. sparse trace-level) and inference paradigm (static vs. iterative).

**Baseline (MedS³):** Self-evolved soft dual-sided process supervision. Neither RAG-augmented nor tool-integrated at evaluation.

**Overlap analysis:** Med-TIV's reward is strictly simpler (binary, trace-level) than both baselines. The reward function itself is not a source of confounding — it does not overlap with or contain the baselines' reward terms. The critical question is therefore not reward overlap but infrastructure overhead: the training data source (Med-PRM dataset) and evaluation corpora are shared with Med-PRM in controlled comparison (Appendix B.3), which is appropriate. No reward-explained confound in the traditional sense applies here.

**Flag:** The curriculum filtering criterion creates a potential self-selection bias: the model trains on instances where G sampled trajectories produce non-uniform rewards (some correct, some incorrect). This "decision-boundary" selection is a standard curriculum approach but may systematically exclude questions where the model is uniformly wrong (could benefit from training) or uniformly right (may be worth reinforcing generalization). The paper does not discuss whether filtering out "impossible" instances (where the model consistently fails) could cause underperformance on genuinely hard clinical questions.

## Counter-hypothesis attempts (at least 2 per headline claim)

### Counter-hypotheses for Claim 1 (23.5% MedQA improvement)

**Counter-hypothesis 1a: Training-evaluation overlap via Med-PRM dataset.**
Med-TIV training data derives exclusively from the Med-PRM dataset, which is built from USMLE-style medical question reasoning traces. MedQA is also a USMLE-derived benchmark. If the Med-PRM dataset contains questions or question types drawn from the same USMLE question pools that generated MedQA evaluation questions, the verifier may be learning to recognize question-specific reasoning patterns rather than generalizing medical verification. The paper provides no decontamination analysis — no overlap check between Med-PRM training questions and MedQA test questions is reported.
**Status: Not ruled out.** Appendix C.1 describes the four benchmarks but gives no decontamination statement.

**Counter-hypothesis 1b: Hard-Weighted Self-Consistency aggregation explains most gain.**
The Hard-Weighted SC strategy filters traces with verifier label ℓ=1 then majority-votes. If any binary classifier with moderate precision on trace-level labels is applied (even a weak classifier with 65% trace-level accuracy), a majority vote over 32 filtered candidates will concentrate on correct answers. The gain from 60.96% (single sample) to 75.26% may be largely attributable to the N=32 aggregation with any decent filter, not specifically to Med-TIV's retrieval-augmented quality. Evidence for this: Best-of-N (Med-TIV) with N=32 already reaches ~75% (Figure 3, extrapolating the N=32 endpoint), and Self-Consistency alone reaches 66.38% without any verifier (Table 2). The marginal contribution of Med-TIV's verifier quality above a random 50% classifier with the same filtering rate is not decomposed.
**Status: Partially ruled out.** Table 2 shows Best-of-N (Med-TIV) at N=32 achieves 75.02% — slightly below Hard-Weighted SC at 75.26%, suggesting the filtering step adds ~0.24 pp over pure ranking. The majority-voting combination matters, but the table does not provide a control with a weaker baseline verifier at the same N=32 sampling budget.

### Counter-hypotheses for Claim 2 (8× sampling efficiency)

**Counter-hypothesis 2a: Med-TIV's total inference cost at N=4 equals or exceeds Med-PRM at N=32.**
Each Med-TIV verification call involves an unbounded iterative search loop (Table 5 prompt: "You can search as many times as you want"). If the verifier issues 3–8 search queries per trace, each requiring a FAISS query over 24 million snippets plus a model forward pass for reasoning, the per-trace cost of Med-TIV could be 4–10× that of Med-PRM's single static RAG pass. At N=4, total cost would be 4 × (1 + k) generator equivalents. For the 8× claim to hold, Med-TIV's per-trace overhead must not exceed 1× (i.e., verifier cost must be negligible). This is an extraordinary assumption given the multi-turn agentic architecture.
**Status: Not ruled out.** The Limitation section (Appendix A) discusses corpus coverage and language scope but makes no mention of inference cost or wall-clock. No timing data appears anywhere.

**Counter-hypothesis 2b: The Figure 3 crossing point is benchmark-specific and sensitive to the MedQA test-set difficulty distribution.**
The 8× ratio is read from Figure 3 (MedQA), which shows Med-TIV reaching ~72.1% at N=4 vs. Med-PRM reaching ~70.0% at N=32. But the crossing point depends on the difficulty distribution of test questions. If MedQA questions at this particular test split skew toward cases where the verifier's retrieval is especially helpful (e.g., pharmacology questions where the corpus contains definitive references), the N=4 cross-over may not generalize to other benchmarks. Figure 3 also shows MedMCQA and MMLU-Med curves — the 8× annotation appears on all three plots, but the exact sampling budgets at which Med-TIV crosses Med-PRM are not numerically reported, only shown graphically. It is unclear from the figure resolution whether the crossing happens at precisely N=4 for all benchmarks.
**Status: Partially unresolved.** The paper reports "8×" for all three benchmarks in Figure 3, but does not provide numerical cross-points for MedMCQA or MMLU-Med.

### Counter-hypotheses for Claim 3 (dynamic retrieval drives principled improvement)

**Counter-hypothesis 3a: RL training on tool-integrated trajectories accounts for the full benefit, not tool use at inference.**
The Table 3 ablation compares (a) RL-trained verifier without tool access at inference vs. (b) RL-trained verifier with tool access at inference. The RL training in both cases uses tool-integrated trajectories (since curriculum filtering and training data come from running the verifier with tool access enabled). A third condition is missing: an RL verifier trained *without* tool-integrated trajectories but evaluated with tool access at inference. Without this condition, the +0.94 pp tool contribution at inference may actually reflect the policy distribution learned during RL (which involved tool use during training) rather than the runtime retrieval benefit.
**Status: Not ruled out.**

**Counter-hypothesis 3b: Additional compute steps (reasoning tokens) rather than retrieved content explain the tool benefit.**
The iterative search process introduces additional `<think>` reasoning tokens before and after each retrieval. Even if retrieval returns uninformative documents, the extra chain-of-thought reasoning could improve final judgment quality. A control with "null retrieval" (same iterative loop structure, search queries are acknowledged but return empty or random results) would isolate whether the retrieved content itself contributes or whether the reasoning scaffolding alone drives the +0.94 pp gain.
**Status: Not ruled out.**

### Counter-hypotheses for Claim 4 (curriculum formulation as key contribution)

**Counter-hypothesis 4a: Standard RL on full dataset achieves equivalent performance.**
The adaptive curriculum retains only "boundary cases" where the model produces mixed correct/incorrect outputs across G trajectories. For a policy at early training stages, boundary cases may dominate (most questions are neither trivially correct nor impossible). As training progresses, filtering out trivial instances could accelerate convergence, but the final checkpoint performance may be indistinguishable from training on all instances with standard RL. No no-curriculum ablation is reported.
**Status: Not ruled out. This is the most significant missing ablation in the paper.**

## Mechanism-attribution audit

**Coupled novelties (three components introduced simultaneously):**
1. Tool-augmented iterative retrieval during verification.
2. Dr.GRPO RL training on trace-level binary reward (vs. prior step-level supervision).
3. Adaptive curriculum formulation (filtering trivial and impossible instances).

**Ablations actually provided:**
- Table 3: RL-only vs. RL+Tool (one dimensional across two generators). This isolates tool integration at inference from RL alone, but not curriculum from RL.
- Figure 4: Base → Iter-1 → Iter-2 training iterations. Shows iterative training helps but does not ablate curriculum — curriculum filtering is always active.
- Table 4: Hyperparameters differ between Iter-1 (G=5) and Iter-2 (G=8). The group size change between iterations is a confound — the Iter-1 to Iter-2 improvement cannot be attributed solely to iterative training because the RL group size (G) changes simultaneously.

**Missing ablations:**
1. **No curriculum ablation.** A curriculum = off variant (RL training on randomly sampled balanced batches without boundary-case filtering) is absent. Curriculum is listed as a primary contribution but receives no isolated evaluation.
2. **No static-RAG vs. dynamic-retrieval ablation.** Med-TIV (RL + static single-pass RAG) vs. Med-TIV (RL + iterative search) — the "dynamic" in the paper's core claim is never isolated.
3. **No ablation of training objective novelty.** Dr.GRPO with trace-level binary reward vs. a simpler GRPO variant or a fine-tuning baseline on the same data and model is absent. The choice to use Dr.GRPO is treated as implementation but not ablated.

**Unrefuted alternative-mechanism explanations:**
- The curriculum may be functioning primarily as a deduplication/cleanliness filter rather than a difficulty curriculum — boundary cases are well-formatted, unambiguous training examples by definition (since the model can succeed on them sometimes). The improvement could reflect cleaner training batches rather than appropriate difficulty targeting.
- The iterative training benefit (Iter-1 → Iter-2) could reflect simply more gradient steps on a good data distribution rather than the self-bootstrapping mechanism the paper claims.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

Med-TIV is a verifier, not a multi-objective policy, so the classical reward-term trade-off analysis applies differently. However:

**Construct: "reliable medical reasoning systems"**
The paper frames its contribution as improving reliability. Reliability in medical AI includes multiple axes:

| Axis | Measured? | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| MCQ final-answer accuracy | Yes (4 benchmarks) | Primary metric |
| Verifier calibration (confidence scores) | No | r_τ ∈ [0,1] used for Soft-SC but never calibrated |
| Verifier precision/recall (binary classification) | No | Only downstream accuracy reported |
| Hallucination rate in verifier reasoning traces | No | Case study (Appendix D) is qualitative, N=1 |
| False positive rate (labeling incorrect traces as correct) | No | Not decomposed anywhere |
| Latency / inference cost | No | Central efficiency claim unmeasured |
| Open-ended generation quality | No | All benchmarks are MCQ |

The paper measures one axis (MCQ accuracy) and frames this as demonstrating broader "reliability." Five measurement axes that would substantiate the "reliable medical reasoning systems" claim are absent.

## Framing-vs-demonstration audit

**Framing 1 — "23.5% improvement... relative to the base generator model alone" (Abstract)**
Actual denominator: Qwen2.5-7B single-sample inference, 60.96% (Table 2, first row). This is the weakest possible baseline — no aggregation, no verifier, no inference-time compute. The most directly comparable baseline is Self-Consistency at N=32 with the same generator (66.38%, Table 2), which uses identical inference budget. Relative gain vs. that baseline: 13.4%. The abstract's choice of denominator inflates the headline by ~75% compared to the compute-matched comparison.

**Framing 2 — "equivalent performance at one-eighth the generator inference cost in practical deployment settings" (Sec. 4.3, lines 375–378)**
This converts the sampling-budget ratio directly to an inference cost claim. The framing requires that verifier cost per trace be negligible or equal across systems. Med-TIV's verifier is architecturally more expensive per trace than Med-PRM (iterative multi-turn search vs. single static RAG pass). The word "generator" in "generator inference cost" is doing substantial work — it is technically accurate (only generator sampling differs by 8×) but is framed as if total deployment cost is 8× lower. In a real deployment, verifier inference is also a deployment cost. This is a denominator-scope mismatch on a load-bearing efficiency claim.

**Framing 3 — "Tool integration provides a critical secondary boost" (Sec. 4.3 / Table 3)**
Actual numbers: Qwen2.5-7B RL alone = 69.60%; RL+Tool = 70.54%, a +0.94 pp absolute gain. The word "critical" is used for a gain of less than 1 percentage point on a benchmark with no reported confidence intervals or standard deviations. If the standard deviation of MedQA accuracy across runs is ≥0.47 pp (plausible for a system with stochastic retrieval), this "critical" gain may be within noise. No statistical testing is reported for any comparison in the paper.

**Framing 4 — "surpasses the performance of models that are up to 4× larger in scale" (Introduction, line 103)**
Actual: Med-TIV (7B verifier guiding Qwen2.5-7B generator) achieves 75.26% MedQA vs. Qwen2.5-32B standalone at 75.26% — an exact tie, not surpassing (Table 1). The 4× larger claim requires that 75.26% > 75.26%, which is false. The paper later clarifies this in Sec. 4.2 ("rival substantially larger models, even surpassing the base performance of Qwen2.5-32B"), but the Introduction claims are harder, and the Table 1 numbers show a tie, not superiority.

## All weaknesses found

**Severity: Score-2 trigger (load-bearing claim)**

- **Cost/wall-clock claim without measurement.** The "8× sampling efficiency" = "one-eighth the generator inference cost in practical deployment settings" (lines 375–378) is a central contribution (abstract, introduction ×2, Sec. 4.3). Zero wall-clock, FLOPs, per-verification token count, or memory measurements appear anywhere in the paper or appendices A–D. The cost-linearity assumption (lines 375–376) is stated without empirical support and is structurally false given Med-TIV's iterative agentic verifier vs. Med-PRM's static RAG. **Fires the "Cost / wall-clock claim without measurement" trigger.**

**Severity: Framing-vs-demonstration (load-bearing secondary claim)**

- **Denominator selection for the 23.5% headline.** The denominator is single-sample inference (60.96%), not compute-matched Self-Consistency (66.38%). The abstract is technically accurate but selects the weakest possible comparator. The most directly useful number for readers (Med-TIV vs. same-budget unguided SC) would give 13.4% relative improvement. Framing-vs-demonstration trigger for the headline number, though a softer instance than the cost claim.

- **"Surpasses Qwen2.5-32B" vs. actual tie.** Introduction (line 103) says "surpasses the performance of models that are up to 4×  larger in scale." Table 1 shows Med-TIV = 75.26% MedQA, Qwen2.5-32B = 75.26% MedQA — identical. This is a framing overclaim on a supporting contribution claim.

**Severity: Missing primary ablation (medium-high)**

- **Curriculum formulation not ablated.** Listed as a primary contribution in the abstract and intro, but no RL-without-curriculum baseline appears anywhere. The contribution of curriculum vs. standard balanced-batch RL is entirely undemonstrated.

- **Dynamic retrieval not isolated from static retrieval.** The "dynamic" mechanism — the paper's architectural core — is not isolated from simply having any retrieval. Static-RAG variant of Med-TIV is absent.

**Severity: Confound in iterative training comparison (medium)**

- **Group size G changes between Iter-1 (G=5) and Iter-2 (G=8)** (Table 4). The Iter-1 → Iter-2 improvement is confounded with hyperparameter change. The paper states "we maintain mostly consistent settings between iterations to isolate the effect of iterative training" (Appendix B.1) but G changes, which directly affects the GRPO advantage estimation and batch coverage.

**Severity: Missing statistical validation (medium)**

- **No confidence intervals, standard deviations, or statistical significance tests** for any result in Table 1, Table 2, Table 3, or Figure 3. The "critical secondary boost" of +0.94 pp is described as critical without any uncertainty quantification.

**Severity: Training-evaluation overlap risk (medium, unresolved)**

- **No decontamination analysis** between Med-PRM training questions and MedQA evaluation questions, despite both being derived from USMLE-style clinical reasoning corpora.

**Severity: Construct gap (medium)**

- **Verifier confidence scores r_τ never calibrated.** Used in Soft-Weighted SC and Best-of-N inference strategies. Reliability of the confidence signal is assumed, not demonstrated.

**Severity: Low (cosmetic / minor)**

- **MedXpertQA absolute accuracy is 16.04%** — a very low floor. The 32.0% relative gain corresponds to 3.89 pp absolute. The expert-level benchmark framing implies clinical utility, but at 16%, the system is far from expert-level.

## Defense paragraph

The paper's owners can make the following strongest rebuttal: The "8× efficiency" figure is explicitly qualified in the paper as referring to sampling budget (number of generator traces), not total system FLOPs. The claim reads "8× reduction in sampling budget requirement" (abstract) and the deployment cost claim in Section 4.3 is specifically tied to the generator's inference cost as the dominant and controllable variable (since the verifier is fixed and lightweight per call). If the verifier's cost per trace is small relative to running N=32 generator forward passes (each generator trace involves multi-step chain-of-thought reasoning, potentially thousands of tokens), then the sampling ratio does approximate the cost ratio. The 7B verifier model doing a few retrieval calls may be cheaper than 32 full generator reasoning traces from a 7B model. A rebuttal could also note that for MCQ settings, each generator trace involves extended multi-step reasoning (the Med-PRM dataset uses full reasoning chains), so N=32 generator traces is a substantial compute budget against which N=4 generator traces plus verifier overhead is plausibly cheaper — even if the precise ratio is not 8×. The strength of this defense depends entirely on empirical measurement that the paper does not provide, so the claim remains unsubstantiated rather than demonstrably wrong. The paper's MCQ accuracy results across four benchmarks and two base models are robust and well-replicated, and the controlled comparison with Med-PRM using the same retrieval corpus (Appendix B.3) is methodologically sound for the accuracy-level claims.
