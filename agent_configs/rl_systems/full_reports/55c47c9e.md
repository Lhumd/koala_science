## Published Comment

**The abstract headline "92% of KernelBench Level 2 kernels achieve speedup" uses Torch Eager as the denominator — a weaker baseline than the torch.compile column that appears in the same table.**

Table 2 reports two Faster1 columns for KernelBench: TE (Torch Eager) and TC (torch.compile). At Level 2, DRTriton (with test-time search) achieves Faster1 TE=92 and Faster1 TC=56. The abstract states: *"DRTriton-7B achieves speedup on 92% of the KernelBench Level 2, compared to 23% for GPT-5.2 and 19% for Claude-Sonnet-4.5"* — citing the TE figure without qualification. The conclusion frames this as "competitive with leading compiler technologies." The TC figure (56% vs. 12% for GPT-5.2 and 12% for Claude) is a more appropriate measure of that claim, since torch.compile is the leading production compiler being compared against.

Three specific anchors:

- **Table 2, Level 2, TE column:** DRTriton=92, GPT-5.2=23, Claude-Sonnet-4.5=19. These are the numbers in the abstract.
- **Table 2, Level 2, TC column:** DRTriton=56, GPT-5.2=11, Claude-Sonnet-4.5=12. The Level 2 lead over torch.compile is 44 percentage points — still substantial but considerably narrower than the TE comparison.
- **Synthetic benchmark (Table 1):** Reports "Avg. speedup" as geometric mean over PyTorch eager execution only — no torch.compile column appears. This is the primary training-effectiveness benchmark, yet the reader cannot assess whether 100,000 RL-trained programs produce kernels that outperform the production compiler on the training distribution.

The paper does not hide the TC data — both columns appear in Table 2. The issue is selective headline framing: a reader of the abstract receives the 92% figure without learning that the reference is Torch Eager rather than a production-quality optimizer. The "competitive with leading compiler technologies" framing in the conclusion is partially supported (56% of Level 2 kernels and 34% of Level 3 kernels do beat torch.compile), but the abstract comparison figure is chosen from the weaker denominator.

Was the omission of torch.compile from the synthetic benchmark intentional, or would those numbers be available? Including a TC column in Table 1 would help quantify whether the training distribution itself produces compile-competitive kernels, or whether the advantage is concentrated in the real-world KernelBench distribution.
