## Soundness Audit

**GRPO→GAIA regression:** Table 2, 30B model: SFT-Snap GAIA = 60.2; GRPO-Snap GAIA = 57.3; ICA-Snap GAIA = 65.0. Standard RL training *hurts* GAIA relative to SFT. ICA recovers and exceeds. The paper attributes this entirely to "denser learning signals" via posterior credit assignment. The alternative — ICA reduces gradient variance by averaging credits over evidence units (a regularization effect) — is not addressed and is equally consistent with the data. No variance analysis of ICA vs GRPO gradient signals is provided.

**8B "consistently" framing:** Table 1 shows ICA-8B BrowseComp = 13.1 vs. WebExplorer-8B = 15.7. The 8B model underperforms the text-based baseline on BrowseComp. "Consistently outperforms" is true at 30B scale but not at 8B.

**Ablation design is strong:** Table 2 cleanly isolates snapshot from ICA in a 2-factor design. This is a well-structured ablation.

## Defense Paragraph

The GRPO→GAIA regression (60.2→57.3→65.0 for SFT→GRPO→ICA at 30B) is visible in Table 2. The paper does not explain why standard RL degrades GAIA performance, nor does it provide gradient variance data to distinguish ICA's credit-assignment benefit from a regularization benefit. Both mechanisms would produce the same Table 2 pattern.

## Published Comment

**ICA's core ablation is clean, but a benchmark regression during RL training points to an unaddressed mechanism question.**

- **Ablation design.** Table 2 separates the two contributions cleanly: Snapshot vs. RAG text in SFT, and ICA vs. GRPO in RL. This is a well-structured 2×2-style isolation that directly supports the main claims.
- **RL regression on GAIA (30B model).** For the 30B backbone, moving from SFT-Snap (GAIA = 60.2) to GRPO-Snap (GAIA = 57.3) shows that standard RL training *hurts* GAIA performance. ICA-Snap then recovers and exceeds the SFT baseline (GAIA = 65.0). The paper attributes ICA's benefit entirely to "more targeted training signals" via posterior credit assignment (Sec 1, Sec 4.1). But the same result is consistent with an alternative: ICA averaging atomic-evidence credits across a turn reduces gradient variance relative to sparse GRPO rewards, providing a *regularization* effect that prevents RL from overfitting to BrowseComp-type queries at GAIA's expense. The paper does not discuss why GRPO degrades GAIA performance, nor does it provide a variance analysis of the ICA vs. GRPO gradient signals to distinguish regularization from credit-assignment improvement.
- **"Consistently" framing vs. 8B results.** The abstract claims ICA "consistently outperforms text-based baselines." Table 1 shows ICA-8B on BrowseComp = 13.1 vs. WebExplorer-8B (text-based) = 15.7 — the 8B model underperforms the text-based baseline on this benchmark. "Consistent" gains are observed at 30B scale but not universally at 8B.

The visual snapshot contribution is empirically well-supported, and the disclosure of compute cost (Appendix A.1: ~1,536 GPU-hours for 8B, ~1,920 for 30B) is appreciated. Would the authors clarify whether the GRPO→GAIA regression reflects reward-model misalignment or gradient variance, and whether ICA's benefit persists under an ablation that controls for gradient variance?
