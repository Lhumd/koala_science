# Full report — Robust and Consistent Ski Rental with Distributional Advice (86271aa6)

## Headline empirical claims

1. **Abstract:** "Experimental results across diverse distributions (Gaussian, geometric, and bi-modal) demonstrate that our framework improves consistency significantly over existing point-prediction baselines while maintaining comparable robustness."
2. **Sec 1.1 Contributions:** "Our method consistently improves consistency by roughly 5–20% over the baselines, with the largest gains observed on bi-modal and geometric distributions, where uncertainty is most impactful. Importantly, these improvements are achieved while maintaining the same robustness guarantees."
3. **Sec 6.1 Table 2:** at `(b, R) = (50, 1.7)`, ours vs Purohit-maj vs Purohit-mix on five distributions (`unif100`, `unif200`, `gauss`, `geom`, `twopoint`).
4. **Sec 6.2 Figure 1:** under Wasserstein perturbation `η` with true `p = N(90, 12²)` truncated, mean over 25 random transports.

These four together carry the abstract's "significantly… while maintaining comparable robustness" claim. Table 2 alone is the load-bearing object for the 5–20% range cited in Sec 1.1.

## Construct-validity audit

- **Construct:** "consistency" in the learning-augmented sense — performance when the prediction is accurate.
- **Operational metric (Eq. 5):** `Cons(p) := E[g(Z)] / min_t g(t)`, where `g(·)` is derived from the *true* `p`. This is internally faithful to the LA literature.
- **Aspect not measured by the operational metric:** the **robustness–consistency trade-off frontier itself**. The paper's central conceptual contribution is that randomized policies trade `R` (robustness) for consistency; the experimental section evaluates exactly *one* point on the 2D `(b, R)` grid (`b = 50`, `R = 1.7`). Whether the 5–20% advantage persists for tight robustness (`R` near 1, where the LP constraints bind harder) or for loose robustness (`R → 2`, where the geometric update permits front-loading more mass) is untested. The buy cost `b` is also fixed at 50 throughout Sec 6.1; no `b` sweep.
- **Aspect not measured (cont.):** empirical worst-case competitive ratio under adversarially realized `D`. The paper's "comparable robustness" claim is enforced by the analytical mapping `λ = 1/b − log(1 − (1+1/b)/R)` (Eq. 4) and by Lemma 5.1's LP constraints — all three methods are `R`-robust *by construction*. So "comparable robustness" in the experiments is mechanical, not empirical. Whether the actual realized worst-case ratios coincide is never measured.
- **Robustness:** a single empirical robustness check appears in Figure 1 (consistency under Wasserstein perturbation of the prediction) — but this measures degradation of *consistency* under prediction error, not the worst-case competitive ratio (the quantity `R` is supposed to bound).

## Cost / objective audit (proposed vs. baselines)

The cost function `g(t)` (Eq. 23, Sec 5.2) is identical for the proposed water-filling, Purohit-majority, and Purohit-mixture; all three evaluate `Cons(p)` using the same `g` derived from the true `p`. There is no reward-overlap concern: each method produces its own PMF over stopping days, then the same `g` is integrated. Set-relationship between proposed and baseline objectives: identical. Sign-of-dropped-terms: not applicable. Mechanism-orthogonal-component: single mechanism (water-filling), no auxiliary regularizer that could explain gains. So the comparison is methodologically clean within its narrow scope; the score-2 reward-explained / orthogonal-mechanism triggers do not fire.

What does load-bear: the *baselines themselves* are the authors' two adaptations of Purohit et al. (2018)'s point-prediction algorithm. Sec 6.1 explicitly defines them: the "majority-branch" picks one of the two Purohit branches based on `Pr[D ≥ b] > 1/2`, the "mixture" combines both branches weighted by that probability. The paper's framing ("improves consistency significantly over existing point-prediction baselines") is technically defensible — the qualifier "point-prediction" excludes Besbes et al. (2025) and Cui & Dinitz (2026) — but Sec 1.2 acknowledges Besbes et al. "studied the ski rental problem with full distributional predictions" and Cui & Dinitz (2026) as concurrent work on the same `unknown error` setting. Neither appears in Table 2 even with a fitted distance bound for Besbes. A reader interested in distributional-prediction methods has no head-to-head data.

## Counter-hypothesis attempts

**CH1 (sweet-spot artifact on `twopoint`):** the `twopoint = 0.7δ_30 + 0.3δ_120` distribution at `b = 50` has both modes straddling `b`. Any water-filling method that explicitly conditions on the bi-modal structure trivially beats a point-prediction baseline that collapses the distribution to one branch via `Pr[D ≥ b] > 1/2 = 0.3 < 1/2 → buy at b/3 branch`. At a different `(b, R)` where the modes don't straddle `b` (e.g., `b = 20` so both modes are above `b`, or `b = 200` so both are below), the gap likely shrinks. **Ruled in/out by paper's evidence:** *not ruled out* — the paper does not vary `b` for `twopoint`. The 17% gap (1.04 vs 1.25) is established at exactly one configuration where bi-modality is maximally exploitable.

**CH2 (Gaussian sweet-spot at `b`):** the `gauss` distribution `N(50, 12²)` is centered exactly at `b = 50` — the regime where rent-vs-buy is most ambiguous and where distributional information should be most decisive. Off-center Gaussians (e.g., `N(30, 12²)` mostly below `b`, or `N(100, 12²)` mostly above) would test the method outside this sweet spot. **Ruled in/out:** *not ruled out* — Sec 6.1 reports only one Gaussian configuration. Figure 1 uses `N(90, 12²)` for the perturbation experiment, which is partially off-center, but only consistency-under-error is reported there, not the Sec 6.1-style baseline comparison.

**CH3 (strawman baseline):** "Purohit-majority" hard-branches on `Pr[D ≥ b] > 1/2`, discarding almost all of the predicted distribution's information. A natural stronger baseline is Purohit (2018) given the **mean** of `p̂` (the canonical point projection of a distribution) using their optimal randomized scheme, rather than two ad-hoc adaptations. **Ruled in/out:** *not ruled out* — the paper does not report this projection-to-mean baseline.

**CH4 (single-(b, R) artifact on the trade-off claim):** the paper's *conceptual* contribution is the robustness–consistency *trade-off* (consistency improves as `R` loosens). At `R = 1.7` (mid-range), the geometric envelope `F⋆(x) = (R−1)((b/(b−1))^x − 1)` is well-conditioned. At `R` close to 1, the envelope collapses and water-filling has very little room to redistribute; at `R → 2`, all methods can essentially fill freely and the gap likely shrinks. **Ruled in/out:** *not ruled out* — no `R` sweep is reported.

**CH5 (Figure 1 perturbation distribution choice):** the perturbation experiment uses `N(90, 12²)` not `N(50, 12²)`. The paper does not justify this switch. The mixture baseline's degradation could be specific to perturbations of off-center Gaussians; mixture's flatness for the on-center Gaussian (Sec 6.1, mixture = 1.4169 ≈ majority = 1.4195) suggests the two baselines are nearly indistinguishable at `N(50, 12²)`. **Ruled in/out:** *partially refuted* — Sec 6.1 does report the on-center comparison (so at `η = 0` for an `N(50, 12²)` setup, mixture and majority would coincide). But the *trajectory* of mixture vs majority under perturbation is only reported for `N(90, 12²)`; whether the same separation appears for an on-`b` Gaussian is unmeasured.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

The two axes of evaluation are robustness (`R`) and consistency. Robustness is held constant by construction across methods (via Eq. 4 and Lemma 5.1 LP constraints), so the "comparable robustness" claim is mechanical, not empirical. Consistency is reported but only at one `(b, R)`. The paper's central conceptual contribution — the robustness–consistency *trade-off* itself — is not plotted in Section 6: no consistency-vs-`R` plot at fixed `b`, no consistency-vs-`b` plot at fixed `R`. A paper whose thesis is "we trade robustness for consistency more efficiently than baselines" should sweep at least one of those two axes.

## Framing-vs-demonstration audit

- **Abstract:** "improves consistency significantly… while maintaining comparable robustness". *Demonstration:* "significantly" is established at exactly one `(b, R)` slice on five distributions. The diversity is real (5 shapes); the slice is narrow.
- **Sec 1.1:** "5–20% over the baselines, with the largest gains observed on bi-modal and geometric distributions, where uncertainty is most impactful". *Demonstration:* the 17% twopoint gap and 12% geom gap come entirely from sweet-spot configurations (`twopoint` straddles `b`, `geom` is heavy-tailed past `b`). A reader interpreting "where uncertainty is most impactful" as a generic statement would expect this advantage to hold across `b` values; the demonstration is at one `b`.
- **No denominator switch.** The 5–20% range maps cleanly to Table 2 cells with the Purohit baselines as the denominator. Sec 6.2's perturbation switches `p` to `N(90, 12²)` (different from Sec 6.1's `N(50, 12²)`); a reader could be momentarily confused but the captions are explicit.
- **No "comparable robustness" measurement.** The phrase appears in the abstract; the paper's only empirical check related to robustness is the perturbation curve in Figure 1, which measures consistency degradation under prediction error, not realized worst-case ratios.

## Mechanism-attribution audit

Single component (water-filling with binary search on `h`, plus the geometric tightness-update from Lemma 5.1). No coupled-novelty issue — the algorithm is one mechanism, ablations are not required.

## Variance / confidence intervals

- **Table 2:** point estimates only, no error bars or seed counts. The distributions are deterministic (no sampling in `g`); the methods produce deterministic PMFs given `(b, R, p̂)`; the bisection in water-filling has a tolerance. So a single number per cell is technically defensible.
- **Figure 1:** explicitly reports "mean over 25 random transports per η" — variance reporting was considered for Sec 6.2 but omitted for Sec 6.1.
- **Asymmetry:** if the authors thought variance reporting was important enough to include for Sec 6.2 (where the perturbation is randomized), the absence in Table 2 is a presentation choice, not a fundamental constraint. Confidence bands (over the bisection tolerance, or over distribution-parameter resampling) would clarify whether the Gaussian gap (1.34 vs 1.42, ≈6%) is meaningfully larger than implementation noise.

## Missing comparisons

- **Besbes et al. (2025):** Sec 1.2 acknowledges as the most directly relevant prior work (full distributional predictions for ski rental). Excluded from Table 2 with the rationale that Besbes assumes a known distance bound. A fitted-bound version would be a natural inclusion.
- **Cui & Dinitz (2026):** acknowledged as concurrent work on the same `unknown error` setting. Excluded from Table 2.
- **Diakonikolas et al. (2021):** sampling-based stochastic model for ski rental. Different threat model but a natural sanity check.
- **Mean-projection Purohit baseline:** the canonical point-prediction reduction (use the mean of `p̂` as the point estimate) is not reported.

## Theoretical context (out of Soundness scope)

The theoretical sections are well-developed: the proof of the geometric optimum (Theorem 5.2) is clean, the extension to general `g` (Theorem 5.3) is non-trivial, and Appendix C.4's exact one-hot solution covering all `y` is a substantive technical contribution. The Clamp Policy's robust-consistent guarantee (Theorem 4.4) is also well-stated. None of this affects Soundness scoring (theory is the Theory sibling's lane), but it is offsetting strength worth noting in the verdict's overall picture.

## All weaknesses (with severity)

- **(Major)** Single `(b, R) = (50, 1.7)` slice on a fundamentally 2D `(b, R)` frontier; the paper's central claim is the trade-off itself, but neither axis is swept.
- **(Major)** `gauss` centered exactly at `b`; `twopoint` straddling `b`; both are sweet-spot configurations for distributional methods.
- **(Moderate)** Baselines are self-constructed adaptations of a point-prediction algorithm; no Besbes/Cui-Dinitz/mean-projection comparison.
- **(Moderate)** No error bars in Table 2 despite variance reporting being considered for Figure 1.
- **(Minor)** Figure 1 silently switches the true distribution from `N(50, 12²)` to `N(90, 12²)`.
- **(Minor)** "Comparable robustness" is enforced by construction (Eq. 4, Lemma 5.1) and never measured empirically.

## Defense paragraph

If challenged, the strongest evidence is the verbatim contrast between Sec 1.1's framing — *"Our method consistently improves consistency by roughly 5–20% over the baselines, with the largest gains observed on bi-modal and geometric distributions, where uncertainty is most impactful"* — and Sec 6.1's experimental setup, which fixes `(b, R) = (50, 1.7)` and uses `gauss = N(50, 12²)` (centered exactly at `b`) and `twopoint = 0.7δ_30 + 0.3δ_120` (modes straddling `b = 50`). The two largest reported gains (17% on `twopoint`, 12% on `geom`) come from configurations where distributional information is maximally exploitable; whether the 5–20% range persists for off-center Gaussians, different `b` values, or tighter `R` is empirically untested. The paper's *conceptual* contribution is the robustness–consistency trade-off, which inherently requires sweeping `R` (or `b`) to demonstrate; Sec 6 reports zero such sweep.
