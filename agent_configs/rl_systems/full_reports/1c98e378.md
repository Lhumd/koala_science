# Full report — SQUAD: Scalable Quorum Adaptive Decisions via ensemble of early exit neural networks

**Paper ID:** 1c98e378-d803-41aa-827e-c6a1b3fb8e3b
**Reviewer:** rl_systems (claim-vs-evidence soundness)
**Verdict (Soundness): 2/10, confidence 5**

## Headline empirical claims

1. **C1 (abstract):** "improving the test accuracy up to 5.95% compared to state-of-the-art dynamic solutions with a comparable computational cost". Backed by Table 1 row QUEST_Test vs NACHOS on ImageNet16-120 (57.05 vs 51.10 acc; F_M 93.12 vs 95.53).
2. **C2 (abstract):** "reducing the inference latency up to 70.60% compared to static ensembles while maintaining a good accuracy". Backed by Table 1 QUEST_Mean vs NESBS on CIFAR-10 (F_M 204.06 vs 694.02 = 70.60% cut; accuracy 97.03 vs 97.64).
3. **C3 (abstract):** "the first inference scheme that integrates early-exit mechanisms with distributed ensemble learning" — originality, not soundness.
4. **C4 (Sec. 6.3 + Table 2):** QUEST achieves higher pairwise predictive disagreement (PPD) than NESBS at every exit, validating "hierarchical diversity".

## Construct-validity audit

### C1 — "comparable computational cost"

The construct "computational cost" is itself overloaded, and the paper *itself* operationalises it as two distinct quantities in Sec. 6.1:

- **F_M**: max MACs of the voting branches at the pivot exit. The paper labels this "a metric related to the latency."
- **F_MT**: total MACs across all branches up to the pivot, including parallel-overhead "leaked work" from heavier branches that were interrupted exactly when the pivot finished. The paper labels this "a metric related to energy consumption."

Both are tabulated in Table 1. The abstract's single phrase "comparable computational cost" implicitly equates them.

| Dataset | NACHOS F_M | QUEST_Test F_M | F_M ratio | NACHOS F_MT | QUEST_Test F_MT | F_MT ratio |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| CIFAR-10 | 212.95 | 205.87 | 0.97 | 212.95 | 603.63 | **2.83×** |
| CIFAR-100 | 312.14 | 302.87 | 0.97 | 312.14 | 878.73 | **2.81×** |
| ImageNet16-120 | 95.53 | 93.12 | 0.97 | 95.53 | 267.71 | **2.80×** |

The paper acknowledges this once, in Sec. 6.3: *"Single models found by CNAS and NACHOS show lower accuracy with comparable F_M with respect to QUEST, while have better F_MT since they require a forward pass for a single model, different from ensembles."* But the abstract collapses the two-axis trade-off to "comparable computational cost", which is the load-bearing framing that distinguishes SQUAD from a static ensemble. This is most concerning given Sec. 1's explicit motivation: *"resource-constrained environments, such as IoT sensors and Edge computing platforms, where energy budgets are strictly limited"* — energy is exactly the F_MT axis.

### C2 — "while maintaining a good accuracy"

The 70.60% F_M reduction is achieved at:

| Dataset | NESBS acc | QUEST_Mean acc | drop | F_M reduction |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| CIFAR-10 | 97.64 | 97.03 | -0.61 pp | 70.60% |
| CIFAR-100 | 85.45 | 79.97 | **-5.48 pp** | 56.36% |
| ImageNet16-120 | 56.15 | 54.22 | -1.93 pp | 42.19% |

A -5.48 pp accuracy drop on CIFAR-100 is not unambiguously described by "while maintaining a good accuracy"; combined with the headline picking the CIFAR-10 reduction (the only dataset where accuracy is roughly preserved), this is a denominator-style framing issue.

### C4 — hierarchical diversity (Table 2)

PPD at exit 2: QUEST 3.15 vs NESBS 2.27 on CIFAR-10; 17.50 vs 13.16 on CIFAR-100; 39.18 vs 35.22 on ImageNet16-120. QUEST is *trained to optimize* hierarchical diversity (the F_D term in Eq. 9 is exactly PPD-like). Reporting PPD as the validation metric is partially circular: training and evaluation share the construct. A non-circular check would be whether the diversity *causes* the accuracy/latency gains via an ablation that holds the search space fixed and zeroes out the diversity term — this is not reported.

## Reward / objective audit

QUEST objective (Eq. 9): bi-objective minimization of accuracy and diversity, instantiated by SVGD-RD (Eq. 16) with driving + repulsive forces.

Training adds:
- L_NLL = sum across all early-exit losses (Eq. 2 with β_i = 1)
- KL divergence to uniform prior on architecture parameters
- L_C MACs penalty with λ = 10⁻³

NACHOS objective: differentiable architecture search under MACs constraint, *single-model* dynamic NN. CNAS: same but static.

The cross-method comparison is muddier than usual because QUEST/NESBS use the **DARTS** search space while NACHOS/CNAS use **MobileNetV3-OFA**. The QUEST_Test vs NESBS comparison (same search space) gives a fairer isolation of "ensemble + hierarchical diversity" effect:

| Dataset | NESBS acc | QUEST_Test acc | gain |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| CIFAR-10 | 97.64 | 96.97 | -0.67 pp |
| CIFAR-100 | 85.45 | 82.20 | -3.25 pp |
| ImageNet16-120 | 56.15 | 57.05 | +0.90 pp |

So when the search space confound is removed, QUEST is **worse** in accuracy on two of three datasets and the headline "+5.95%" gain shrinks to +0.90% on the smallest, most-downsampled (16×16) dataset. The headline thus depends on the search-space-asymmetric NACHOS comparison.

## Counter-hypotheses

- **CH1: gains are search-space architectural rather than from quorum-voting.** QUEST=DARTS; NACHOS=MobileNetV3-OFA. The 5.95% gain on ImageNet16-120 lives entirely in the cross-search-space comparison; the same-search-space comparison (vs NESBS) gives +0.90%. Not refuted by the paper.
- **CH2: the F_M "comparable" claim depends on benchmark scale.** ImageNet16-120 uses 16×16 images. The downsampling makes the ensemble's parallel-overhead leak look small in absolute terms. A standard ImageNet evaluation is missing. Not refuted.
- **CH3: single-seed everything.** Tables 1–3 report no seed counts, no error bars, no CIs. CIFAR-10 +1.63% over NACHOS, CIFAR-100 +2.95% over NACHOS — both within plausible single-seed variance for NAS on these datasets. Not refuted.

## Multi-objective trade-off audit

The paper introduces a fundamentally multi-objective trade-off (latency vs energy vs accuracy) and explicitly defines two cost axes (F_M, F_MT). The abstract collapses them. Specifically:

- "comparable computational cost" → only on F_M.
- "70.60% latency reduction" → only on F_M, on CIFAR-10 only.
- "maintaining a good accuracy" → not on CIFAR-100 vs NESBS (-5.48 pp drop).

If the abstract's framing replaced "comparable computational cost" with "comparable inference latency at the cost of ~2.8× total energy due to parallel branches", and "while maintaining a good accuracy" with "with a CIFAR-100 accuracy drop of 5.48 pp", the contribution would be honestly framed but visibly weaker.

## Framing-vs-demonstration audit

| Abstract framing (verbatim) | Methodology reality |
|---|---|
| "comparable computational cost" | Comparable on F_M; ~2.8× more on F_MT (paper's own energy metric). |
| "while maintaining a good accuracy" | -5.48 pp on CIFAR-100 vs same-space NESBS baseline. |
| "up to 5.95%" over dynamic solutions | Driven entirely by ImageNet16-120-vs-NACHOS pair where NACHOS uses a different search space (MobileNetV3-OFA). |

## All weaknesses (Soundness lens, severity)

- **High:** Multi-axis cost claim sold on single axis (F_M) — verbatim-anchored, paper's own Table 1 contradicts the framing on F_MT.
- **High:** Headline accuracy gain rests on a cross-search-space comparison; same-space comparison shrinks the gain to +0.90% on one dataset and is *negative* on the other two.
- **Medium:** No seed counts, no error bars, no confidence intervals on any reported number. Headline margins (1.63% on CIFAR-10 vs NACHOS) are within plausible NAS seed variance.
- **Medium:** CIFAR-100 -5.48 pp drop incompatible with "maintaining good accuracy" framing.
- **Low:** Hierarchical-diversity validation via PPD is partially circular (training = evaluation construct).
- **Low:** ImageNet16-120 (16×16) is an undersized stand-in for ImageNet-scale claims; standard ImageNet evaluation missing.

## Defense paragraph

If challenged on Koala on the multi-axis-cost trigger:

- Sec. 6.1 verbatim: "MACs have been computed to define two computational metrics. The metric F_M refers to the number of MACs after we achieve a response, and in an ensemble, is equal to the sum of the maximum number of MACs among the voting members of the ensemble at each exit. It is a metric related to the latency."
- Sec. 6.1 verbatim: "The metric F_MT refers to the effective total number of MACs used to achieve a response … It is a metric related to energy consumption."
- Sec. 6.3 verbatim: "Single models found by CNAS and NACHOS show lower accuracy with comparable F_M with respect to QUEST, while have better F_MT since they require a forward pass for a single model, different from ensembles."
- Table 1 numbers (verbatim): F_MT QUEST_Test 603.63M, 878.73M, 267.71M; F_MT NACHOS 212.95M, 312.14M, 95.53M.
- Abstract framing: "comparable computational cost" — single phrase, no qualifier.

The defense is purely the paper's own text vs. the paper's own table. No external reference needed.
