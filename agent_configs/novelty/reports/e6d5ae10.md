# Novelty Audit — Hard Labels Mislead Rotation Invariance (e6d5ae10)

**The paper extends Warmuth et al. (2025) from square loss to logistic loss: rotation-invariant algorithms (including gradient descent on fully connected NNs with rotation-invariant initialization) cannot recover a sparse target from hard labels when the link function is logistic — with significantly more involved proof techniques.**

## Decomposition

- **Warmuth et al. 2025 (direct predecessor):** The same lower bound result was shown for square loss. The paper explicitly positions itself as a generalization to logistic loss, stating: "Analogous results have been shown before for the square loss... The generalization to logistic loss is important because of its ubiquitous use in Machine Learning."
- **Logistic loss proof (the novel technical contribution):** The paper claims the proof for logistic loss is "significantly more involved" than the square loss case. The logistic link's non-linearity introduces additional analytic challenges. Whether this constitutes a sufficiently novel contribution depends on how much the proof techniques differ — the paper asserts this distinction but the extent is not quantifiable without reading the full proofs.
- **Practical scope (important):** The lower bound applies to "a general class of rotation invariant algorithms including gradient descent on feed forward neural networks with a fully connected input layer that is initialized with a rotation invariant distribution" — this covers most practical neural network settings, making the result broadly applicable to knowledge distillation theory.

## Reframe

"We prove that the failure of rotation-invariant algorithms to recover sparse targets, already known for square loss (Warmuth 2025), extends to logistic loss via more involved proof techniques, covering all standard NN gradient descent settings." The practical implication: soft labels (from distillation) are needed for sparse target recovery with logistic classifiers.

## Concrete fix

The paper claims "significantly more involved" proof techniques without detailed comparison to the Warmuth 2025 proof approach. Highlighting the specific new technique (the key lemma or analytic innovation required for logistic vs. square) would make the theoretical contribution more transparent.
