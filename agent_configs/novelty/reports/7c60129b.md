# Novelty Audit — AI Empathy Erodes Cognitive Autonomy (7c60129b)

**The framing contribution is applying Bjork's (1994) Desirable Difficulties framework to AI alignment: RLHF-optimized "helpfulness" for adult annotators may systematically remove cognitive friction that is developmentally beneficial for younger users.**

## Decomposition

- **Sycophancy as AI failure mode (prior art):** Sycophancy in RLHF-aligned models is documented (Perez et al. 2022, various). This paper extends the concern specifically to developmental harm for younger users via the cognitive autonomy lens.
- **Desirable difficulties framework (theoretical framing):** Bjork (1994) showed cognitive friction — requiring effort, active retrieval, desirable difficulty — improves learning retention. AI systems optimized to minimize friction (RLHF reward for "helpful, harmless, honest") systematically remove this friction. This is a clear and important framing.
- **Empirical claim vs. theoretical argument:** The title's claim ("erodes cognitive autonomy") implies empirical evidence, but from the abstract it is unclear whether the paper provides controlled studies or primarily a theoretical/conceptual argument. If primarily theoretical, the framing contribution is real but the score reflects that limitation.

## Reframe

The contribution is most accurately stated as: "RLHF's adult-centric reward signals may create developmental misalignment for younger users, where the cognitive friction removed by 'helpfulness' optimization is precisely the friction that supports cognitive development per Desirable Difficulties theory." This is a perspective/framing contribution with important safety implications.

## Concrete fix

Empirical grounding via user studies comparing cognitive outcome metrics (retention, transfer, problem-solving) between AI-assisted and unassisted learning in younger age groups would elevate this from a framing contribution to an empirical finding.
