**Med-TIV = (TIR-Judge RL+tool paradigm) × (medical domain transfer) × (curriculum boundary-case filtering); the novel piece is the domain instantiation with iterative-only RL training — the tool-integration contribution is smaller than the title suggests.**

The paper's novelty sits primarily on the **domain** axis, not the algorithmic axis. Breaking down the contribution:

- **Algorithmic recipe:** TIR-Judge (Xu et al., 2025, "Incentivizing agentic reasoning in LLM judges via tool-integrated reinforcement learning") already trains LLM judges with RL + tool integration in the general domain. Med-TIV replaces code execution with medical search and pairwise scoring with binary trace verification — a domain substitution rather than a new training paradigm.
- **Training mechanism:** The iterative RL approach without SFT cold-start ("RL-Zero paradigm") follows Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) and related RL-for-search work. The curriculum formulation (retaining only boundary instances with non-zero reward variance) is the most technically distinctive piece and is presented as supporting infrastructure rather than the headline contribution.
- **Performance delta:** The paper's own ablation (Table 3) reveals that RL training provides ~8-9% absolute accuracy gain while tool integration adds only ~1% — suggesting the "tool-integrated" framing in the title weights the tool contribution more heavily than the evidence supports.

Reframed honestly, the contribution is: **a medical verifier trained with iterative RL and curriculum-based difficulty adaptation, with dynamic retrieval as a secondary supporting component.** The strongest claim is not about tools per se but about the RL training approach producing a data-efficient verifier (20K training instances vs. Med-PRM's 111K) that transfers across generator models.

Could the authors clarify the precise technical delta between Med-TIV's iterative RL training and TIR-Judge's approach — specifically, whether the performance gains relative to Med-PRM are attributable to the training paradigm itself (iterative RL, curriculum) or to the dynamic retrieval component, and whether a TIR-Judge-style model domain-adapted to medical data would serve as a more informative baseline?
