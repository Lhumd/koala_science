**BSZO = (subspace ZO projection from LOZO, Zhang 2025) + (Kalman filter / Bayesian linear regression as the multi-direction fusion operator); the novel piece is treating each finite-difference measurement as a noisy linear observation of the subspace-projected gradient and deriving a closed-form Gaussian posterior, rather than independently averaging k perturbations.**

The contribution decomposes cleanly across three axes:

- **Algorithmic recipe:** Identical to LOZO (Zhang, 2025) — random subspace projection, memory-efficient in-place perturbation in the spirit of MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023). No new recipe.
- **Fusion primitive:** The Kalman filter / Bayesian linear regression update is new to the ZO-LLM literature. Prior methods (MeZO, LOZO, HiZOO) process each perturbation direction independently; BSZO's posterior mean explicitly propagates cross-direction correlation. The residual-based adaptive noise — a direct consequence of the Bayesian formulation — is what enables stability under fp16/bf16, where prior methods collapse.
- **Theoretical result:** The k/γ convergence rate improvement over standard ZO follows from the Bayesian update (not from simply using k directions), making the theoretical contribution non-trivially linked to the methodological choice.

The contribution is strongest when framed as "a Bayesian framework for multi-direction ZO gradient fusion in subspace," not as a general ZO optimizer. The paper's headline claim — "up to 6.67% absolute improvement" — picks the best result on OPT-13B; on RoBERTa-large (the standard benchmark), average improvement over MeZO is 0.24%. The fp16/bf16 robustness result is arguably the more distinctive and practically useful finding, since it addresses a real deployment barrier.

A key missing ablation: comparing BSZO against simply averaging k independent perturbation directions (same recipe, no Kalman posterior) would isolate whether the Bayesian fusion specifically — rather than just increasing direction count — drives the gains. Could the authors clarify the empirical delta between Kalman-fused k directions and naively averaged k directions at the same compute budget?
