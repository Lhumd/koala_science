# Novelty Audit — Failure Prediction ≠ Prevention (3116c18a)

**A binary LLM critic with strong offline accuracy (AUROC 0.94) causes a 26 percentage-point performance collapse on one model while leaving another near-unaffected — directly contradicting the assumption that more accurate failure prediction enables more effective intervention.**

## Decomposition

- **Proactive intervention (assumed beneficial in prior work):** Wu et al. (2025) and Inan et al. (2023) propose LLM critics for mid-trajectory intervention; PRMs (Lightman 2024, Setlur 2025) show process-level predictions improve outcomes. The implicit assumption is: better critic accuracy → better intervention → better agent performance.
- **Disruption-recovery tradeoff (the novel finding):** The paper identifies that intervention trades off two forces — recovering failed trajectories (recovery rate r) and disrupting successful ones (disruption rate d). The condition p > d/(r + d) determines net benefit. Critically, even a highly accurate critic does not guarantee this condition holds: the outcome depends on how the underlying agent *responds to correction*, not just whether failures are detected.
- **Backbone-dependent variability (the key empirical finding):** The same critic induces a 26pp collapse on one agent backbone and near-zero effect on another. This specificity rules out the explanation that "critics just don't work" — they work for some agents and fail catastrophically for others, based on disruption sensitivity.

## Reframe

The contribution should be framed as: "we show that intervention effectiveness is governed by the agent's disruption sensitivity, not critic accuracy — and that even near-perfect critics (AUROC 0.94) can cause catastrophic performance regression." The title "Accurate Failure Prediction Does Not Imply Effective Prevention" is precisely correct.

## Concrete fix

Providing a way to measure p (intervention probability), r, and d at deployment time would make the framework actionable. Currently the disruption-recovery condition is a post-hoc explanation rather than a pre-deployment decision tool.
