# Novelty Audit — RAPO: Risk-Aware Preference Optimization (d1e20336)

**The paper's novel axis is the empirical finding that safe-reasoning token proportion predicts jailbreak defense success, which motivates a composite RL reward (adequacy + suitability); the RAPO method is GRPO + this reward signal, incremental over TARS and IPO which already apply RL to safe reasoning.**

## Decomposition

- **Safe reasoning RL (concurrent):** TARS (mixed prompts + balanced reward) and IPO (corrective interventions + preference learning) already apply RL to train LRMs for safe reasoning. The related work section acknowledges both. The space is crowded.
- **Adaptive allocation (most novel):** TARS uses a static reward; RAPO introduces *adequacy* (is the proportion of safe-reasoning tokens sufficient for this risk level?) as a dynamic reward. This explicitly rewards the model for scaling safe reasoning depth with attack complexity — a specific new reward signal not in TARS or IPO.
- **ICL framing (framing contribution):** The framing that safe reasoning = in-context self-alignment under in-context optimization generates one testable prediction: safe-reasoning token proportion predicts defense success. The paper verifies this empirically. The prediction is accurate, not counterintuitive.
- **SFT warm-up:** Standard format alignment step; not a novel contribution.

## Reframe

The key contribution is the empirical principle + its operationalization as a reward: "safe reasoning success correlates with proportion of safe-reasoning tokens, so reward the model for allocating more safe-reasoning tokens to higher-risk inputs." Framing this as "risk-aware preference optimization" is accurate but risks conflating the framing contribution with a full theoretical framework, which it is not.

## Concrete fix

Adding ablations that isolate the adequacy reward vs. suitability reward vs. GRPO baseline on the same benchmark would clarify which component drives the generalization gain. The current comparison vs. TARS/IPO doesn't isolate the reward contribution. Could the authors also clarify the exact difference between RAPO and IPO in their treatment of preference data?
