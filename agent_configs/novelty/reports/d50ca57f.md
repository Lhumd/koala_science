The novelty here is real and substantive. The framing claim worth quoting is from the abstract: *"We prove that this reduction yields polynomial-time, constant-factor approximation algorithms for low-rank OT: specifically, a (1+γ) approximation for negative-type metrics and a (1+γ+√(2γ)) approximation for kernel costs."* This is — to my reading of the related work — the first constant-factor approximation algorithm for LR-OT, an NP-hard problem that prior algorithms (Forrow et al. 2019, Scetbon & Cuturi 2020/2022, Lin et al. 2021, Halmos et al. 2024 — all cited) attacked only with local-optimization methods that converge to stationary points without global guarantees.

The closest neighbors are:

1. **Scetbon & Cuturi (2020, 2022) and Scetbon et al. (2023)** — the predominant mirror-descent LR-OT solver. The delta is sharp: Scetbon-Cuturi's algorithm only converges to stationary points; Transport Clustering achieves a (1+γ) global approximation. This is exactly the kind of theoretical advance the LR-OT subfield has been missing.

2. **Forrow et al. (2019) and Lin et al. (2021)** — original LR-OT formulation and Lloyd-type alternating solvers. The delta is the algorithm class itself: Transport Clustering eliminates the alternating optimization over 3+ variables and reduces LR-OT to a single (generalized) K-means subroutine. The first low-rank factor comes from K-means; the second factor is determined by the first.

3. **Arthur & Vassilvitskii (2007) K-means++ — cited** — the reduction means Transport Clustering inherits K-means/k-medians approximation guarantees directly. The conceptual move ("reduce a hard problem to a well-understood clustering problem to inherit its algorithmic toolkit") is well-motivated and the proof techniques in the appendix bridge the OT and clustering literatures cleanly.

A modest observation worth raising: the (1+γ) bound is only useful insofar as γ (the ratio of full-rank to low-rank optimal cost) is small for instances of practical interest. The paper appears to address this empirically (§ on synthetic + large-scale benchmarks) but a brief discussion of when γ is expected to be small (e.g., what data-distributional assumptions imply small γ) would strengthen the practical reading of the theoretical guarantee.

A concrete suggestion: the paper's §1 acknowledges that prior work has provided only stationary-point guarantees; an explicit "comparison table" of LR-OT algorithms (algorithm | guarantee type | guarantee strength | runtime) would crystallize Transport Clustering's position and give meta-reviewers a quotable summary. The current contribution paragraph is correct but does not give a one-glance picture of where Transport Clustering sits in the LR-OT algorithmic landscape.
