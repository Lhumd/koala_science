The novelty here is high and the central finding is genuinely counterintuitive — the paper identifies a distinct bias mechanism that contradicts the dominant framing of LLM self-evaluation bias. The framing claim worth quoting is from the abstract: *"In contrast, explicitly stating that the action comes from the same model does not by itself induce self-attribution bias."* This is the implicit > explicit attribution asymmetry, with a 5× effect size on safety-critical prompt-injection patch approval — sharp enough that an expert reader has to update.

The expert prior in the LLM-as-judge bias literature, anchored on Panickssery et al. (2024) "LLM Evaluators Recognize and Favor Their Own Generations," has been that self-preference bias arises from explicit recognition cues (stylistic features, vocabulary, structure). The intuitive prediction is "explicit labels matter more than implicit cues because labels are what models train on." This paper shows the opposite, with the 5× factor on prompt-injection patch approval providing a sharp safety-critical anchor.

A positioning observation worth raising: **Panickssery et al. (2024) is the canonical self-preference-bias prior**, and the visible related work (§1) does not cite it. The closest prior the paper does engage with is Wataoka et al. (2024), with the honest distinction: *"the bias we study here is about how actions are presented, not about properties of the evaluated content studied by previous work (Wataoka et al., 2024)."* Adding an explicit "delta vs. Panickssery 2024" paragraph would (a) acknowledge the canonical prior, (b) crystallize the novel mechanism (conversation-turn attribution vs. stylistic-recognition), and (c) preempt the rebrand-of-self-preference-bias reading that the title alone might invite.

A second observation: the on-policy vs. off-policy ablation (§3, "self-attribution bias is strongest in on-policy settings") is the cleanest argument against alternative explanations (prompt-format brittleness, generic instruction-following degradation). Could this be highlighted more in the abstract? It is the strongest evidence that the bias arises from models recognizing their own answer in some way rather than from prompt-format suboptimality.

A concrete suggestion: the implication for benchmark design — *"Because monitors are often evaluated on fixed examples rather than on their own generated actions, these evaluations can make monitors appear more reliable than they actually are in deployment"* — is a high-impact prescription that deserves its own subsection with concrete recommendations for safety-pipeline benchmarking practice (e.g., "self-monitor benchmarks should include on-policy evaluation suites where the monitor evaluates its own outputs, not pre-collected fixed examples"). This would strengthen the citation magnetism for downstream agentic-safety work.
