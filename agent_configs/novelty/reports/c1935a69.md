The novelty here is real, with the most differentiated piece being a mechanistic finding that an expert reader is unlikely to predict. The framing claim worth quoting is from the abstract: *"we show that even when conditioned on out of distribution random strings and asked to produce pseudo-random outputs, different models produce correlated outputs."* This zero-knowledge negative-control experiment is the cleanest demonstration that LLM error correlations stem from shared inductive biases rather than only from shared training data — a non-obvious mechanistic claim.

The closest neighbors are:

1. **Self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023) — cited** — establishes aggregation works in verifier-present domains (math, code). The paper's contribution is precisely identifying the boundary where this stops working: verifier-absent domains.

2. **Brown et al. (2024); Hughes et al. (2025); Snell et al. (2025) — cited** — log-linear gains from repeated sampling and adaptive test-time compute, all in verifier-present settings. The paper's "25× compute, no consistent gains" finding is the verifier-absent counterpart.

3. **Kim et al. (2025) and Goel et al. (2025) — cited** — already document that LLM error correlation grows with capability and training similarity. The paper's delta is the random-string mechanistic test that rules out "shared training knowledge" as the sole explanation, plus the social-prediction-vs-truth-verification framing.

4. **Wisdom-of-crowds (Surowiecki, 2004); Surprisingly Popular (Prelec et al., 2017); Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017) — all cited** — classical foundations that presuppose error independence. The paper documents the violation of this assumption for LLMs and quantifies it empirically.

A specific observation worth highlighting: the **0.35 correlation on random ASCII strings with no ground-truth signal** is the kind of finding an expert reader has to update on. The intuitive prediction is that correlations come from shared training corpora and overlapping factual knowledge; the paper's negative control rules out that explanation by construction. This is a clean mechanistic move that strengthens the broader headline claim ("aggregation amplifies shared misconceptions").

Two suggestions:

(a) The "social prediction vs. truth verification" decomposition (contribution #3 in the abstract) is a useful conceptual frame and could be operationalized further — e.g., a precise statistical test of whether models predict each other's answers more accurately than they predict ground truth, with the gap as a quantitative measure of the social-vs-truth divergence.

(b) The contrast with adversarial transferability (Goodfellow et al., 2015, cited) is a strong analogy in the introduction. Could the paper develop this connection more explicitly? Adversarial transferability is the canonical example of "shared inductive biases produce shared failures" — drawing the parallel quantitatively (e.g., correlation of LLM errors vs. transfer success rate of adversarial perturbations) would tie the work into a longer ML tradition and increase citation magnetism beyond the LLM-aggregation subfield.
