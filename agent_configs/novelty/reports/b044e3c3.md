The novelty here is real but is best framed as a domain-specific theoretical formalization of established mathematical machinery, rather than a new methodological direction. The framing claim worth quoting is from the abstract: *"the theoretical connection between embedding geometry and optimization dynamics remains unexplored"* for SPD manifolds in EEG. The contribution is to fill that formal gap with three quantitative theoretical results, then validate them empirically across 1,500+ runs.

The closest neighbors are:

1. **Log-Euclidean framework (Arsigny et al., 2006) — cited extensively** — foundational SPD embedding for tangent-space mapping. The paper provides the formal embedding-comparison analysis that prior SPD-EEG work used Log-Euclidean by default without articulating its conditioning properties.

2. **Bures-Wasserstein distance (Bures 1969; Villani 2009) — cited** — established mathematical tool. The paper applies it systematically to EEG SPD networks for the first time, with the Daleckii-Krein matrix calculus giving the √κ vs κ conditioning comparison.

3. **SPDNet (Huang & Van Gool, 2017) and ManifoldNet (Chakraborty et al., 2022) — cited** — SPD neural networks with fixed geometric operations. The paper's delta is learnable embedding selection with theoretical guidance.

4. **Daleckii-Krein matrix calculus** — classical mathematical framework, applied here to derive gradient conditioning bounds for SPD networks. The application is non-trivial and gives the channel-count-dependent prediction (advantage emerges at d≥22).

The substantive novelty axis is the **channel-count-dependent prediction** structure: the theory predicts that BWSPD's gradient conditioning advantage emerges only at high channel counts, that BN-Embed approximates Riemannian BN with O(ε²) error proportional to channel count, etc. — and the empirical results match these predictions across the 8-channel SSVEP, 22-channel motor imagery, and 56-channel ERP datasets. This kind of pre-registered theoretical prediction validated empirically is unusually rigorous for the SPD-EEG subfield and is the most differentiated contribution.

A modest observation: the SOTA framing in the abstract (BCI2a: 95.37%, BCIcha: 95.21%, MAMEM: 99.07%) is impressive but should be contextualized — could the authors clarify whether these numbers improve over the most recent SPD-EEG papers (e.g., other 2024-2025 SPD Transformer variants) and over raw-time-series approaches like EEG-Conformer? The current text positions against "classical Riemannian classifiers" and "recent SPD baselines" but a head-to-head comparison table with the closest concurrent SPD-Transformer methods would strengthen the SOTA claim.

A concrete suggestion: the multi-band tokenization gain (+3.96 to +4.24 pp on BCI2a/BCIcha) is the largest empirical effect and is briefly mentioned in the abstract. This deserves its own contribution bullet — the multi-band-token framing is a clean architectural choice and could be highlighted as the practical recipe for BCI deployment, not just as a marginal gain.
