**ICA contributes two partially independent novelties: (1) visual-native web representation (rendered snapshots instead of text parsing, shared with prior visual web agents like WebVoyager and SeeAct), and (2) ICA — a post-hoc mechanism estimating each retrieval step's information contribution to the final answer and back-propagating dense GRPO credits; the ICA axis is the more distinctive contribution.**

The paper's novelty decomposes across three axes:

- **Observation modality (visual snapshots):** Rendering web pages as screenshots rather than parsing HTML overlaps substantially with WebVoyager (He et al. 2024) and SeeAct (Zheng et al. 2024), both of which use rendered visual observations for web agents. The paper's natural comparison class for this axis should include these visual agents, but the evaluation compares primarily against text-based web RL agents (He et al. 2025, Wu et al. 2025), making this contribution appear larger than it is.

- **Base optimizer (GRPO):** The paper adopts GRPO (Shao et al. 2024) directly. The delta vs. GRPO is entirely the ICA credit signal; the optimizer itself is established.

- **Credit signal (ICA posterior analysis):** Prior web-agent RL uses sparse terminal outcome rewards only. ICA introduces post-hoc estimation of each search step's contribution to the final answer, enabling dense per-step GRPO updates. This is the genuinely new mechanism — analogous in spirit to hindsight credit assignment (RL literature) but domain-adapted for information retrieval, with the specific posterior information-contribution framing appearing novel for this setting.

Framed primarily as "ICA for information-seeking RL with visual observations," the contribution is cleaner and more defensible. The dual-billing of visual snapshots as a co-equal contribution to ICA inflates the novelty footprint, since the visual observation modality has clear precedents in the visual web agent literature.

A key evidentiary gap: the paper lacks an ablation comparing text-based observations + ICA versus visual observations + ICA. Without this, the reported improvements cannot be attributed specifically to ICA's dense credit mechanism versus the representation change. Could the authors clarify whether ICA's gains hold under text-based observations, to isolate the credit assignment contribution from the observation modality effect?
