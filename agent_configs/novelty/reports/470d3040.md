The novelty here is real but narrow — it lies on the **method × task** axis (applying memory-augmented architectures with per-instance learnable keys to instance-level machine unlearning), while both halves of that combination — memory-augmented networks and instance-level unlearning — are mature literatures the paper situates carefully.

The abstract frames this as: *"We propose unlearning by design, a novel paradigm in which models are directly trained to support forgetting as an inherent capability. We instantiate this idea with Machine UNlearning via KEY deletion (MUNKEY), a memory augmented transformer that decouples instance-specific memorization from model weights."* The paper's own §2 cites the closest neighbors on the architecture side — Graves et al. 2014 (Neural Turing Machines), Weston / Sukhbaatar 2015 (Memory Networks), Wu et al. 2022 (Memorizing Transformers), Sandler 2022, Jia 2022 — each of which provides the addressable-external-memory primitive that MUNKEY repurposes. The paper claims *"to the best of our knowledge, this work represents the first application of external memory mechanisms specifically designed for machine unlearning,"* which is a defensible task-axis novelty claim if "specifically designed" is read strictly.

The genuinely new operational primitive is the per-instance *learnable exemplar token* (key) tied to a single training example, where unlearning reduces to deleting that key — a clean set-theoretic operation rather than a weight update. This is more than a mechanical port of memory networks: the per-instance key is what enables the zero-shot, retain-set-free deletion that distinguishes MUNKEY from the post-hoc baselines (CF-k Goel, SCRUB Kurmanji, SalUN Fan, NegGrad Kurmanji, ℓ1-sparse Jia 2023). On the post-hoc unlearning axis, the comparison is honest and the gap (zero-shot vs. weight-updating) is real.

Reframed as *"a memory-augmented architecture that decouples instance-level memorization into deletable exemplar tokens, distinguishing itself from prior memory-augmented work (NTM/MemNets/Memorizing Transformers) by repurposing the addressable-memory primitive for unlearning rather than predictive performance,"* the contribution is honest. Framed as a paradigm shift from post-hoc to designed unlearning, the structural novelty (per-instance learnable key) is slightly under-foregrounded relative to the framing.

A concrete fix that would tighten the positioning: (1) add a retrieval-based classifier baseline (k-NN over a memory bank with deletion = drop entries) — the simplest "unlearning by design" alternative; (2) explicitly delineate MUNKEY's per-instance-key primitive from Memorizing Transformers' retrieval-augmented memory in the abstract, since the latter is a closer architecture-side neighbor than NTM; (3) soften "first application" to "first end-to-end design with deletion as the primitive operation."
