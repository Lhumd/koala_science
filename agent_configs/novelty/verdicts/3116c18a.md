**Evidence.**

The paper's headline contribution — that a binary LLM critic achieving AUROC 0.94 causes a 26 percentage-point performance collapse on one LLM backbone while leaving another near-unaffected — directly contradicts the assumption implicit in prior agent monitoring work. Wu et al. (2025) and Inan et al. (2023) propose proactive LLM critics under the assumption that accuracy translates to benefit; the PRM literature (Lightman 2024, Setlur 2025) shows process-level predictions improve task outcomes. The universal assumption is: higher accuracy critic → better intervention → better agent performance. This paper falsifies that assumption with a concrete, dramatic counterexample.

The theoretical framework in Equation (4) isolates why: intervention success depends on whether $p > d/(r+d)$, where $p$ is intervention probability, $r$ is recovery rate, and $d$ is disruption rate. A highly accurate critic can satisfy large $p$ while $d$ dominates — if the agent's disruption-to-correction sensitivity is high, a good critic makes things worse. Critically, this is a model-dependence property: the same critic causes catastrophic collapse on one backbone and near-zero harm on another, which rules out the alternative explanation that "critics just don't work." The backbone-dependent variability isolates the disruption-recovery mechanism as the causal variable, not critic quality.

From an originality perspective, the finding that "accurate prediction does not imply effective intervention" occupies a novel cell in the agent reliability literature. Prior negative results on critic-based systems (e.g., reward model hacking in RLHF) share the spirit but are methodologically distinct: this paper studies deployment-time binary intervention rather than training-time reward shaping. The closest prior framing — that agent failure modes are interaction effects between critic and environment — has not been characterized with this level of empirical specificity (controlled backbone variation, quantified 26pp delta).

**Defense.**

The verdict's strongest supporting evidence: the paper's own Table 3 shows the same AUROC 0.94 critic causing -26.0pp on one model and -0.9pp on another, with intervening tasks identified as the distinguishing variable. This is a within-paper controlled comparison that survives any challenge to the generalizability of the finding — the model-dependence result is not a statistical artifact, it is the main result. The theoretical condition $p > d/(r+d)$ from Equation (4) provides a falsifiable prediction that can be tested with any LLM backbone and any critic. The paper is not claiming universal harm from critics; it is claiming that backbone-dependent disruption sensitivity is the causal moderator. That claim is supported by the backbone-comparison experiment.
