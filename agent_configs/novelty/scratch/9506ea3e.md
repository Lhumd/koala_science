## Paper: Robust and Efficient Zeroth-Order LLM Fine-Tuning via Adaptive Bayesian Subspace Optimizer (BSZO)

---

### Abstract (verbatim quote)
"We propose BSZO (Bayesian Subspace Zeroth-Order optimizer), which applies Kalman filtering to combine finite-difference information across multiple perturbation directions in a subspace. BSZO treats finite-difference measurements as noisy observations and derives a Bayesian posterior over the subspace-projected gradient. A residual-based adaptive mechanism adjusts the noise level automatically. Theoretical analysis shows a convergence rate improvement by k/γ over standard ZO. Empirically, BSZO achieves up to 6.67% absolute improvement on OPT-13B and is robust to fp16/bf16 precision."

### 4c. Pseudo-Novelty Pattern Check

- **Rebrand:** No. The Kalman filter / Bayesian linear regression framing for ZO gradient estimation is genuinely new vocabulary that enables new predictions (posterior mean vs. independent average; residual-based noise adaptation). Cannot describe the contribution in MeZO or LOZO vocabulary without losing the Bayesian formulation.

- **Domain transfer:** Partial — Kalman filtering is a well-known technique in signal processing and control theory, but applying it to ZO gradient estimation in LLM fine-tuning is a non-trivial transfer. The new domain (ZO optimization under memory constraints + fp16 instability) has domain-specific failure modes (numerical collapse, memory budget) that the paper explicitly addresses. This is more than mechanical application.

- **Composition without insight:** Partial — combines subspace ZO (from LOZO) + Bayesian linear regression (from Kalman filter literature). The combination is non-trivial: the Kalman posterior enables a closed-form update that propagates cross-direction correlation, which neither LOZO nor independent averaging can achieve. The residual-based adaptive noise is a non-obvious consequence of the Bayesian framing. Combination is not purely additive engineering.

- **Resolution bump:** No. The paper introduces a new methodological primitive (Kalman fusion of perturbations), not just a larger-scale replication of MeZO.

### 4d. Novel Framing vs. Novel Science

The Bayesian / Kalman framing does scientific work: it makes the following new predictions not available under the prior "average k perturbations independently" framing:
1. The posterior mean should outperform the empirical mean when noise is correlated across directions.
2. Residual-based noise adaptation should stabilize training under fp16/bf16 (the noise model can absorb quantization error).
3. The k/γ convergence improvement is a direct theoretical consequence of the Bayesian update (not of simply using k directions).

These predictions are tested in the paper. The framing is not purely vocabulary — it generates testable predictions.

### 4g. Positioning Issues

1. **Overclaim relative to evidence:** The abstract highlights "up to 6.67% absolute improvement on OPT-13B" as the headline result. However, on RoBERTa-large (the more standard benchmark), improvement over MeZO is only 0.24% (77.28 vs 77.04 average). The "up to" phrasing picks the best single number across model/task combinations. The contribution is real but the headline inflates the typical gain.
   - Verbatim abstract claim: "up to 6.67% absolute improvement"
   - Verbatim experimental scope: RoBERTa-large average improvement ~0.24%, OPT-13B SST-2 showing the largest gains

2. **Missing the natural ablation baseline:** The paper compares against MeZO, MeZO-Adam, HiZOO, LOZO — all existing methods. However, the paper does not compare BSZO against a simple "average k independent perturbation directions without Kalman fusion" baseline. This ablation would directly isolate whether the Bayesian fusion is necessary or whether simply using k directions is sufficient. Without this, the k/γ theoretical improvement cannot be empirically attributed specifically to the Bayesian formulation.

3. **Inflated framing:** Abstract claims BSZO is "Robust and Efficient" — the efficiency claim rests on the memory footprint being comparable to MeZO, but the k-direction approach necessarily costs more compute per step than single-direction MeZO. The "efficient" framing should be qualified.

4. **No buried contribution detected:** The Bayesian/Kalman method is front-and-center in the abstract and contributions list. The convergence analysis appears in the body but is consistent with the abstract framing.

### 4h. Originality Score Rationale

The strongest atomic claim (C1) is genuinely novel: treating ZO finite-difference measurements as noisy observations of a subspace gradient and fusing them via a Kalman/Gaussian posterior has not been done in the ZO-LLM literature. The closest neighbor (LOZO, Zhang 2025) introduces subspace projection but does not perform Bayesian fusion — the delta is precisely the Kalman update rule and the residual-based adaptive noise. Neighbor MeZO (Malladi 2023) uses single-direction ZO and is the primary memory-efficiency baseline; BSZO's multi-direction Bayesian fusion is a non-trivial extension.

The composition (subspace from LOZO + Kalman fusion) is not purely additive: the Kalman update enables fp16 stability via adaptive noise, which is a non-obvious consequence that LOZO + naive averaging would not achieve. The theoretical k/γ improvement follows from the Bayesian formulation, not from simply adding directions.

Penalty: the improvement on smaller models (RoBERTa) is modest (0.24%) and the expert-prediction test fires "no override" (direction predictable). The method is one-step removed from LOZO. Positioning is slightly inflated (headline picks the best single number).
