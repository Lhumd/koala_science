## 4b. Closest Prior Work

- **Neighbor 1:** AesBench (Huang et al., 2024b) — *"VideoAesBench is a direct extension of AesBench from image to video: same framework (holistic dimensions, multiple Q-types, LMM benchmarking) applied to the video modality; the 12 video dimensions expand AesBench's 8 image dimensions."*

- **Neighbor 2:** Q-Bench-Video (Zhang et al., 2025b) — *"Q-Bench-Video evaluates video perceptual quality (not aesthetic quality) with 4 dimensions and UGC/AIGC/CG video types; VideoAesBench differs by focusing on aesthetic quality and adding RGC/game types, expanding to 12 dimensions."*

- **Neighbor 3:** Q-Bench (Wu et al., 2023c) — *"Q-Bench is the image quality counterpart; VideoAesBench's relationship to Q-Bench parallels AesBench's relationship to Q-Bench — the aesthetic evaluation arm for the video modality."*

- **Neighbor 4:** VADB (Qiao et al., 2025) — *"VADB provides fine-grained aesthetics scores for ~10K videos, but is a regression dataset not an LMM benchmark; VideoAesBench transforms the aesthetics scoring problem into structured Q-A evaluation for LMMs."*

## 4c. Pseudo-Novelty Pattern Check

- **Rebrand:** No. The paper is genuinely proposing a new benchmark resource, not just renaming something.

- **Domain transfer:** YES. The clearest characterization of the contribution. AesBench (image aesthetics) → VideoAesBench (video aesthetics). The transfer includes two substantive additions: (a) temporal/motion-related content that has no image analog (RGC, game video types) and (b) new MC question format. The new domain (video) does have properties that images don't have, but the paper does not deeply address what makes video aesthetics fundamentally different from image aesthetics at a methodological level — it largely applies the same taxonomy-expansion approach. **Verdict: substantive domain transfer with evolutionary rather than revolutionary extension.**

- **Composition without insight:** Partial. The benchmark combines corpora from 10 existing video datasets (Table 2 lists all sources) with a Q-A annotation pipeline adapted from AesBench's methodology. The contribution is the annotation and curation effort, not a new theoretical insight about aesthetics. **Verdict: additive engineering of known annotation methodology applied to video.**

- **Resolution bump:** Partial. More content types (5 vs. 3), more questions (1,804 with MC), more dimensions (12 vs. 8). **Verdict: quantitative expansion of AesBench's framework.**

## 4d. Novel Framing vs. Novel Science

The "video aesthetics" framing is substantive because video has temporal properties (motion, duration, pacing) that image aesthetics frameworks cannot capture. However, the taxonomy (visual form / visual style / visual affectiveness) is adapted from the image aesthetics tradition with additive dimension expansion; the paper does not develop a new theory of what makes video aesthetics distinct from image aesthetics. The genuine novelty is primarily the resource (benchmark + results on 23 LMMs) rather than a new conceptual framework.

## 4g. Positioning Issues

1. **Overclaim relative to evidence:** The abstract claims this addresses "a fundamental ability for human" and the paper positions video aesthetics as "underexplored." Both are accurate framings. The claim that VideoAesBench is "the first comprehensive benchmark elaborately designed for evaluating the video aesthetics perception capabilities of LMMs" is technically accurate given the Table 1 comparison. No significant overclaim.

2. **Wrong reference class:** The paper claims to be "the first" video aesthetics LMM benchmark, but the comparison table (Table 1) shows Q-Bench-Video (2025) already evaluates video with 4 dimensions. The distinction hinges on "aesthetics vs. perceptual quality" — a genuine difference but one that should be articulated more precisely, as the boundary between perceptual quality and aesthetics is not sharp.

3. **Missing natural baseline:** The paper doesn't include a comparison to human annotator performance levels on the VideoAesBench questions. Without a human upper bound, the finding "current LMMs only contain basic ability" lacks a calibrated reference.

4. **Inflated framing words:** "The first comprehensive benchmark elaborately designed for evaluating the video aesthetics perception capabilities of LMMs." The "first" claim is plausible given the Table 1 comparison, but "comprehensive" is contested when the benchmark covers only 1,804 videos from 10 source datasets (vs. AesBench's 8,400 Q&A pairs).

5. **Buried real contribution:** No — the actual contribution (benchmark + 23-model evaluation) is clearly stated in the abstract and introduction.

## 4h. Originality Score Rationale

The strongest atomic novelty is the benchmark resource itself: VideoAesBench fills a genuine gap (no LMM video aesthetics benchmark existed), extends the AesBench framework to video with 4 additional dimensions, and provides the RGC/game video coverage that Q-Bench-Video lacks. The closest prior (AesBench) was image-only; VideoAesBench's extension to video is the one genuinely new axis. The method (annotation pipeline, Q-A creation) is identical to AesBench's methodology adapted for video. The experimental findings (closed-source > open-source, unbalanced performance) are predictable and match expectations from image aesthetics benchmarks.

For a benchmark contribution, this is a solid but incremental extension: domain transfer from image to video aesthetics, with evolutionary expansion of the taxonomy. The lack of a new evaluation methodology or theoretical framework limits the novelty.
