- **Neighbor 4: Zhao & Tresp 2019 (density-ratio replay prioritization)** — *"Zhao & Tresp use density-ratio estimation for replay prioritization to improve coverage; VLM-RB uses semantic scoring for task-relevance prioritization — different motivation (coverage vs. task-relevance), same pipeline slot (replay priority weight). A direct comparison would sharpen the contribution."*

## 4c. Pseudo-Novelty Pattern Check

- **Rebrand?** NO. "VLM-scored replay prioritization" is not a renaming of an existing concept. PER is TD-error prioritization; using a VLM semantic signal is a distinct mechanism. The new name ("VLM-RB") maps to a new technical operation.

- **Domain transfer?** PARTIAL. The paper applies a VLM (used elsewhere in RL for planning/exploration) to a new RL component (replay buffer). The new component has specific properties the paper addresses: the VLM must run asynchronously to avoid blocking the training loop, and must score sub-trajectories rather than single frames (a non-trivial design choice). So this is not purely mechanical transfer — there is method adaptation.

- **Composition without insight?** BORDERLINE. The composition is VLM + replay buffer prioritization. The key question is whether the combination produces non-obvious results. The result (VLM > TD-error for semantic task-relevance) is somewhat intuitive given the motivation — we already know VLMs have semantic understanding and TD error lacks it. However, the empirical scale of the gains (11–52%) and the demonstration that VLMs identify genuinely critical transitions (door-unlatching, grasping) that TD error systematically misses is substantive validation of an expected but unconfirmed hypothesis. Lean towards "expected insight, solid validation" rather than "non-obvious composition."

- **Resolution bump?** NO. The paper introduces a new mechanism, not a scale-up of an existing experiment.

**Dominant pattern: Domain transfer with non-trivial adaptation.** The conceptual move is applying VLMs to a previously unexplored RL component. The adaptation (async scoring, sub-trajectory scope) is non-trivial but secondary. The core insight (semantic > TD-error for semantic tasks) is intuitive once stated.

## 4d. Novel Framing vs. Novel Science

The paper introduces the framing "VLMs as replay buffer prioritizers." This framing does enable new experiments: ablations comparing VLM-only vs. TD-only vs. hybrid priority signals, and analysis of which transition types VLMs score differently from TD error. However, the framing does not generate new theoretical predictions — it is primarily an engineering hypothesis ("VLM semantic scoring will outperform TD-error for semantically structured tasks"). The framing is doing engineering work (opening a design space) but limited scientific work (new theoretical predictions). Overall: incremental framing contribution.

## 4g. Positioning Issues

- **Overclaim relative to evidence?** MINOR. Abstract says "replay buffer, a core component for storing and reusing experiences, remains unexplored" — this is defensible but sweeping. The claim is that VLM-based replay prioritization is unexplored, not that replay buffers generally are. The framing could be tightened from "replay buffer remains unexplored" to "semantic prioritization of replay buffers remains unexplored."

- **Wrong reference class?** PARTIAL. The paper compares primarily to PER (TD-error-based). A natural reference class would include other semantically-aware prioritization methods (Zhao & Tresp 2019 density-ratio) and other auxiliary-reward methods that use VLMs to reshape rewards (a different but closely related mechanism). Without this comparison, "11–52% better than PER" is measured against a weaker baseline than necessary.

- **Missing natural baseline?** YES. The most important missing comparison: **VLM as auxiliary reward signal** (adding VLM score as a shaped reward rather than as a replay priority weight). This would isolate whether the priority-weighting mechanism specifically matters vs. general VLM semantic supervision. Without this ablation, the gain is attributable to "any semantic signal helps" rather than "replay prioritization specifically helps."

- **Inflated framing words?** PRESENT but moderate. The paper uses "unexplored" and "gap" to describe replay buffer + VLM integration. These are defensible but slightly inflated given the broad VLM+RL literature. No "first to" claims detected. No "paradigm shift" language.

- **Buried real contribution?** NO. The main contribution (async VLM scorer, sub-trajectory scoring mechanism, priority weighting scheme) is in the abstract and introduction. No buried novelty detected in section headers.

## 4h. Originality Score Rationale

The paper's strongest novelty claim is the specific application site: using a frozen VLM to score sub-trajectories as priority signals in a replay buffer, filling a gap in the VLM+RL integration landscape that prior work (planning and exploration) left open. The closest prior is PER (Schaul 2015), whose entire architecture is retained with the TD-error signal swapped for a VLM semantic score — the delta is the priority signal source, not the buffer architecture. The method is novel as a combination but intuitive in motivation: given that TD error is semantically blind and VLMs have semantic understanding, the hypothesis is obvious in retrospect. The paper's value is empirical validation of this expected result at non-trivial scale (two domains, multiple tasks), plus the async engineering design. This places the contribution in the "real but moderate" range — it is not a rebrand, not purely a domain transfer without adaptation, but also not a counterintuitive or theoretically new contribution. The expert-prediction test found no counterintuition. Score: 5 (incremental, real, moderate novelty). Confidence: 4 (named PER and Liang/Zhang as key neighbors, delta is articulated, but full text retrieval of Zhao & Tresp not performed).
