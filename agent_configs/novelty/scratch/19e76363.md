### 4b. Closest Prior Work Identification

- **Neighbor 1: Themis (Li et al., 2024)** — "Tool-Augmented Reward Modeling." Established the foundational framework for equipping reward/judge models with tool calls (calculators, search engines, knowledge bases) during evaluation. Med-TIV's key delta over Themis: Med-TIV applies this paradigm in the medical domain AND, crucially, trains the verifier via iterative RL rather than relying on the tool-augmented inference setup alone. Themis does not include RL-based verifier training.

- **Neighbor 2: TIR-Judge (Xu et al., 2025)** — "Incentivizing agentic reasoning in LLM judges via tool-integrated reinforcement learning." The most direct prior work: trains LLM judges using RL + tool integration in the **general domain**, specifically for pairwise response evaluation (code execution for judging). Med-TIV is essentially TIR-Judge instantiated in the medical domain, replacing code-execution with medical search, and replacing pairwise evaluation with binary trace-level verification. The RL training paradigm and the tool-integration-during-training are shared. Delta: domain specialization (medical corpora, clinical reasoning traces), different tool type (medical search vs. code execution), and iterative curriculum formulation (TIR-Judge alternates SFT+RL; Med-TIV is pure iterative RL, though both use RL).

- **Neighbor 3: TIM-PRM (Kuang et al., 2025)** — "Verifying multimodal reasoning with tool-integrated PRM." Applies tool integration to process reward modeling for multi-modal reasoning. Med-TIV differs in being a binary outcome verifier (not PRM step-level), text-only (not multi-modal), and trained with RL (vs. TIM-PRM's SFT-based approach). Delta is real but modest: different reward granularity (outcome vs. process), different training method (RL vs. SFT).

- **Neighbor 4: Med-PRM (Yun et al., 2025)** — "Medical reasoning models with stepwise, guideline-verified process rewards." Direct in-domain medical reward model baseline. Med-PRM uses static RAG (retrieves docs upfront, fixes them in context); Med-TIV uses iterative dynamic retrieval during verification. Med-PRM is a PRM; Med-TIV is an ORM-style verifier. Delta on the retrieval axis is the paper's primary claimed contribution over Med-PRM.

- **Neighbor 5: Search-R1 / RL + search paradigm (Jin et al., 2025)** — "Training LLMs to reason and leverage search engines with reinforcement learning." Establishes RL training for search-augmented reasoning models. Med-TIV applies this recipe to the verifier role (instead of the generator) in the medical domain. Delta: Med-TIV is a *verifier*, not a *generator*, and the task is binary verification of someone else's trace rather than generating correct answers.

### 4c. Pseudo-Novelty Pattern Check

- **Rebrand:** No. Med-TIV is not just renaming something existing. There is a genuine architectural choice (iterative retrieval during verification, not just at inference time) and an RL training setup specific to verifier training. The combination is labeled as "Med-TIV" but the paper's related-work section accurately acknowledges its predecessors.

- **Domain transfer:** Partial YES. TIR-Judge (Xu et al., 2025) already trains LLM judges with RL + tool integration in the general domain. Med-TIV transfers this to the medical domain. The new domain does have relevant properties the paper addresses: (a) the need for authoritative medical corpora vs. code execution, (b) the clinical hallucination problem specific to medical facts, (c) trace-level binary verification vs. pairwise response scoring. The paper partially addresses these differences but the core training paradigm (RL + tool) is transferred rather than invented.

- **Composition without insight:** Partial YES. The system combines: (1) tool-augmented inference-time verification (from Themis/TIR-Judge), (2) iterative RL training (from Search-R1 / RL-Zero lineage), (3) curriculum-based difficulty filtering (standard in RL), (4) test-time Hard-Weighted Self-Consistency (standard). The combination is sensible engineering. The ablation (Q6) shows RL provides ~8-9% gain and tools provide ~1% further — suggesting the RL training is the dominant driver, and the tool novelty in the medical domain is marginal in terms of performance delta. The combination does not produce a strongly non-obvious result beyond what component contributions would predict.

- **Resolution bump:** No. The headline contribution is not a scale-up of a known experiment; there are genuine architectural choices.

### 4d. Novel Framing vs. Novel Science

The paper introduces "agentic verification" as the framing concept — the verifier acts as an agent that decides when and what to search. This framing is adopted from the broader "Agent-as-a-Judge" and TIR-Judge paradigms. The new framing does not make fundamentally new predictions not enabled by TIR-Judge's framing, but it does enable a new experimental question: can tool-augmented iterative RL training improve medical verifiers specifically? That specific question is unstudied. So the framing is largely inherited; the experimental contribution (applying this to medical, with iterative RL, with curriculum, specifically for verification) is the actual novel piece.

### 4e. Concurrent Work Calibration

TIR-Judge (Xu et al., 2025, October) and Med-TIV are temporally close. TIR-Judge was published ~Oct 2025; this paper is under review at ICML 2026. They are not concurrent — TIR-Judge precedes this work and is cited by it. The paper correctly acknowledges TIR-Judge as prior art. No concurrent work problem here; the delta is acknowledged but should be clearer.

### 4g. Positioning Issues

- **Overclaim relative to evidence:** The abstract claims Med-TIV "introduces an iterative RL paradigm" — but TIR-Judge (2025) already trains LLM judges using RL + tool integration. The paper's actual contribution is a domain-specific instantiation (medical) with a curriculum formulation mechanism. The framing "we introduce an iterative RL paradigm" overclaims when TIR-Judge already established this in the general domain. The distinction (pure iterative RL vs. TIR-Judge's SFT+RL alternation, and medical vs. general domain) is real but narrower than the abstract suggests.

- **Wrong reference class:** The paper compares against MedS3 and Med-PRM as primary baselines but not directly against TIR-Judge (the closest paradigmatic predecessor). This leaves the reader unable to assess how much the medical domain specialization contributes vs. the general RL+tool paradigm already established by TIR-Judge.

- **Missing the natural baseline:** TIR-Judge is the direct predecessor (RL + tool for judges) in the general domain. A TIR-Judge-style model fine-tuned on medical data would be the missing ablation to assess whether the curriculum formulation and iterative-only-RL approach (vs. TIR-Judge's SFT+RL) provide actual gains beyond domain adaptation.

- **Inflated framing words:** Abstract uses "rigorous verification," "principled path toward more reliable medical reasoning systems," and "novel tool-integrated verification framework." The word "novel" is used for a framework that is architecturally close to TIR-Judge. The claim "novel" is partially justified by the domain and curriculum mechanism, but risks overclaiming.

- **Buried real contribution:** The curriculum formulation mechanism (Section 3.2) — online filtering to retain only boundary-case instances with non-zero reward variance — is arguably the most technically distinctive contribution and is buried in the methodology section. The abstract highlights the tool integration more prominently. The curriculum mechanism is a clean, independently useful technique that could be extracted and applied to any RL-based verifier training.

### 4h. Originality Score Rationale

The strongest novelty in Med-TIV lies on the **method** axis: training a verifier using pure iterative RL (without SFT cold-start, without step-level labels) combined with tool-integrated evaluation and curriculum filtering. This is a non-trivial engineering achievement in the medical domain. However, the components are all prior art: the RL-for-judges-with-tools framework is TIR-Judge (2025); the curriculum filtering (boundary-case selection) is a known technique in RL training (RLVR literature); the iterative retrieval paradigm is from Themis/Search-R1.

The domain transfer (general → medical) is the primary contribution, and the paper does address medical-specific challenges (clinical corpora, hallucination in medical facts). But the ablation (Q6) reveals that RL contributes ~8-9% accuracy gain while tool integration contributes only ~1% — suggesting the "iterative tool use" framing in the title somewhat overstates the tool's contribution relative to the RL training effect.

The closest prior (TIR-Judge) is explicitly cited, and the paper honestly notes TIR-Judge in related work. The delta (medical domain + iterative RL instead of SFT+RL alternation + curriculum formulation) is real but incremental. The curriculum formulation is the most distinctive piece, but it is presented as supporting infrastructure rather than as the headline contribution.

Score: **5** (real but moderate incremental contribution — extends TIR-Judge paradigm to medical domain with curriculum mechanism; domain challenges partially addressed; RL training dominates the gain and the tool-augmentation novelty is marginal in terms of ablation evidence).
