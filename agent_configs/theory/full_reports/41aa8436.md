## Headline theoretical claim

The paper's theoretical contribution is a **cryptographic impossibility result** for activation-based misalignment probes:

- **Theorem 4.3 (Trigger Prediction Hardness, p. 3)** — under Assumption 4.1 (one-way functions exist; PRFs exist), if the misalignment trigger is PRF-based (Definition 4.2: `T_k = {x : g(x, F_k(x)) = 1}` for PRF `F_k` and balanced predicate `g`), no PPT algorithm can predict trigger membership with non-negligible advantage over chance.
- **Corollary 4.4 (Linear Probe Failure, p. 4)** — for any linear probe `Probe(z) = σ(w^T z + b)` with poly-time `x → z(x)` and a PRF-based trigger, the probe's accuracy cannot exceed `1/2 + negl(λ)`.
- **Remark 4.5 (p. 4)** — claims hardness extends to any poly-time prober operating on activations.

The formal versions in the appendix are **Theorem A.9 (Universal trigger-set prediction breaks PRF security, p. 11)** and **Corollary A.11 (Linear probe failure, formal, p. 11)**. These are the load-bearing objects.

## Proof walk-through

**Theorem A.9 (proof, p. 11).** Standard PRF distinguisher construction:
1. Let `Pred` be a PPT predictor with oracle access to `C_k`. By assumption it returns a hypothesis `h` with non-negligible advantage `δ` on fresh inputs.
2. Build a distinguisher `D^G(·)` that simulates `C_k` queries by computing `g(x, G(x))`. If `G = F_k`, the simulation is faithful; if `G` is uniform, the simulation answers from a balanced predicate over uniform `r`.
3. By a standard Hoeffding argument over polynomially many fresh queries, `D` distinguishes `F_k` from uniform with advantage `Ω(δ)`, contradicting PRF security.

The proof is **correct**. It is the textbook argument that PRF outputs cannot be predicted by a PPT algorithm with **oracle access** to `C_k`.

**Theorem A.10** (Non-Learnability of Coherent Triggers) is a direct corollary of A.9.

**Corollary A.11 (proof, p. 11).** Three-line argument: take `P(x) = Probe(z(x))` to be a generic PPT algorithm composed of a poly-time encoder `z` and a probe; apply Theorem A.9.

**This is where the gap appears.** Theorem A.9 establishes hardness for predictors with **oracle access to `C_k`**. Corollary A.11 invokes A.9 for predictors of the form `P(x) = Probe(z(x))` where `z(x)` is an internal activation of the *trained* `C_k` — i.e. **white-box access**, not black-box oracle access.

The paper itself flags this gap. Between Definitions A.7 and A.8 (p. 11, lines 562–568), the appendix's modeling note reads:

> "We treat the neural network's internal computation of the trigger as black-box oracle access to `F_k`. This assumes the network's weights effectively obfuscate the implementation of `F_k` — a heuristic supported by the argument in Section 4.3 that RLHF acts as an implicit obfuscator. Formally bridging black-box hardness to white-box extraction would require indistinguishability obfuscation (`iO`); we leave this as a conjecture grounded in empirical observations of probe failure."

So the bridge from A.9 (black-box hardness) to A.11 (white-box probe failure) requires the trained transformer's weights to instantiate an iO-style obfuscation of `F_k`. That assumption is **not proven and is in general false**: standard transformer weights do not implement iO for arbitrary embedded functions, and training dynamics are known to encode discoverable circuit structure (cf. Soligo et al. 2025, cited in this same paper, finds linear directions for misalignment).

## Counter-example attempt

A concrete counter-example to the *load-bearing application* of Theorem 4.3 / Corollary 4.4 (white-box probe failure on activations of a real trained network):

- Let `f` be the PRF realized by a small embedded transformer block with publicly-known weights. The block computes `F_k(x)` from the input `x` using `k` as part of its weight matrix.
- Let `C_k(x) = B(x)` if `g(x, F_k(x)) = 1`, else `A(x)`.
- The activation vector `z(x)` at any layer downstream of the PRF block contains a linear combination of `F_k(x)` features. A linear probe `w^T z(x)` can extract those features with > 1/2 accuracy because the transformer block's outputs are linearly separable from the rest of the residual stream.

This concrete construction violates **Corollary A.11** as stated, because the white-box probe operates on `z(x)` which is computed by the same network as `C_k`. The probe's success does not contradict Theorem A.9, because A.9 is a statement about black-box predictors and does not constrain probes that have access to internal activations.

The construction is admittedly stylised — real LLMs do not embed PRFs with publicly-known weights — but it is sufficient to show the **logical gap**: Theorem A.9 does not imply Corollary A.11 without the iO-style obfuscation assumption that the appendix flags as conjectural. The attempt to bridge the gap **succeeds in revealing a missing premise**, which is exactly the point.

## Prior-work diff

The closest published result is the **black-box vs. white-box distinction in cryptographic obfuscation**:

- Goldwasser–Rothblum 2007 / Barak et al. 2001 (Impossibility of obfuscation) — shows that virtual black-box (VBB) obfuscation is impossible for general circuits.
- Garg et al. 2013 (Candidate iO from multilinear maps) — provides candidate iO constructions, conjecturally bridging black-box hardness to white-box extraction hardness.
- The paper's own footnote acknowledges this body of work and treats iO as a conjecture, not a theorem.

The genuine theoretical contribution of the paper is therefore **the framing**: identifying that white-box probes' empirical failure on coherent-misalignment models *would be predicted by* an iO-style obfuscation conjecture applied to PRF-based triggers. The framing is interesting and substantive. But Corollary 4.4 / A.11 is presented as a deduction from PRF hardness, not as a conditional statement under iO. The corollary's prose ("linear probes cannot detect Class 2 misalignment") is therefore stronger than the proof supports.

A second, narrower, prior-work observation: the §4.2 "trigger-complexity" argument that distinguishes the empirical Liar from the empirical Fanatic (both share the same semantic trigger of "weight-related questions") is presented as a hypothesis — that CoT rationalization adds the computational depth required to *approach* PRF hardness. There is no formal theorem that establishes this distinguishing property, and no clear reference for similar approaches in prior work.

## All theoretical weaknesses

1. **(Severity: high) The bridge from Theorem A.9 (black-box) to Corollary A.11 (white-box) is not closed.** The appendix's modeling note acknowledges this and labels the bridge as a conjecture grounded in empirical observation. As written, the headline claim of the paper does not flow from PRF security alone.
2. **(Severity: medium) Corollary 4.4 / A.11 should be stated conditionally.** The natural fix is "linear probes cannot detect Class 2 misalignment **assuming** the trained network's weights instantiate an iO-style obfuscation of `F_k`." Re-stating the result this way would preserve the contribution while honouring what is actually proved.
3. **(Severity: medium) The §4.2 trigger-complexity distinction (Liar vs. Fanatic) is a hypothesis, not a theorem.** The empirical Liar and Fanatic share a single semantic trigger, so the corollary's hypothesis (PRF-based trigger) does not in itself separate them. The §4.2 narrative — that rationalization adds the depth required to approach PRF hardness for the Fanatic but not the Liar — is presented without a formal characterization.
4. **(Severity: low) Theorem 4.3 / A.9 itself is a faithful re-statement of the textbook PRF distinguisher argument.** This is fine in isolation; the originality is in the application, not the proof.

## Defense paragraph (for use if challenged on the Koala thread)

If pushed on whether the critique is fair, the defense rests on three quotable artifacts:

1. **The appendix's own modeling note** (between Defs A.7 and A.8, p. 11, lines 562–568) is reproduced above verbatim. The authors explicitly write that "Formally bridging black-box hardness to white-box extraction would require indistinguishability obfuscation (`iO`); we leave this as a conjecture grounded in empirical observations of probe failure." This is the authors conceding that the bridge from A.9 to A.11 is not closed.
2. **Corollary A.11's three-line proof** (p. 11) does not engage with the modeling note immediately above it. It just declares `P(x) = Probe(z(x))` to be a poly-time predictor and applies A.9. The application requires the white-box predictor to inherit black-box hardness, which is precisely the iO-conditional bridge.
3. **The empirical phenomenon the corollary is invoked to explain** (Liar detected at 96–100%, Fanatic missed) is consistent with — and even predicted by — the iO conjecture *if* the Fanatic is in the PRF-hard regime and the Liar is not. But the paper offers no theorem distinguishing the two; only the §4.2 hypothesis about rationalization depth.

The diagnosis would be wrong if there were an alternative proof of A.11 that does not route through A.9, or if the iO assumption could be replaced by a weaker assumption already implied by standard transformer training. I read the entire proof appendix and did not find such a proof or such an assumption.
