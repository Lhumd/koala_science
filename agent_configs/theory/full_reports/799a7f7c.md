## Theorem / lemma / definition inventory

- **Definition 4.1** (Reward-Aligned Distributions): $dD^\pm_{(r,\theta)} \propto [\pm(r(x,y) - \mu^\theta_r(x))]_+ \cdot e^{\pm r(x,y)} dP(x)$. Note the $[\cdot]_+$ indicator: $D^+$ is supported on $\{y : r(x,y) > \mu^\theta_r(x)\}$, $D^-$ on the complement. **The two distributions are by construction disjoint (mutually singular).**
- **Assumption 3.1**: $p^\pm(y|x) \propto \exp(\pm r(x,y))$. Stated as motivation only.
- **Assumption 4.2** (weak): $p^+(y|x)$ non-decreasing in $r(x,y)$, $p^-(y|x)$ non-increasing. Claimed as the "operative" assumption for the main theorem.
- **Theorem 4.3**: Three claims about f-HAL and f-GRPO under $G \to \infty$ (almost surely):
  - Divergence Estimation: $-L^{(f,g)}_{fGRPO}(\theta^{(t+1)}_{RL}) \propto D_f(D^+_{(r,\theta^{(t)}_{RL})} \| D^-_{(r,\theta^{(t)}_{RL})}) = f'_\infty$.
  - Alignment Consistency: $\pi^{(t+1)}_\theta(y|x) \propto \pi_{ref}(y|x) \exp(\beta^{-1} h_t(x,y))$ with $h_t$ as specified.
  - Average Reward Improvement: $\mu^{\theta_{ref}}_r(x) < \mu^{\theta^{(t)}}_r(x)$ for all $t > 0$; if $g^{-1}(f'_\infty) = \infty$, strict iteration-wise improvement until reaching $\max_y r(x,y)$.
- **Theorem 4.4**: For unclipped GRPO ($\epsilon \to \infty$) as $G \to \infty$, $\pi^{(t+1)}_\theta(y|x) \propto \pi_{ref}(y|x) \exp(\beta^{-1} a_t(x,y))$ with $a_t$ standardized advantage; $\mu^{\theta^{(t)}_{GRPO}}_r > \mu^{\theta_{ref}}_r$ for all $t > 0$.
- **Lemma A.3** (Variational rep of f-divergence): $D_f(P\|Q) = \sup_T \{E_P[T] - E_Q[f^*(T)]\}$, with optimum at $T^* = f'(dP_{ac}/dQ)$ on supp($P_{ac}$) and $f'_\infty$ on the singular part.
- **Lemma B.1** (Comonotone Covariance): $\text{Cov}(f(X), g(X)) \geq 0$ when $f, g$ both monotone in same direction. Standard.
- **Lemma B.2** (Monotone Reweighting Increases Mean): For nonneg weight $w_x$, $E_{q_w}[r] - E_q[r] = \text{Cov}_q(r, w_x)/E_q[w_x]$; if $w_x$ monotone in $r$ then $E_{q_w}[r] \geq E_q[r]$.

## Load-bearing technical pieces

- **Importance-weight estimator (Eq. 12)**: $\hat{w}^\pm_i = \vec{\sigma}_i(\{\pm r_j - \ln\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_j|x)\}) \cdot \mathbb{1}_{\{\pm a_i > 0\}}$. The $\vec{\sigma}_i(\cdot)$ is softmax over the $G$ samples.
- **Plug-in for $p^\pm$ (Eq. 11)**: $\hat{p}^\pm(y_i|x) = \exp(\pm r_i) / \sum_j \exp(\pm r_j - \ln\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_j|x))$. Uses Bengio & Senécal's Log-Q correction. This is consistent (by SLLN) only as $G \to \infty$.
- **FP iteration (Eq. 17)**: $\theta^{(t)} = \arg\inf L(\theta)$ s.t. $\theta_{old} = \theta^{(t-1)}$. The "iterate" is the *exact minimizer* given the old policy — not the gradient-descent iterate the algorithm runs in practice.

## Theory score rationale (theory grounds only)

The paper has three formal results: Theorem 4.3 (f-GRPO/f-HAL divergence-estimation + alignment consistency + reward improvement), Theorem 4.4 (unclipped GRPO behavior), and the supporting Lemmas A.3, B.1, B.2. The lemmas are textbook material executed correctly. The two theorems are technically correct under the actually-used assumptions, but the framing has three real concerns:

1. **The "divergence estimation" interpretation of f-GRPO is structurally vacuous (Thm 4.3 part 1).** By Definition 4.1, $D^+_{(r,\theta)}$ is supported on $\{y : r(x,y) > \mu^\theta_r(x)\}$ and $D^-_{(r,\theta)}$ on the complement. The two measures are by construction mutually singular. The f-divergence between mutually singular measures is the constant $f(0) + f'_\infty$, depending only on the choice of $f$ — not on the policy, not on the reward, not on anything the algorithm controls. The proof itself states "the divergence is just... = $f'_\infty$." So saying "f-GRPO is a divergence estimator that estimates an f-divergence between above-average and below-average reward distributions" is technically true but trivially so: the estimated divergence is a constant, and there is no estimation problem in any meaningful sense. The actual content of f-GRPO is in the optimization of a variational *lower bound* tied to the importance-weight construction (Eq. 12), not in the estimation of any non-trivial divergence quantity. The hybrid f-HAL ($\lambda \in (0,1)$) does have a non-trivial divergence-estimation interpretation because the mixture distributions $M^\pm$ overlap, but this is a special-case rescue, not the principled extension promised by the framework.

2. **The stated assumption of Theorem 4.3 (Assumption 4.2, weak monotonicity) is not the assumption actually used in the proof (Assumption 3.1, explicit exponential form).** The proof's convergence step "$\vec{\sigma}_i(\{\pm r_j - \ln\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_j|x)\}) \to dQ^\pm/\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_i|x)$, where $dQ^\pm \propto \exp(\pm r(x,y))$" pins down the explicit functional form. This is exactly Assumption 3.1, which the paper says is used "solely to motivate" the framework. The downstream alignment-consistency claim *does* benefit from monotonicity (Lemma B.2 needs only monotone $w$), but the divergence-estimation conclusion uses the explicit form. The paper should either state Theorem 4.3 under Assumption 3.1 (the form actually used) or restate the divergence-estimation conclusion to depend only on the monotonicity assumption.

3. **The "iterate-wise strict reward improvement up to $\arg\max_y r(x,y)$" claim (Thm 4.3 last sentence) requires both $G \to \infty$ AND exact FP iteration**. The FP iteration $\theta^{(t)} = \arg\inf_\theta L(\theta)$ (Eq. 17) is an exact minimization step; the actual algorithm (Algorithm 1) does one gradient step per iterate. The paper does not characterize how far the gradient-step iterate is from the FP iterate, nor how the support-shrinking argument behaves under inexact minimization. Combined with the practical $G = 4$ in the experiments (Table 7), the gap between the asymptotic theorem and the algorithm is large and unanalyzed.

The Appendix A/B algebra is clean and the lemmas are correct. The overall theoretical contribution is real (the FDO-to-on-policy extension is a non-trivial methodological move) but the central "divergence estimation" framing significantly overstates what the proofs deliver in the f-GRPO case.

**Score: 5.** Proofs are technically correct; methodological extension is real; but the central theoretical claim (f-GRPO as a divergence estimator) is structurally trivial because the constructed distributions are mutually singular by construction, and the strict-improvement claim relies on idealizations (G → ∞, exact FP iteration) far from the practical algorithm. Sits in "weak but defensible — proof technically correct but novelty marginal, scope of theorem narrower than the prose suggests."
