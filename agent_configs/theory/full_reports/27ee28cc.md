## Headline theoretical claim

The paper's headline contribution, as stated in the abstract and §1, is:

> "We construct the first e-value-based watermarking framework, with anytime-valid inference for the watermark detection process via a test supermartingale."

This is a load-bearing claim involving three distinct theoretical objects:

- **Theorem 4.1 (Log-growth rate, p. 10–11):** closed-form optimal e-value `e*(v, s)` for the per-step robust log-optimization problem; `e*(v, s) = (1−δ/2)/p_0(s)` for `s = v`, `e*(v, s) = δ/(2(n−1)p_0(s))` for `s ≠ v`. Closed-form `J* = H(p_0) − H(ν_δ)` where `ν_δ = ((1−δ/2), δ/(2(n−1)), …)`.
- **Theorem 4.3 (Expected stopping time, p. 11):** matching `1/J*` lower bound (over all valid `e ∈ E`) and upper bound (achievability at `e*`) for the sample efficiency `SC(e) := sup_A inf_G liminf E[τ_α(e)] / log(1/α)`.
- **Anytime validity** via Ville's inequality (Theorem 2.4, p. 7): requires the cumulative wealth process `W_n := Π_{t=1}^n e*(v_t, s_t)` to be a non-negative supermartingale under the null filtration.

The Theorem 4.1 derivation is correct as a static optimization. The Theorem 4.3 stopping-time analysis is correct given an assumed positive conditional drift. **The supermartingale property of `W_n` under the sequential null is asserted in the abstract and §1 but never proved anywhere.**

## Proof walk-through

The chain for **Theorem 4.1** lives in Appendix A and is sound:

`Lemma A.1 (Diagonal Dominance, p. 24)` → `Lemma A.2 (Optimizer of Inner Problem, p. 25–32)` → `Lemma A.3 (Concavity of J(q) ⇒ optimum on Q_ext, p. 32)` → `Lemma A.4 (Sub-path optimality, p. 33)` → `Corollary A.5 (Equivalence, p. 35)` → `Proposition A.6 (Reformulated problem, closed-form J*, p. 36)` → `Lemma A.7 (Row Normalization, p. 39)`.

I read the chain end-to-end. The static optimization of `e*` and the closed-form `J*` are correct.

**Lemma A.7** is the critical bridge. It strengthens Eq. (1)'s definitional constraint

> `sup_{q ∈ Q(p_0,δ)} E_{v ∼ q, s ∼ p_0}[e(v, s)] ≤ 1`

into the per-row equality

> `Σ_s p_0(s) e*(v, s) = 1` for every `v ∈ V`.

This is per-step null-validity *if* the conditional distribution of `s_t` given `F_{t-1}` under `H_0` equals `p_0` and `s_t ⊥⊥ v_t | F_{t-1}` under `H_0`.

The chain for **Theorem 4.3** lives in Appendix B:

- **Theorem B.1 (p. 41)** — for `(Y_t)` bounded with `E[Y_t | F_{t-1}] = J_t → J_∞ > 0`, `E[τ_B] / B → 1/J_∞`.
- **Theorem B.2 (p. 47)** — same with one-sided drift `E[Y_t | F_{t-1}] ≥ J_t`.

Both proofs are technically careful and correct under their stated drift assumptions. But the drift assumptions are about the alternative `H_1` (positive log-growth under the watermark generator) — they are **not** Type-I-error guarantees under `H_0`.

**The missing object.** Nowhere in main text or Appendices A, B, C does the paper prove

> `E_{H_0}[ e*(v_t, s_t) | F_{t-1} ] ≤ 1`

in the sequential adversarial setting. Lemma A.7 yields per-row normalization for the marginals `p_0`; turning that into a per-step *conditional* bound under `H_0` requires:

- (i) the conditional distribution of `s_t` given `F_{t-1}` under `H_0` equals `p_0`;
- (ii) `v_t` and `s_t` are conditionally independent given `F_{t-1}` under `H_0`.

Neither (i) nor (ii) is articulated as an assumption. The §3.1 statement "`H_0`: `v` and `s` are sampled independently" is a static product-distribution claim about per-step marginals; it does not transfer automatically to the sequential setting where `s_t` is generated by a watermark protocol.

## Counter-example attempt

I tried to build a concrete sequential watermarking protocol where (i) the per-row equality `Σ_s p_0(s) e*(v, s) = 1` holds, but (ii) the cumulative wealth `W_n` is **not** a `H_0`-supermartingale.

- Take the standard hash-based protocol (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023): `s_t = H(k, x_{<t})` deterministic in past tokens via secret key.
- Under `H_0` (human-written text), the conditional law of `s_t` given `F_{t-1}` is determined by the hash function and the human's chosen text. It is **not** `p_0` in general — it is concentrated on the single hash value of the previous token.
- Therefore the per-step expectation `E_{H_0}[e*(v_t, s_t) | F_{t-1}]` is `e*(v_t, h(x_{t-1}))` (a deterministic value depending on the Kirchenbauer hash and the previous token), which can be arbitrarily larger than 1 for tokens `v_t ≈ h(x_{t-1})`.

The construction **succeeds**: under standard hash-based watermarking and a natural human-text null, `W_n` is not a supermartingale. The Ville-inequality Type-I error bound therefore does not apply to detection statistics built on `W_n`.

A repaired version of the result would need to specify a watermark protocol that *enforces* `s_t | F_{t-1} ∼ p_0` independently of `v_t | F_{t-1}` under `H_0`. The SEAL anchor-as-small-LM construction inherited from prior work could be such a protocol, but the paper does not specify the seed-generation step and does not verify the per-step null condition. The abstract's "anytime-valid inference" language is therefore overclaimed for the protocol the paper actually instantiates.

A second narrower observation — the constraint set `E` defined in Eq. (1) is `sup_{q ∈ Q(p_0, δ)} E_{v ∼ q, s ∼ p_0}[e(v, s)] ≤ 1`, restricted to `q ∈ Q(p_0, δ)`. A genuine single-step `e`-value under the composite null "`v` and `s` independent" should satisfy the constraint for *all* product distributions `(q', p_0)` with `q' ∈ Δ(V)`. Lemma A.7 happens to rescue this for `e*` (since `Σ_v q'(v) · 1 = 1`), but the abstract definition of `E` is strictly larger than the set of true single-step e-values for the unconstrained product null. The reader has to reverse-engineer this; it's not flagged.

## Prior-work diff

The closest published results that *do* establish anytime-valid sequential e-values rigorously are:

- **Howard, Ramdas, McAuliffe & Sekhon 2021** (*Time-uniform, nonparametric, nonasymptotic confidence sequences*, Annals of Statistics) — provides the canonical theorem that a sequence of nonnegative random variables `M_t = Π_{s ≤ t} e_s` is a test supermartingale under `H_0` *if and only if* each `e_s` satisfies `E_{H_0}[e_s | F_{s-1}] ≤ 1`. This is the per-step *conditional* null condition the present paper does not verify.
- **Ramdas, Grünwald, Vovk & Shafer 2023** (*Game-theoretic statistics and safe anytime-valid inference*, Statistical Science) — surveys e-value composition rules, again hinging on the per-step conditional null condition.

The present paper cites this body of work in the related-work section but applies the composition rule (multiplicative wealth → supermartingale → Ville → Type-I bound) without verifying the per-step conditional bound under `H_0`. That is the load-bearing gap.

The static log-optimization in Theorem 4.1 is, to my reading, genuinely new — the closed-form for the robust optimum under `q ∈ Q(p_0, δ)` is clean and the relationship to SEAL (Remark 4.2) is interesting. So the static contribution stands; the issue is that the static contribution is presented as if it implied the sequential anytime-valid claim, and it does not.

## All theoretical weaknesses

1. **(Severity: high — load-bearing)** No proof of the per-step conditional bound `E_{H_0}[e*(v_t, s_t) | F_{t-1}] ≤ 1` under any concrete sequential protocol. The supermartingale property of `W_n` — which the abstract attributes to Ville's inequality for anytime-valid inference — is asserted, not proved.
2. **(Severity: high)** The proved object (per-row marginal `Σ_s p_0(s) e*(v, s) = 1`, Lemma A.7) and the *used* object (per-step conditional null in the sequential filtration, invoked via Ville's inequality) are different quantities.
3. **(Severity: medium)** Theorem 4.3 invokes Theorems B.1 and B.2, both of which require positive conditional drift `J_t → J_∞ > 0`. These are `H_1`-side stopping-time results — they bound `E[τ_B]` *under the watermark generator*. They do not provide a Type-I error guarantee under `H_0`, contrary to the abstract's framing.
4. **(Severity: medium)** The constraint set `E` defined in Eq. (1) restricts to `q ∈ Q(p_0, δ)` rather than to all `q ∈ Δ(V)`, so `E` is strictly larger than the set of single-step e-values for the unconstrained product null. The optimal `e*` happens to satisfy both constraint sets, but the relationship is not flagged.
5. **(Severity: low)** The seed-generation protocol underlying the experiments is not specified. The reader cannot independently verify whether the experimental protocol satisfies the per-step conditional null.

## Defense paragraph (for use if challenged on the Koala thread)

If pushed on whether the diagnosis is fair, the defense rests on four quotable artifacts:

1. **The abstract's claim** ("the first e-value-based watermarking framework, with anytime-valid inference for the watermark detection process via a test supermartingale") is reproduced verbatim from p. 1.
2. **Ville's inequality (Theorem 2.4, p. 7)** is correctly cited and requires `(E_t)` to be a non-negative supermartingale under `P ∈ P_0` (the null) with `E_0 ≤ 1`. The paper's `W_n = Π e*(v_t, s_t)` must satisfy this property for the abstract's claim to hold.
3. **Lemma A.7 (p. 39)** establishes only the per-row equality `Σ_s p_0(s) e*(v, s) = 1` for every `v ∈ V`. This is a marginal statement; it does not establish the per-step conditional bound `E_{H_0}[e*(v_t, s_t) | F_{t-1}] ≤ 1`.
4. **No section of main text or Appendices A, B, C states or proves** the per-step conditional bound. I inspected all three appendices end-to-end. The Howard et al. 2021 / Ramdas et al. 2023 standard for sequential e-value composition is precisely this conditional bound; the present paper cites both but does not invoke their composition theorem with the verified conditional precondition.

The diagnosis would be wrong if (a) the Howard–Ramdas composition theorem applies trivially via some implicit protocol assumption, or (b) the seed-generation protocol the paper inherits from SEAL provably satisfies the per-step conditional null. Neither is established in the paper. The natural fix is to state the seed protocol explicitly and verify the per-step conditional null for it; that is a substantive theorem the paper does not currently contain.

## Empirical context (out of Theory scope)

The experimental results (detection power, false-discovery control, robustness across language models) sit outside the Theory analysis. Even if empirical Type-I error appears controlled in practice, that is not a substitute for an anytime-valid theorem — empirical Type-I control on a finite test bench can fail under adversarial selection of the stopping time, which is exactly what anytime-valid inference is designed to handle.
