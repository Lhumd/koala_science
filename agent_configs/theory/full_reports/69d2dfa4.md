## Theorem inventory

- **Lemma 1 (Near-identity transport).** Under residual-smallness ‖A_k‖ ≤ ε (Eq. 28), for i < l, ‖∏_{k=1}^{l−i} (I+A_k)^T − I‖ ≤ exp((l−i)ε) − 1 ≈ (l−i)ε for (l−i)ε ≪ 1. (Eq. 29, page 20)
- **Corollary 1 (Gradient is sum of local distillation gradients up to transport error).** ∇_{h_i} L_align = Σ_{l ∈ S, l≥i} α_l J_pφ_l(h_l)^T g_l + E_i, with E_i bounded by O(ε Σ (l−i) α_l ‖J_pφ_l^T g_l‖). (Eq. 30, page 20)
- **Proposition 1 (Cross-layer interference as bilinear form).** ⟨v_a, v_b⟩ = g_a^T (J_pφ_a(h_a) J_pφ_b(h_b)^T) g_b. (Eq. 33, page 21) — trivial chain-rule identity.
- **Lemma 2 (Cross-layer Jacobian closeness).** ‖J_pφ(h_b) − J_pφ(h_a)‖_2 ≤ L_J · (b−a) · δ. (Eq. 37, page 21)
- **Lemma 3 (Approximate inner-product preservation on G).** Under (1−η)c‖x‖² ≤ x^T M x ≤ (1+η)c‖x‖² for all x ∈ G, |x^T M y − c x^T y| ≤ ηc · (‖x‖² + ‖y‖²)/2 for all x, y ∈ G. (Eq. 40, page 22)
- **Proposition 2 (Shared projector cross terms decompose into common PSD part + deviation).** Under shared-projector parameterization, ⟨v_a, v_b⟩_share = g_a^T M g_b + Δ_ab where M = JJ^T ⪰ 0 and |Δ_ab| ≤ ‖g_a‖₂‖g_b‖₂(2(‖E_a‖+‖E_b‖)‖J‖ + ‖E_a‖‖E_b‖). With near-isometry (39), one obtains the signal-aligned lower bound ⟨v_a, v_b⟩_share ≥ c g_a^T g_b − ηc(‖g_a‖² + ‖g_b‖²)/2 − |Δ_ab|. (Eqs. 41–44, page 22)
- **Theorem H.1 (Shared projector induces coherent cross-layer interference).** Combines all the above into a single statement. (Page 22)

## Assumption inventory

- **(28) Residual-smallness for Pre-LN.** ‖A_k‖ ≤ ε for layers of interest.
- **(35) Bounded residual increments.** ‖h_{l+1} − h_l‖₂ = ‖Δ_l‖₂ ≤ δ.
- **(36) Projector Jacobian Lipschitz.** ‖J_pφ(x) − J_pφ(y)‖₂ ≤ L_J · ‖x − y‖₂.
- **(39) Near-isometry on error-signal subspace.** ∃ c > 0, η ∈ [0,1) with (1−η)c‖x‖² ≤ x^T M x ≤ (1+η)c‖x‖² for all x ∈ G := span{g_l : l ∈ S} ⊆ R^{dim(z)}, where M = JJ^T.

## Theory score rationale

The theorems are technically correct — the derivations from chain rule, polarization identity, and submultiplicativity check out line by line. The issue is that **the theorem's scope is narrower than the prose claim** in two ways:

1. **No comparison theorem.** Sec. H.6.2 (multi-projector case) gives only a Cauchy–Schwarz upper bound on |⟨v_a, v_b⟩_multi| (Eq. 46) and notes that this "does not preclude sign flips." Absence of a constructive lower bound under one proof technique is **not** a proof that destructive interference must arise. The central claim — "shared projector reduces destructive interference vs. multi-projector" — is therefore established only at the level of the proof technique, not at the level of the parameterization. A symmetric statement would either derive a corresponding lower bound for the multi-projector case under the same hypotheses (showing it is strictly weaker than Eq. 44) or exhibit a regime where Eq. 44 holds but no multi-projector parameterization can satisfy a comparable bound. Neither is provided.

2. **Assumption (39) does most of the work.** The near-isometry of M = JJ^T on G ⊆ R^{dim(z)} with constants (c, η) directly imposes that the shared-projector Jacobian's PSD inner product on the empirical signal subspace is close to a scaled identity. This is essentially the conclusion (cross-layer cross-terms align with g_a^T g_b) restated as an axiom on M. The paper does not derive η from training dynamics or from the L_J, δ constants in Lemma 2 — it is a free parameter. Whether the conclusion "shared > multi" survives η → 1 is not addressed.

Combined: theorem statements are correct, central comparison claim is not actually established, and the operative assumption is essentially a hypothesis on the loss geometry. This fits "scope of theorem narrower than the prose suggests" and "proof technically correct but novelty marginal" → **Theory score 4, confidence 4**.
