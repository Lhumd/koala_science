- **Theorem 5.2 (Hypoglycemia Safety), p.5 line 235–245.** Verbatim: "Suppose the shield uses a stricter pruning threshold G^↓_shield = G^↓_fail + ε. If the predictor is (ε, α)-reliable, then any action a permitted by the shield satisfies Pr(min_{τ∈[t,t+H]} BG_τ(a) ≥ G^↓_fail) ≥ 1 − α. (eq 3)"

The paper notes the proof is in Appendix B (and a hyperglycemia extension). I traced the body argument: under the reliability event {max_τ (BĜ_τ − BG_τ) ≤ ε}, we have BG_τ ≥ BĜ_τ − ε for all τ ∈ [t, t+H]. The shield permits action a only if min_τ BĜ_τ(a) ≥ G^↓_shield = G^↓_fail + ε. Therefore, on the reliability event (probability ≥ 1 − α), min_τ BG_τ(a) ≥ min_τ BĜ_τ(a) − ε ≥ G^↓_fail. Conclusion follows.

## Score rationale (theory-grounded only — no empirical content used)

The single theoretical claim — Theorem 5.2 — is **mathematically correct**. Under (ε, α)-reliability and the chosen shield threshold, the shielded action's safety guarantee follows in a few lines. The proof is a routine "reliability + margin = safety" calculation that's standard in safety-critical control settings.

What's limited:

1. **Scope mismatch with the paper's motivation.** The paper's central problem is *distribution shift* (Table 1 documents 8–14% TIR drops under unseen patients). The theorem's reliability parameter ε is precisely the kind of quantity that degrades under such shift — yet ε is taken as a static constant in Def 5.1 with no analysis of how it scales with the training–deployment shift severity. The paper's empirical centerpiece (shielding restores safety under OOD shift) is therefore not theoretically connected to its safety theorem; the theorem applies *under the deployment distribution* with whatever ε holds there, but no result tells the reader how to bound that ε in terms of training error or shift magnitude.

2. **Source of randomness in Def 5.1 is unspecified.** The probability "Pr(...)" in eq. 2 doesn't say whether it's over BG | BĜ, over (BG, BĜ) jointly, or over the simulator's stochastic dynamics. This matters for chaining the bound through the shield's BĜ-dependent selection logic. The most natural reading is "BG | BĜ", which makes the proof work — but the paper doesn't pin it down.

3. **Per-action vs top-k reliability.** Section 5.3 says the shield evaluates top-k = 4 bolus candidates and verifies each. Def 5.1's reliability is "for any action a" — read as a per-fixed-a bound. Strictly applied to all 4 candidates simultaneously would need a union bound 1 − 4α, not 1 − α. This isn't load-bearing for the theorem-as-stated (which is about a *given* permitted action, not about a maximum over candidates), but it does affect the practical interpretation when the shield commits to one of 4 candidates based on BĜ-derived rankings.

4. **No proof that BA-NODE satisfies (ε, α)-reliability.** The paper's own dynamics model is the natural candidate predictor, but the paper offers no theoretical bound (or even an empirical confidence-band analysis) of the ε required. Table 2 reports MAE / RMSE in glucose units, which gives a rough proxy, but no formal connection.

What's correct: the theorem itself, as stated and under its assumption, is right. The (ε, α)-reliability + margin construction is a clean way to formalize what shielding accomplishes.

**Score: 5.** Weak but defensible. The single theorem is correct but narrow: a routine reliability-to-safety conversion that doesn't engage with the paper's distribution-shift motivation, doesn't connect ε to BA-NODE's actual error properties, and doesn't bridge to the top-k candidate-evaluation pipeline used in practice.
