## Verification of claims

**(1) α < 1/2 ⟹ R_LR ordering (top of p.4):** ✓
- min_{V=1} R_LR = 1 + (1-2)α s_L = 1 - α s_L; minimized over s_L ∈ [0,1] at s_L = 1: 1 - α.
- max_{V=0} R_LR = 0 + (1-0)α s_L = α s_L; maximized over s_L at s_L = 1: α.
- 1 - α > α ⟺ α < 1/2. ✓

**(2) Eq (14) population identity for R_LR:** ✓ — just E[V] + α E[(1-2V) s_L], split into V=0 and V=1 terms.

**(3) Eq (15) population identity for R_T2T:** ✓
- E[R_T2T] = E[V] + α E[(1-V) s_L (1-p)] - α E[V s_L p]
- = p + α(1-p) E[(1-V) s_L] - αp E[V s_L]
- = p + α(1-p) Pr(V=0) E[s_L|V=0] - αp Pr(V=1) E[s_L|V=1]
- = p + α(1-p)² E[s_L|V=0] - αp² E[s_L|V=1]. ✓

**(4) Reward-induced ordering for R_T2T (assuming α ∈ (0,1), s_L ∈ [0,1], p ∈ [0,1]):**
- correct short ≻ correct long: R = 1 - α s_L p, decreasing in s_L. ✓ (requires p > 0; ties at p=0)
- incorrect long ≻ incorrect short: R = α s_L (1-p), increasing in s_L. ✓ (requires p < 1)
- correct long ≻ incorrect long: min over correct = 1 - α p; max over incorrect = α(1-p). Need 1 - αp > α(1-p), i.e., 1 > α. ✓ (the α < 1/2 ⊂ (0,1) suffices.)

**(5) "Length-related correction is a second-order term in p" (line after Eq 15):** loose framing.
- At boundaries: at p=0, correction = α E[s_L|V=0] (magnitude O(α)). At p=1, correction = -α E[s_L|V=1] (magnitude O(α)). Not O(p²).
- The "second-order" refers to the fact that the (1-p)² and p² *modulation* of the conditional expectations vanishes faster than (1-p) and p respectively at their respective boundaries. But this is interior-modulation behavior, not a global second-order property. Calling it "a second-order term in p" is misleading at first read.

## Theory score rationale

The mathematical content of Section 4.2 is correct as algebra. The constraint α < 1/2 correctly enforces the correct-vs-incorrect ordering for R_LR. The piecewise reward R_T2T (Eq 16-17) does induce the claimed four-way ordering. The expectation identities (Eq 14, Eq 15) are derivable in two lines.

But the theoretical content is *thin*: it consists of three algebraic identities and a four-way ordering check. There is no convergence proof, no regret/sample-complexity bound, no novel mathematical machinery. The contribution-level claim "T2T realizes a thickening-to-thinning process" is supported by reading off the sign pattern of Eq (17), not by a non-trivial theorem.

The most concerning theoretical gap is the **disconnect between Eq (15) (presented as the targeted population objective) and the implemented update**. The paper's text frames Eq (15) as "we target [an objective]" and Eq (16) as "we implement Eq (15)." But the explicit stop-gradient choice on p̂ (line below Eq 17, "we treat this estimate as a stop-gradient statistic when updating θ") means the implemented policy gradient is **not** ∇_θ of Eq (15). The full gradient of Eq (15) carries additional ∇_θ p terms — which dominate near boundary regimes (p ≈ 0 or p ≈ 1) where the Thickening/Thinning asymmetry is sharpest. The stop-gradient implementation drops these terms, so the implemented update direction targets a different (frozen-shaping) objective rather than Eq (15) itself. The connection to Eq (15) is heuristic, not provable.

A secondary concern: the on-policy estimator p̂ in Eq (18) is computed from the same group {o_i} whose rewards are being computed. Including o_i in the p̂ used to score o_i induces a self-correlation bias of magnitude O(1/K). The cited Yang et al. (2026) ("Your group-relative advantage is biased") analyzes this kind of bias for GRPO; the paper does not engage with whether T2T's specific reward design inherits, mitigates, or amplifies this bias.

These are real concerns but mostly framing-level / acknowledged-in-limitations. The math itself is correct. Score: **4** (weak but defensible — sound algebra, limited theoretical depth, loose framing of Eq 15 as a "target objective"). Confidence: **4** (read full paper + appendix, anchored to specific equations, did not cross-check against Yang et al. 2026's exact bias formulas).
