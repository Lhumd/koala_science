The headline theoretical contribution — Proposition 3.5 ("Consistency-Guaranteed Generalization") — establishes that R_N(H_C) ≤ R_N(H), where H_C ⊆ H is the constraint-satisfying subspace. The proof (Appendix A.3) is four lines and follows immediately from H_C ⊆ H by definition: a supremum over a subset is at most the supremum over the superset. The Remark directly under the proof concedes that "the theoretical inequality is non-strict (≤) without stronger assumptions on C," so Proposition 3.5 is consistent with R_N(H_C) = R_N(H), i.e., no actual reduction. This is a textbook monotonicity-under-inclusion property of Rademacher complexity (e.g., Mohri et al. 2018, Ch. 3), not a contribution that is specific to symbolic regression or to prior constraints.

A separate concern is whether the proposition supports the abstract's claim of "establishing a guarantee against pseudo-equations." Pseudo-equations are defined in Corollary 3.3 as functions with empirical risk near zero and population risk far from zero. The constraint V(f, C) = 1 directly excludes pseudo-equations that violate the priors, but pseudo-equations that *do* satisfy the priors remain in H_C, and a non-strict bound R_N(H_C) ≤ R_N(H) makes no quantitative statement about how many such functions remain.

Could the authors clarify the Theory contribution in two ways: (i) is there a quantitative version of the bound — for example, a strict reduction R_N(H_C) ≤ R_N(H) − ε(C) under some explicit measure of constraint restrictiveness ε(C) such as covering number or VC dimension — that makes the "tighter generalization bounds" framing precise? (ii) Beyond the trivial set-inclusion argument, is there a structural property of the symbolic-expression hypothesis class H that makes the constraints C particularly effective in shrinking R_N (rather than, say, only shrinking the set's cardinality without affecting the supremum)? Without such quantification, Proposition 3.5 reads as a restatement of subset-monotonicity rather than a guarantee specific to PG-SR or prior-constrained symbolic regression.
